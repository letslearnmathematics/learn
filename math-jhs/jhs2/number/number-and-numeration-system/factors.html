Introduction

In this lesson, we'll learn how to:

  1. Identify factors of numbers and find common factors
  2. Use Venn diagrams to represent relationships between sets
  3. Solve real-life problems involving union and intersection of sets

These skills are useful in organizing information, analyzing data, and making decisions.

Factors of Numbers

Finding Factors

The factors of a number are the whole numbers that divide it exactly without leaving a remainder. To find all factors of a number:

  1. Start with 1 and the number itself
  2. Test divisibility by numbers up to half of the number
  3. List all numbers that divide the original number exactly

Example: Find the factors of 42 and 36

Factors of 42:

1 × 42 = 42

2 × 21 = 42

3 × 14 = 42

6 × 7 = 42

∴ Factors of 42 = {1, 2, 3, 6, 7, 14, 21, 42}

Factors of 36:

1 × 36 = 36

2 × 18 = 36

3 × 12 = 36

4 × 9 = 36

6 × 6 = 36

∴ Factors of 36 = {1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36}

Common Factors

The common factors of two numbers are factors that appear in both lists.

Example: Find common factors of 42 and 36

Factors of 42: {1, 2, 3, 6, 7, 14, 21, 42}

Factors of 36: {1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36}

Common factors = {1, 2, 3, 6}

The Greatest Common Factor (GCF) is 6

Examples

Question 1

Find the Greatest Common Factor (G.C.F) of 12 and 18.

Watch how the question above is solved practically.



Solution:

Factors of 12 $\Rightarrow$ {1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12}

Factors of 18 $\Rightarrow$ {1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18}

Common factors $\Rightarrow$ {1, 2, 3, 6}

$\Rightarrow$ Greatest Common Factor $=$ 6

$\therefore$ the G.C.F of 12 and 18 is 6.

Question 2

Find the Greatest Common Factor (G.C.F) of 24 and 36.

Watch how the question above is solved practically.



Solution:

24 $= 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 3$

36 $= 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 3$

G.C.F $\Rightarrow 2 \times 2 \times 3$

G.C.F $\Rightarrow 12$

$\therefore$ the Greatest Common Factor of 24 and 36 is 12

Venn Diagrams

Understanding Venn Diagrams

A set is a well-defined collection of distinct objects, which can be numbers, letters, or other items. In mathematics, we usually use a capital letter to represent a set, and the elements of the set are enclosed in curly braces. For example, the set of natural numbers less than 5 can be written as:

$$M = \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$$

A Venn diagram is a diagram that uses overlapping circles to show relationships between sets. Given two sets $A = \{\text{factors of 36}\}$ and $B = \{\text{factors of 42}\}$, we can represent them on the Venn diagram as follows:

Venn Diagram showing sets A and B

  • Union

    $A \cup B \Rightarrow$ All elements in A or B or both

    In the diagram above, the union of sets A and B includes all factors of both 36 and 42. That is;

    $A \cup B$$\Rightarrow $ {1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 7, 9, 12, 14, 18, 21, 36, 42}


  • Intersection

    $A \cap B \Rightarrow$ Elements common to both A and B

    In the diagram above, the intersection of sets A and B includes only the factors that are common to both 36 and 42, which are {1, 2, 3, 6}.

    Hence, $A \cap B$$\Rightarrow $ {1, 2, 3, 6}


  • Complement

    The complement of a set A, written as $A'$ or $A^c$, is the set of all elements in the universal set that are not in A.

    In the diagram above:

    $A^\prime \Rightarrow$ {7, 14, 21, 42}

Real-Life Problems

Problem Solving Approach

To solve real-life problems involving union and intersection of sets:

  1. Identify what each set represents
  2. Determine what is given and what is asked
  3. Draw a Venn diagram if helpful
  4. Apply the appropriate formula (union, intersection, etc.)
  5. Solve the equation and interpret the result

Practice Exercise

Question 1

In a group of 50 people, 28 own cars, 23 own bicycles, and 10 own both. How many own neither a car nor a bicycle?

n(C ∪ B) = n(C) + n(B) - n(C ∩ B) = 28 + 23 - 10 = 41

Neither = Total - n(C ∪ B) = 50 - 41 = 9

Question 2

A factory has 120 workers. 80 can operate machine A, 60 can operate machine B, and 30 can operate both. How many can operate neither machine?

n(A ∪ B) = n(A) + n(B) - n(A ∩ B) = 80 + 60 - 30 = 110

Neither = Total - n(A ∪ B) = 120 - 110 = 10

Question 3

In a class of 35 students, 20 like mathematics, 15 like science, and 5 like both. How many students like only mathematics?

Only mathematics = Total math - both = 20 - 5 = 15

Question 4

In a class of 50 students, 30 study French, 25 study Spanish, and 10 study both languages. Represent this on a Venn diagram and find how many study only French.

Let F = French, S = Spanish

Both languages = 10

Only French = 30 - 10 = 20

Only Spanish = 25 - 10 = 15

Neither language = 50 - (20 + 10 + 15) = 5

Question 5

In a survey of 100 people, 60 like tea, 45 like coffee, and 20 like both. How many like neither tea nor coffee?

Let T = tea, C = coffee

n(T ∪ C) = n(T) + n(C) - n(T ∩ C) = 60 + 45 - 20 = 85

Neither = Total - n(T ∪ C) = 100 - 85 = 15
