Introduction

Standard form (also called scientific notation) is a way of writing very large or very small numbers in a compact form. It expresses numbers as a product of a number between 1 and 10 and a power of 10.

Standard form is particularly useful in science and engineering where we often deal with extremely large numbers (like distances between stars) or extremely small numbers (like the size of atoms).

In this lesson, we will learn how to:

  1. Write integers as powers of 10
  2. Write multiples of 10 in standard form
  3. Write any integer in standard form

Writing Integers as Powers of 10

Understanding Powers of 10

A power of 10 is any number that can be expressed as 10 raised to an integer exponent. For example:

Positive Exponents:

  • \(10^1 = 10\)
  • \(10^2 = 100\)
  • \(10^3 = 1,000\)
  • \(10^6 = 1,000,000\) (one million)

Negative Exponents:

  • \(10^{-1} = 0.1\)
  • \(10^{-2} = 0.01\)
  • \(10^{-3} = 0.001\)

Example 1: Express 10,000 as a power of 10

Solution: Count the number of zeros: 10,000 has 4 zeros

\[ 10,000 = 10^4 \]

Example 2: Express 0.0001 as a power of 10

Solution: Count the number of decimal places: 0.0001 has 4 decimal places

\[ 0.0001 = 10^{-4} \]

Writing Multiples of 10 in Standard Form

Standard Form Structure

Standard form has two parts:

  1. A number between 1 and 10 (can be 1 but must be less than 10)
  2. A power of 10

The general form is:

\[ a \times 10^n \]

where \(1 \leq a < 10\) and \(n\) is an integer.

Example 1: Write 300 in standard form

Solution: \[ 300 = 3 \times 100 = 3 \times 10^2 \]

Example 2: Write 45,000 in standard form

Solution: \[ 45,000 = 4.5 \times 10,000 = 4.5 \times 10^4 \]

Key Steps

  1. Identify the number between 1 and 10 by placing a decimal after the first non-zero digit
  2. Count how many places you moved the decimal from its original position to its new position
  3. This count becomes the exponent on the 10
  4. If the original number was greater than 10, the exponent is positive
  5. If the original number was less than 1, the exponent is negative

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Write 7,000 in standard form.

Move decimal after 7 (3 places):

\[ 7,000 = 7 \times 10^3 \]

Question 2

Write 820,000 in standard form.

Move decimal after 8 (5 places):

\[ 820,000 = 8.2 \times 10^5 \]

Question 3

Write 0.0045 in standard form.

Move decimal to after 4 (3 places to the right):

\[ 0.0045 = 4.5 \times 10^{-3} \]

Writing Integers in Standard Form

General Method

The process is similar for any integer, not just multiples of 10:

  1. Place a decimal point after the first non-zero digit
  2. Count how many places you moved the decimal from its original position
  3. Write as a product of this new number and 10 raised to the count

Example 1: Write 45,678 in standard form

Solution: \[ 45,678 = 4.5678 \times 10,000 = 4.5678 \times 10^4 \]

Example 2: Write 203 in standard form

Solution: \[ 203 = 2.03 \times 100 = 2.03 \times 10^2 \]

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting to make the first number between 1 and 10
  • Counting the wrong number of decimal places moved
  • Using the wrong sign for the exponent (positive for large numbers, negative for small numbers)
  • Including trailing zeros that aren't significant

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Write 123,456 in standard form.

Move decimal after 1 (5 places):

\[ 123,456 = 1.23456 \times 10^5 \]

Question 2

Write 7,050 in standard form.

Move decimal after 7 (3 places):

\[ 7,050 = 7.05 \times 10^3 \]

Question 3

Write 90,000,000 in standard form.

Move decimal after 9 (7 places):

\[ 90,000,000 = 9 \times 10^7 \]
