Introduction

Comparing numbers involves determining which of two numbers is greater, smaller, or if they are equal. We use the symbols $>$ (greater than), $<$ (less than), and $=$ (equal to) to show these relationships.

To compare large numbers effectively, we need to understand place values and how each digit's position affects the number's overall value. This lesson will teach you how to compare and order whole numbers up to 9 digits.

Comparing Numbers

Steps to Compare Numbers

Follow these steps to compare two numbers:

  1. Write the numbers vertically, aligning them by place value
  2. Compare the digits from left to right (starting with the highest place value)
  3. When you find digits that differ, the number with the larger digit is greater
  4. If all digits are the same, the numbers are equal

Examples

Example 1: Compare 45,678,921 and 45,678,912

Let's align the numbers:

\[ \begin{align*} 45,678,921 \\ 45,678,912 \end{align*} \]

Compare digit by digit from left:

  • Millions place: 5 $=$ 5
  • Hundred-thousands place: 6 $=$ 6
  • Ten-thousands place: 7 $=$ 7
  • Thousands place: 8 $=$ 8
  • Hundreds place: 9 $=$ 9
  • Tens place: 2 $>$ 1

Since 2 $>$ 1 in the tens place, we conclude:

\[ 45,678,921 > 45,678,912 \]

Key Points

Symbol Meanings:

  • $>$ means "greater than" (e.g., 5 $>$ 3)
  • $<$ means "less than" (e.g., 3 $<$ 5)
  • $=$ means "equal to" (e.g., 5 $=$ 5)

Remember:

The "mouth" of the symbol always opens toward the larger number.

Think of it as a hungry alligator that always wants to eat the bigger number!

Examples

Question 1

Compare 123,456,789 and 123,456,798 using $>$, $<$, or $=$

Let's compare digit by digit:

\[ \begin{align*} 123,456,789 \\ 123,456,798 \end{align*} \]

They differ in the tens place: 8 $<$ 9

Therefore:

\[ 123,456,789 < 123,456,798 \]

Question 2

Compare 987,654,321 and 987,564,321 using $>$, $<$, or $=$

Let's compare digit by digit:

\[ \begin{align*} 987,654,321 \\ 987,564,321 \end{align*} \]

They differ in the hundred-thousands place: 6 $>$ 5

Therefore:

\[ 987,654,321 > 987,564,321 \]

Question 3

Compare 500,500,500 and 500,500,500 using $>$, $<$, or $=$

Let's compare digit by digit:

\[ \begin{align*} 500,500,500 \\ 500,500,500 \end{align*} \]

All digits are identical

Therefore:

\[ 500,500,500 = 500,500,500 \]

Question 4

Compare 234,567,890 and 234,567,809 using $>$, $<$, or $=$

Let's compare digit by digit:

\[ \begin{align*} 234,567,890 \\ 234,567,809 \end{align*} \]

They differ in the tens place: 9 $>$ 0

Therefore:

\[ 234,567,890 > 234,567,809 \]

Question 5

Compare 1,000,000,000 and 999,999,999 using $>$, $<$, or $=$

Let's compare digit by digit:

\[ \begin{align*} 1,000,000,000 \\ 999,999,999 \end{align*} \]

The first digit: 1 $<$ 9 (but 1 is in the billions place, 9 is in the hundred-millions place).
1,000,000,000 has more digits, so it is greater.

Therefore:

\[ 1,000,000,000 > 999,999,999 \]

Ordering Numbers

How to Order Numbers

To order a list of numbers from smallest to largest (ascending order) or largest to smallest (descending order):

  1. Compare the numbers two at a time using the comparison method
  2. For ascending order, arrange them from smallest to largest
  3. For descending order, arrange them from largest to smallest

Example: Ascending Order

Example: Arrange these numbers in ascending order: 45,678,921; 45,678,912; 45,679,000

Let's compare them:

  • 45,678,912 $<$ 45,678,921 (from earlier example)
  • 45,678,921 $<$ 45,679,000 (compare thousands place: 8 $<$ 9)

Therefore, the ascending order is:

45,678,912; 45,678,921; 45,679,000

Example: Descending Order

Example: Arrange these numbers in descending order: 123,456,789; 123,456,798; 123,465,789

Let's compare them:

  • 123,465,789 $>$ 123,456,798 (ten-thousands place: 6 $>$ 5)
  • 123,456,798 $>$ 123,456,789 (tens place: 9 $>$ 8)

Therefore, the descending order is:

123,465,789; 123,456,798; 123,456,789

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Arrange these numbers in ascending order: 987,654,321; 987,564,321; 987,654,312

Compare them:

  1. 987,564,321 $<$ 987,654,312 (hundred-thousands place: 5 $<$ 6)
  2. 987,654,312 $<$ 987,654,321 (tens place: 1 $<$ 2)

Ascending order:

987,564,321; 987,654,312; 987,654,321

Question 2

Arrange these numbers in descending order: 500,500,500; 500,505,000; 500,500,050

Compare them:

  1. 500,505,000 > 500,500,500 (thousands place: 5 $>$ 0)
  2. 500,500,500 > 500,500,050 (hundreds place: 5 $>$ 0)

Descending order:

500,505,000; 500,500,500; 500,500,050

Question 3

Arrange these numbers in ascending order: 100,000,000; 99,999,999; 100,000,001

Compare them:

  1. 99,999,999 < 100,000,000 (99 million < 100 million)
  2. 100,000,000 < 100,000,001 (units place: 0 < 1)

Ascending order:

99,999,999; 100,000,000; 100,000,001

100,000 More or Less

Finding Numbers 100,000 More or Less

To find a number that is 100,000 more or less than a given number:

  1. Identify the hundred-thousands place in the number
  2. To add 100,000, increase the hundred-thousands digit by 1
  3. To subtract 100,000, decrease the hundred-thousands digit by 1
  4. Adjust other digits if necessary (when crossing a millions boundary)

Example: 100,000 More

Example 1: What is 100,000 more than 45,678,921?

Let's identify the hundred-thousands place (6):

45,678,921 (6 is in the hundred-thousands place)

Add 1 to the hundred-thousands digit:

6 (hundred-thousands) + 1 = 7

New number: 45,778,921

Therefore:

45,678,921 $+$ 100,000 $=$ 45,778,921

Example 2: What is 100,000 more than 99,999,999?

Adding 100,000 would make it 100,099,999

Note how we had to increase the millions digit as well

Example: 100,000 Less

Example 1: What is 100,000 less than 45,678,921?

Let's identify the hundred-thousands place (6):

45,678,921 (6 is in the hundred-thousands place)

Subtract 1 from the hundred-thousands digit:

6 (hundred-thousands) - 1 = 5

New number: 45,578,921

Therefore:

45,678,921 $-$ 100,000 $=$ 45,578,921

Example 2: What is 100,000 less than 100,000,000?

Subtracting 100,000 would make it 99,900,000

Note how we had to decrease the millions digit as well

Extending to 500,000

The same principle applies when finding numbers that are 500,000 more or less:

  • For 500,000 more: Add 5 to the hundred-thousands digit
  • For 500,000 less: Subtract 5 from the hundred-thousands digit
  • Adjust higher place values if necessary

Example: What is 500,000 more than 45,678,921?

6 (hundred-thousands) + 5 = 11

This means we need to:

  • Write 1 in the hundred-thousands place
  • Add 1 to the millions place: 5 + 1 = 6

New number: 46,178,921

45,678,921 $+$ 500,000 $=$ 46,178,921

Practice Exercise

Question 1

What is 100,000 more than 123,456,789?

Identify the hundred-thousands place (4):

123,456,789 (4 is in the hundred-thousands place)

Add 1 to the hundred-thousands digit: 4 + 1 = 5

New number: 123,556,789

123,456,789 $+$ 100,000 $=$ 123,556,789

Question 2

What is 100,000 less than 987,654,321?

Identify the hundred-thousands place (6):

987,654,321 (6 is in the hundred-thousands place)

Subtract 1 from the hundred-thousands digit: 6 - 1 = 5

New number: 987,554,321

987,654,321 $-$ 100,000 $=$ 987,554,321

Question 3

What is 500,000 more than 100,000,000?

Identify the hundred-thousands place (0):

100,000,000 (0 is in the hundred-thousands place)

Add 5 to the hundred-thousands digit: 0 + 5 = 5

New number: 100,500,000

100,000,000 $+$ 500,000 $=$ 100,500,000

Question 4

What is 500,000 less than 500,000,000?

Identify the hundred-thousands place (0):

500,000,000 (0 is in the hundred-thousands place)

We need to subtract 5 from 0, which requires borrowing:

  1. Decrease the millions digit by 1 (500 → 499)
  2. Set hundred-thousands digit to 5 (0 → 5 when borrowing)

New number: 499,500,000

500,000,000 $-$ 500,000 $=$ 499,500,000
