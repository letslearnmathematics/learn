Introduction

Indices (singular: index) are a shorthand way of writing repeated multiplication of the same number. They are also called exponents or powers. Understanding the laws of indices is essential for simplifying and evaluating mathematical expressions efficiently.

In this lesson, we will learn how to apply the laws of indices to simplify and evaluate numbers involving powers of numbers, while following the correct order of operations (PEDMAS).

For example:

\[ 5^3 = 5 \times 5 \times 5 = 125 \]

Here, 5 is the base and 3 is the index or exponent.

Key Terms:

  • Base: The number being multiplied (5 in the example above)
  • Index/Exponent: Shows how many times the base is multiplied by itself (3 in the example)
  • Power: The result of the operation (125 in the example)

Basic Laws of Indices

The Fundamental Laws

There are several fundamental laws that govern operations with indices:

1. Multiplication Law

\[ a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n} \]

When multiplying powers with the same base, add the exponents.

Example:

\[ 2^3 \times 2^4 = 2^{3+4} = 2^7 = 128 \]

2. Division Law

\[ \frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n} \]

When dividing powers with the same base, subtract the exponents.

Example:

\[ \frac{5^6}{5^2} = 5^{6-2} = 5^4 = 625 \]

3. Power of a Power Law

\[ (a^m)^n = a^{m \times n} \]

When raising a power to another power, multiply the exponents.

Example:

\[ (3^2)^4 = 3^{2 \times 4} = 3^8 = 6561 \]

4. Zero Exponent Law

\[ a^0 = 1 \quad (a \neq 0) \]

Any non-zero number raised to the power of zero equals 1.

Example:

\[ 7^0 = 1 \]

5. Negative Exponent Law

\[ a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n} \]

A negative exponent indicates the reciprocal of the positive power.

Example:

\[ 2^{-3} = \frac{1}{2^3} = \frac{1}{8} \]

6. Fractional Exponent Law

\[ a^{\frac{m}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{a^m} \]

A fractional exponent represents a root.

Example:

\[ 8^{\frac{2}{3}} = \sqrt[3]{8^2} = \sqrt[3]{64} = 4 \]

Simplifying Expressions

Combining the Laws

We can combine multiple laws of indices to simplify more complex expressions. The key is to apply the laws systematically and follow the correct order of operations.

Simplification Techniques

Example 1: Simplify \( \frac{2^5 \times 2^3}{2^4} \)

Solution:

\[ \begin{align*} \frac{2^5 \times 2^3}{2^4} &= \frac{2^{5+3}}{2^4} \\ &= \frac{2^8}{2^4} \\ &= 2^{8-4} \\ &= 2^4 \\ &= 16 \end{align*} \]

Example 2: Simplify \( (3^2 \times 3^4)^3 \)

Solution:

\[ \begin{align*} (3^2 \times 3^4)^3 &= (3^{2+4})^3 \\ &= (3^6)^3 \\ &= 3^{6 \times 3} \\ &= 3^{18} \end{align*} \]

Evaluating Expressions

Calculating Numerical Values

After simplifying expressions using the laws of indices, we often need to evaluate them to find their numerical values. This involves calculating the powers and performing any remaining operations.

Evaluation Techniques

Example 1: Evaluate \( 2^3 \times 3^2 \)

Solution:

\[ \begin{align*} 2^3 \times 3^2 &= 8 \times 9 \\ &= 72 \end{align*} \]

Example 2: Evaluate \( \frac{5^3 \times 5^{-1}}{5^0} \)

Solution:

\[ \begin{align*} \frac{5^3 \times 5^{-1}}{5^0} &= \frac{5^{3+(-1)}}{1} \\ &= \frac{5^2}{1} \\ &= 25 \end{align*} \]

Applying PEDMAS

Order of Operations

When simplifying and evaluating expressions with indices, we must follow the correct order of operations (PEDMAS) or BODMAS:

  1. Parentheses
  2. Exponents (indices)
  3. Division and Multiplication (left to right)
  4. Addition and Subtraction (left to right)

PEDMAS Application

Example 1: Evaluate \( 2 \times 3^2 + 4 \)

Solution:

\[ \begin{align*} 2 \times 3^2 + 4 &= 2 \times 9 + 4 \\ &= 18 + 4 \\ &= 22 \end{align*} \]

Example 2: Evaluate \( (2 + 3)^2 \times 4 - 5 \)

Solution:

\[ \begin{align*} (2 + 3)^2 \times 4 - 5 &= 5^2 \times 4 - 5 \\ &= 25 \times 4 - 5 \\ &= 100 - 5 \\ &= 95 \end{align*} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

\[ \begin{align*} 3 + 2 \times 4^2 &= 3 + 2 \times 16 \\ &= 3 + 32 \\ &= 35 \end{align*} \]

Question 2
