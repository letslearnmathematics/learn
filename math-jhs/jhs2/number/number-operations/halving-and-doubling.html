Introduction

Mental math strategies are techniques that allow us to perform calculations quickly and efficiently in our heads, without the need for paper, calculators, or other tools. These strategies leverage number properties and mathematical relationships to simplify complex problems.

In this lesson, we'll focus specifically on the halving and doubling strategy, which is particularly useful for multiplication problems. This technique takes advantage of the fact that sometimes it's easier to work with numbers that are half or double the original numbers in a calculation.

Halving and Doubling Strategy

The Basic Idea

The halving and doubling strategy works because of the fundamental property of multiplication:

\[ (a \times b) = \left(\frac{a}{2} \times 2b\right) = \left(2a \times \frac{b}{2}\right) \]

When one number is halved and the other is doubled, the product remains the same. We can use this to simplify multiplication problems by converting them into easier calculations.

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Identify if one of the numbers can be easily halved (even numbers work best)
  2. Halve one number while doubling the other
  3. Repeat the process if it makes the calculation simpler
  4. Multiply the resulting numbers

When to Use

This strategy is particularly useful when:

  • One number is even and can be easily halved
  • One number ends with 5 or 0
  • You're multiplying by numbers like 4, 8, 16, etc. (powers of 2)

Examples

Example 1: 14 × 5

\[ \begin{align*} 14 \times 5 &= (14 \div 2) \times (5 \times 2) \\ &= 7 \times 10 \\ &= 70 \end{align*} \]

Halving 14 gives 7, doubling 5 gives 10, and 7 × 10 is much easier to calculate mentally.

Example 2: 16 × 25

\[ \begin{align*} 16 \times 25 &= (16 \div 2) \times (25 \times 2) \\ &= 8 \times 50 \quad \text{(we can simplify further)} \\ &= (8 \div 2) \times (50 \times 2) \\ &= 4 \times 100 \\ &= 400 \end{align*} \]

We applied the halving/doubling strategy twice to arrive at a simple multiplication by 100.

Practical Applications

Real-World Uses

The halving and doubling strategy is particularly useful in everyday situations like shopping, cooking, and budgeting. Here are some practical examples:

Example 1: Shopping Discounts

If an item costs ₵35 each and is on sale for "buy 1 get 1 half off", what's the cost for 4 items?

\[ \begin{align*} \text{First pair:} & \quad 35 + (35 \div 2) = 35 + 17.50 = 52.50 \\ \text{Second pair:} & \quad \text{Same as first pair} = 52.50 \\ \text{Total:} & \quad 52.50 \times 2 = (50 \times 2) + (2.50 \times 2) = 100 + 5 = 105 \end{align*} \]

Example 2: Recipe Adjustments

A recipe calls for 250g of flour for 8 servings. How much flour for 12 servings?

\[ \begin{align*} \text{For 4 servings:} & \quad 250g \div 2 = 125g \\ \text{For 12 servings:} & \quad 250g + 125g = 375g \end{align*} \]

Advanced Techniques

Combining Strategies

The halving and doubling strategy can be combined with other mental math techniques for even more powerful calculations:

With Distributive Property

Break numbers into parts that are easier to work with:

\[ 24 \times 25 = (20 \times 25) + (4 \times 25) = 500 + 100 = 600 \]

With Rounding

Adjust numbers to make them easier to halve/double:

\[ 19 \times 25 \approx 20 \times 25 - 25 = 500 - 25 = 475 \]

Special Cases

Some number combinations are particularly well-suited for halving/doubling:

  • Multiplying by 5: Halve the other number and multiply by 10
  • Multiplying by 25: Halve twice and multiply by 100
  • Multiplying by 125: Halve three times and multiply by 1000

Example: 48 × 125

\[ \begin{align*} 48 \times 125 &= (48 \div 2) \times (125 \times 2) \\ &= 24 \times 250 \quad \text{(halve/double again)} \\ &= 12 \times 500 \quad \text{(halve/double once more)} \\ &= 6 \times 1,000 \\ &= 6,000 \end{align*} \]

\[ \begin{align*} 12 \times 25 &= (12 \div 2) \times (25 \times 2) \\ &= 6 \times 50 \\ &= 300 \text{ cedis} \end{align*} \]
