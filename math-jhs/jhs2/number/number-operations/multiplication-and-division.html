Introduction

Multiplication and division of multi-digit numbers are fundamental skills in mathematics. In this lesson, we'll explore several methods to multiply and divide large numbers efficiently.

Being able to multiply and divide large numbers quickly and accurately is essential for solving real-world problems in business, science, and everyday life. We'll learn multiple methods so you can choose the one that works best for you.

Area Model Method

What is the Area Model?

The area model breaks down multiplication into simpler partial products by representing the problem as the area of a rectangle. This method is also called the "box method" or "expand and box method".

Steps for Area Model

  1. Break down each number into its place values (expand)
  2. Draw a rectangle and divide it into sections based on the place values
  3. Multiply the place values across the rows and columns
  4. Add all the partial products to get the final answer

Example: 24 × 36

Break down: 20 + 4 and 30 + 6

30 6
20 20×30=600 20×6=120
4 4×30=120 4×6=24

Add partial products: 600 + 120 + 120 + 24 = 864

Vertical Place Value Method

Traditional Multiplication

The vertical place value method is the standard algorithm for multiplication that you're probably familiar with. It involves multiplying each digit and carefully keeping track of place values through proper alignment.

Steps for Vertical Method

  1. Write the numbers vertically with the larger number on top
  2. Multiply the top number by each digit of the bottom number, starting from the right
  3. Write each partial product, shifting left with each new line
  4. Add all the partial products to get the final answer

Example: 657 × 27

      657
    ×  27
    -----
     4599   (657 × 7)
    1314    (657 × 20, shifted one place left)
    -----
    17,739

Lattice Method

Visual Multiplication

The lattice method uses a grid structure to organize multiplication. This ancient method is particularly useful for multiplying large numbers as it keeps all partial products organized in a visual way.

Steps for Lattice Method

  1. Draw a grid with rows equal to digits in first number and columns equal to digits in second number
  2. Divide each cell diagonally
  3. Multiply each digit pair and write the product in the corresponding cell (tens above diagonal, units below)
  4. Add along the diagonals to get the final product

Example: 46 × 37

Adding diagonally: 1, 6+2+8=16 (write 6, carry 1), 1+1+2+1=5, 2 → 1,702

Practice Exercise

Distributive Property

Breaking Down Multiplication

The distributive property states that a × (b + c) = a × b + a × c. We can use this property to break down complex multiplications into simpler ones that are easier to compute mentally.

Using Distributive Property

  1. Break down one of the numbers into a sum of simpler numbers
  2. Multiply the other number by each part of the sum
  3. Add all the partial products together

Example: 325 × 15

Break down 15 into 10 + 5:

325 × 15 = 325 × (10 + 5) = (325 × 10) + (325 × 5)

= 3,250 + 1,625 = 4,875

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Use the distributive property to multiply 248 × 12

Break down 12 into 10 + 2:

248 × 12 = 248 × (10 + 2) = (248 × 10) + (248 × 2)

= 2,480 + 496 = 2,976

Question 2

Break down 24 into 20 + 4:

175 × 24 = 175 × (20 + 4) = (175 × 20) + (175 × 4)

= 3,500 + 700 = 4,200

Divisibility Tests

Quick Division Checks

Divisibility tests are shortcuts to determine if one number divides evenly into another without performing the actual division. These tests are useful for simplifying fractions, factoring numbers, and solving various mathematical problems.

Common Divisibility Tests

Divisor Test Example
2 Last digit is even (0,2,4,6,8) 358 (divisible), 357 (not)
3 Sum of digits divisible by 3 123: 1+2+3=6 (divisible)
4 Last two digits divisible by 4 1,324: 24÷4=6 (divisible)
5 Last digit is 0 or 5 350 (divisible), 352 (not)
6 Divisible by both 2 and 3 114: even and 1+1+4=6 (divisible)
9 Sum of digits divisible by 9 288: 2+8+8=18 (divisible)
10 Last digit is 0 450 (divisible), 455 (not)

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Determine if 4,725 is divisible by 3, 5, and 9

Divisible by 3: 4+7+2+5=18, 18÷3=6 → Yes

Divisible by 5: Ends with 5 → Yes

Divisible by 9: 4+7+2+5=18, 18÷9=2 → Yes

Question 2

Determine if 6,312 is divisible by 4, 6, and 8

Divisible by 4: Last two digits 12, 12÷4=3 → Yes

Divisible by 6: Even (divisible by 2) and 6+3+1+2=12 (divisible by 3) → Yes

Divisible by 8: Last three digits 312, 312÷8=39 → Yes
