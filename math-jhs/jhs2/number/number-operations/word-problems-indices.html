Introduction

Powers of natural numbers (also called indices) are a way to express repeated multiplication of the same number. They are written as a small number (the exponent) to the upper right of a base number.

For example, \( 5^3 \) means \( 5 \times 5 \times 5 \), which equals 125. Here, 5 is the base and 3 is the exponent.

Examples of powers:

\[ \begin{align*} 2^4 &= 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 16 \\ 3^2 &= 3 \times 3 = 9 \\ 10^3 &= 10 \times 10 \times 10 = 1000 \end{align*} \]

Base and Exponent:

In \( a^n \):

  • \( a \) is called the base
  • \( n \) is called the exponent or index

Special Cases:

Any number to the power of 1 is itself: \( a^1 = a \)

Any number to the power of 0 is 1: \( a^0 = 1 \) (where \( a \neq 0 \))

Laws of Indices

The Laws

There are several important laws that govern how indices work:

Remember These Rules

Multiplication Rule

\[ a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n} \]

When multiplying powers with the same base, add the exponents.

\[ 2^3 \times 2^4 = 2^{3+4} = 2^7 = 128 \]

Division Rule

\[ \frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n} \]

When dividing powers with the same base, subtract the exponents.

\[ \frac{5^6}{5^2} = 5^{6-2} = 5^4 = 625 \]

Power of a Power

\[ (a^m)^n = a^{m \times n} \]

When raising a power to another power, multiply the exponents.

\[ (3^2)^3 = 3^{2 \times 3} = 3^6 = 729 \]

Power of a Product

\[ (ab)^n = a^n \times b^n \]

When raising a product to a power, raise each factor to that power.

\[ (2 \times 5)^3 = 2^3 \times 5^3 = 8 \times 125 = 1000 \]

Power of a Quotient

\[ \left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n} \]

When raising a fraction to a power, raise both numerator and denominator to that power.

\[ \left(\frac{4}{3}\right)^2 = \frac{4^2}{3^2} = \frac{16}{9} \]

Negative Exponents

\[ a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n} \]

A negative exponent means the reciprocal of the positive exponent.

\[ 2^{-3} = \frac{1}{2^3} = \frac{1}{8} \]

Fractional Exponents

\[ a^{\frac{1}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{a} \]

A fractional exponent with numerator 1 represents a root.

\[ 8^{\frac{1}{3}} = \sqrt[3]{8} = 2 \]

Question 1

Simplify \( 3^4 \times 3^5 \)

Using the multiplication rule:

\[ 3^4 \times 3^5 = 3^{4+5} = 3^9 = 19683 \]

Question 2

Simplify \( \frac{7^8}{7^3} \)

Using the division rule:

\[ \frac{7^8}{7^3} = 7^{8-3} = 7^5 = 16807 \]

Question 3

Simplify \( (2^3)^4 \)

Using the power of a power rule:

\[ (2^3)^4 = 2^{3 \times 4} = 2^{12} = 4096 \]

Question 4

Simplify \( 5^{-2} \)

Using the negative exponent rule:

\[ 5^{-2} = \frac{1}{5^2} = \frac{1}{25} \]

Question 5

Simplify \( 16^{\frac{1}{2}} \)

Using the fractional exponent rule:

\[ 16^{\frac{1}{2}} = \sqrt{16} = 4 \]

Simple Problems

Basic Applications

Let's apply the laws of indices to solve some basic problems. These will help build your understanding before we move to more complex real-life applications.

Combining Rules

Often problems require using multiple rules together:

Simplify \( \frac{(2^3 \times 2^4)^2}{2^5} \)

Solution:

\[ \begin{align*} \frac{(2^3 \times 2^4)^2}{2^5} &= \frac{(2^{3+4})^2}{2^5} \quad \text{(Multiplication rule)} \\ &= \frac{(2^7)^2}{2^5} \\ &= \frac{2^{7 \times 2}}{2^5} \quad \text{(Power of a power)} \\ &= \frac{2^{14}}{2^5} \\ &= 2^{14-5} \quad \text{(Division rule)} \\ &= 2^9 \\ &= 512 \end{align*} \]

Equations with Indices

Solve for \( x \) in equations involving indices:

Solve \( 3^{x+2} = 81 \)

Solution:

\[ \begin{align*} 3^{x+2} &= 81 \\ 3^{x+2} &= 3^4 \quad \text{(Express 81 as power of 3)} \\ x + 2 &= 4 \quad \text{(Since bases are equal, exponents must be equal)} \\ x &= 4 - 2 \\ x &= 2 \end{align*} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Simplify \( \frac{(3^2 \times 3^5)^2}{3^6} \)

\[ \begin{align*} \frac{(3^2 \times 3^5)^2}{3^6} &= \frac{(3^{2+5})^2}{3^6} \\ &= \frac{(3^7)^2}{3^6} \\ &= \frac{3^{14}}{3^6} \\ &= 3^{14-6} \\ &= 3^8 \\ &= 6561 \end{align*} \]

Question 2

Solve for \( x \) in \( 2^{x-1} = 16 \)

\[ \begin{align*} 2^{x-1} &= 16 \\ 2^{x-1} &= 2^4 \\ x - 1 &= 4 \\ x &= 5 \end{align*} \]

Question 3

Simplify \( \left(\frac{5^3 \times 5^{-2}}{5^4}\right)^2 \)

\[ \begin{align*} \left(\frac{5^3 \times 5^{-2}}{5^4}\right)^2 &= \left(\frac{5^{3+(-2)}}{5^4}\right)^2 \\ &= \left(\frac{5^1}{5^4}\right)^2 \\ &= \left(5^{1-4}\right)^2 \\ &= \left(5^{-3}\right)^2 \\ &= 5^{-6} \\ &= \frac{1}{5^6} \\ &= \frac{1}{15625} \end{align*} \]

Real Life Applications

Practical Uses

Powers of natural numbers are used in many real-world situations. Here are some common applications:

Population Growth

Population often grows exponentially, which can be modeled using indices:

A bacteria population doubles every hour. If there are initially 100 bacteria, how many will there be after 6 hours?

Solution:

\[ \begin{align*} \text{Final population} &= \text{Initial} \times 2^{\text{hours}} \\ &= 100 \times 2^6 \\ &= 100 \times 64 \\ &= 6400 \text{ bacteria} \end{align*} \]

Compound Interest

Money invested grows exponentially with compound interest:

If you invest Gh₵1000 at 5% annual interest compounded yearly, what will be the value after 3 years?

Solution:

\[ \begin{align*} A &= P(1 + r)^n \\ &= 1000(1 + 0.05)^3 \\ &= 1000(1.05)^3 \\ &= 1000 \times 1.157625 \\ &= \text{Gh₵}1157.63 \end{align*} \]

Computer Memory

Computer memory sizes use powers of 2:

How many bytes are in 4 kilobytes (KB) if 1KB = 210 bytes?

Solution:

\[ \begin{align*} 4 \text{KB} &= 4 \times 2^{10} \text{ bytes} \\ &= 2^2 \times 2^{10} \\ &= 2^{12} \\ &= 4096 \text{ bytes} \end{align*} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A town's population grows at 10% per year. If the current population is 20,000, what will it be in 5 years?

\[ \begin{align*} \text{Future population} &= 20000 \times (1 + 0.10)^5 \\ &= 20000 \times (1.10)^5 \\ &= 20000 \times 1.61051 \\ &= 32210 \text{ people} \end{align*} \]

Question 2

A computer has 8GB of RAM. How many bytes is this if 1GB = 230 bytes?

\[ \begin{align*} 8 \text{GB} &= 8 \times 2^{30} \text{ bytes} \\ &= 2^3 \times 2^{30} \\ &= 2^{33} \\ &= 8,589,934,592 \text{ bytes} \end{align*} \]

Question 3

A radioactive substance halves every 3 hours. If you start with 512g, how much remains after 15 hours?

\[ \begin{align*} \text{Number of half-lives} &= \frac{15}{3} = 5 \\ \text{Remaining amount} &= 512 \times \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^5 \\ &= 512 \times \frac{1}{32} \\ &= 16 \text{g} \end{align*} \]

Advanced Problems

Challenging Applications

Now let's tackle some more complex problems that combine multiple concepts and require deeper thinking.

Combined Growth

A bank offers two investment options:

Option A: 8% interest compounded annually

Option B: 2% interest compounded quarterly

Which is better for a 2-year investment of Gh₵10,000?

Solution:

Option A:

\[ \begin{align*} A &= 10000(1 + 0.08)^2 \\ &= 10000 \times 1.1664 \\ &= \text{Gh₵}11,664 \end{align*} \]

Option B (quarterly means n=4):

\[ \begin{align*} A &= 10000\left(1 + \frac{0.02}{4}\right)^{4 \times 2} \\ &= 10000(1.005)^8 \\ &= 10000 \times 1.0407 \\ &= \text{Gh₵}10,407 \end{align*} \]

Option A is better.

Scientific Notation

Light travels at approximately \( 3 \times 10^8 \) m/s. How far does it travel in one day?

Solution:

\[ \begin{align*} \text{Distance} &= \text{Speed} \times \text{Time} \\ &= 3 \times 10^8 \times (24 \times 60 \times 60) \\ &= 3 \times 10^8 \times 86400 \\ &= 3 \times 10^8 \times 8.64 \times 10^4 \\ &= 25.92 \times 10^{8+4} \\ &= 2.592 \times 10^{13} \text{ meters} \end{align*} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A computer's processing speed doubles every 18 months (Moore's Law). If a computer today processes 1 billion operations per second, how many operations will it process in 6 years?

\[ \begin{align*} \text{Number of periods} &= \frac{6 \times 12}{18} = 4 \\ \text{Operations} &= 1 \times 10^9 \times 2^4 \\ &= 16 \times 10^9 \\ &= 16 \text{ billion operations/second} \end{align*} \]

Question 2

The area of Ghana is approximately \( 2.39 \times 10^5 \) km². If the population is 30 million, what is the population density (people per km²)?

\[ \begin{align*} \text{Density} &= \frac{30 \times 10^6}{2.39 \times 10^5} \\ &= \frac{30}{2.39} \times 10^{6-5} \\ &\approx 12.55 \times 10^1 \\ &\approx 125.5 \text{ people/km²} \end{align*} \]

Question 3

A smartphone app's user base grows by 50% every month. If it has 10,000 users now, how many users will it have in 6 months?

\[ \begin{align*} \text{Users} &= 10000 \times (1 + 0.5)^6 \\ &= 10000 \times (1.5)^6 \\ &= 10000 \times 11.390625 \\ &= 113,906 \text{ users} \end{align*} \]
