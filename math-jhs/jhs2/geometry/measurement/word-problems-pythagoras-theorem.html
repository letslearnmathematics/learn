Introduction

The Pythagoras Theorem is a fundamental principle in geometry that relates the sides of a right-angled triangle. It states that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides.

This theorem has numerous practical applications in real life, from construction and navigation to art and design. In this lesson, we'll learn how to use the Pythagoras theorem to calculate distances and areas in various real-world scenarios.

Theorem Statement

The Theorem

For any right-angled triangle with sides a and b, and hypotenuse c:

\[ c^2 = a^2 + b^2 \]

This can be rearranged to find any side:

\[ c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \] \[ a = \sqrt{c^2 - b^2} \] \[ b = \sqrt{c^2 - a^2} \]

Key Points

  1. The theorem only applies to right-angled triangles
  2. The hypotenuse is always the longest side
  3. The theorem can be used to find missing side lengths
  4. It can also be used to verify if a triangle is right-angled

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Find the hypotenuse of a right-angled triangle with other sides 3cm and 4cm.

Using Pythagoras theorem:

\[ c^2 = a^2 + b^2 \] \[ c^2 = 3^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25 \] \[ c = \sqrt{25} = 5 \text{cm} \]

Question 2

One side of a right-angled triangle is 5cm and the hypotenuse is 13cm. Find the other side.

Using Pythagoras theorem:

\[ b^2 = c^2 - a^2 \] \[ b^2 = 13^2 - 5^2 = 169 - 25 = 144 \] \[ b = \sqrt{144} = 12 \text{cm} \]

Area Calculation

Calculating Area

Once we've used the Pythagoras theorem to find all sides of a right-angled triangle, we can calculate its area using the standard formula:

\[ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} \]

For non-right-angled triangles, we may need to first divide them into right-angled triangles to apply the theorem before calculating the total area.

Steps to Find Area

  1. Identify if the triangle is right-angled or can be divided into right-angled triangles
  2. Use Pythagoras theorem to find any missing sides
  3. Apply the area formula using the perpendicular sides (for right-angled triangles)
  4. For other triangles, calculate areas of component right-angled triangles and sum them

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A right-angled triangle has sides of 6cm and 8cm. Find its area.

First verify these are the perpendicular sides (not hypotenuse):

\[ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times 6 \times 8 = 24 \text{cm}^2 \]

Question 2

Find the area of an equilateral triangle with side length 12cm.

Divide the equilateral triangle into two right-angled triangles:

\[ \text{Height} = \sqrt{12^2 - 6^2} = \sqrt{144 - 36} = \sqrt{108} \approx 10.39 \text{cm} \] \[ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times 12 \times 10.39 \approx 62.35 \text{cm}^2 \]

Real-life Applications

Practical Examples

The Pythagoras theorem is widely used in various real-world situations. Here are some examples we'll work through:

Example 1: Boat Navigation

A boat travels 2m South and then 9m East. How far is the boat from its starting point?

The boat's path forms a right-angled triangle:

\[ \text{Distance} = \sqrt{2^2 + 9^2} = \sqrt{4 + 81} = \sqrt{85} \approx 9.22 \text{m} \]

Example 2: Picture Frame

Yeboah hangs a picture frame of width 15cm on the wall. The distance from the nail to the edge of the picture frame is 10cm.

  1. Find the length of the wire used to hang the picture frame
  2. Find the area of the triangle formed

i) The wire forms two right-angled triangles:

\[ \text{Wire length} = 2 \times \sqrt{7.5^2 + 10^2} = 2 \times \sqrt{56.25 + 100} = 2 \times \sqrt{156.25} = 2 \times 12.5 = 25 \text{cm} \]

ii) Area of one triangle:

\[ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times 7.5 \times 10 = 37.5 \text{cm}^2 \]

Example 3: Ladder Problem

A ladder leans against a vertical wall of height 13m. If the foot of the ladder is 6m away from the wall, calculate the length of the ladder.

\[ \text{Ladder length} = \sqrt{13^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{169 + 36} = \sqrt{205} \approx 14.32 \text{m} \]

Example 4: Equilateral Triangle

The length of a side of an equilateral triangle is 12cm. Find:

  1. The height of the triangle
  2. The area of the triangle
  3. The perimeter of the triangle

i) Height:

\[ h = \sqrt{12^2 - 6^2} = \sqrt{144 - 36} = \sqrt{108} \approx 10.39 \text{cm} \]

ii) Area:

\[ A = \frac{1}{2} \times 12 \times 10.39 \approx 62.35 \text{cm}^2 \]

iii) Perimeter:

\[ P = 3 \times 12 = 36 \text{cm} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A rectangular field is 50m long and 30m wide. What is the length of the diagonal path across the field?

\[ \text{Diagonal} = \sqrt{50^2 + 30^2} = \sqrt{2500 + 900} = \sqrt{3400} \approx 58.31 \text{m} \]

Question 2

A television screen is advertised as 40 inches (measured diagonally). If the screen is 24 inches tall, how wide is it?

\[ \text{Width} = \sqrt{40^2 - 24^2} = \sqrt{1600 - 576} = \sqrt{1024} = 32 \text{inches} \]

Question 3

A kite is flying with 25m of string let out. If the kite is 15m above the ground, how far horizontally is the kite from the person holding the string?

\[ \text{Distance} = \sqrt{25^2 - 15^2} = \sqrt{625 - 225} = \sqrt{400} = 20 \text{m} \]
