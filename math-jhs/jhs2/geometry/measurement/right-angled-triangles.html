Introduction

The Pythagorean Theorem is a fundamental principle in geometry that relates the sides of a right-angled triangle. It states that:

In any right-angled triangle:

\[ a^2 + b^2 = c^2 \]

where:

  • \( c \) is the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle)
  • \( a \) and \( b \) are the lengths of the other two sides

Right-Angled Triangle:

A triangle with one angle exactly 90 degrees. The side opposite this angle is called the hypotenuse.

Applications:

The theorem is used to find missing side lengths in right-angled triangles and has applications in construction, navigation, and physics.

Square Grid Method

Constructing Squares on Sides

We can visualize the Pythagorean theorem by constructing squares on each side of a right-angled triangle and comparing their areas.

Step 1:

Draw a right-angled triangle on graph paper with sides a and b forming the right angle, and hypotenuse c.

Step 2:

Construct a square on each side of the triangle using the grid lines.

Step 3:

Count the number of unit squares in each larger square to determine their areas.

Step 4:

Compare the sum of areas of squares on sides a and b to the area of the square on hypotenuse c.

Example with a 3-4-5 triangle:

  • Square on side 3: Area = 9 units²
  • Square on side 4: Area = 16 units²
  • Square on hypotenuse 5: Area = 25 units²
  • Verification: 9 + 16 = 25

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Using graph paper, draw a right-angled triangle with sides 6 cm and 8 cm. Construct squares on all three sides and verify the Pythagorean theorem.

Expected results:

  • Square on 6 cm side: 36 cm²
  • Square on 8 cm side: 64 cm²
  • Square on hypotenuse (10 cm): 100 cm²
  • Verification: 36 + 64 = 100

Question 2

Using graph paper, draw a right-angled triangle with sides 5 cm and 12 cm. Construct squares on all three sides and verify the Pythagorean theorem.

Expected results:

  • Square on 5 cm side: 25 cm²
  • Square on 12 cm side: 144 cm²
  • Square on hypotenuse (13 cm): 169 cm²
  • Verification: 25 + 144 = 169

Compass & Ruler Method

Precise Construction Method

Using geometric tools (compass and ruler), we can construct accurate squares on the sides of any right-angled triangle to verify the Pythagorean relationship.

Step 1:

Draw a right-angled triangle ABC with right angle at B.

Step 2:

To construct a square on side AB:

  • Extend AB beyond B
  • Using compass, mark equal lengths to complete the square

Step 3:

Repeat the process for sides BC and AC.

Step 4:

Measure the sides of each square and calculate their areas.

Step 5:

Verify that the sum of areas of squares on AB and BC equals the area of the square on AC.

Example construction steps:

  1. Draw right angle at point B
  2. Mark AB = 4 cm, BC = 3 cm
  3. Complete the triangle by joining AC
  4. Construct squares on all three sides
  5. Measure AC and verify it's 5 cm
  6. Calculate areas: 16 cm² + 9 cm² = 25 cm²

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Using compass and ruler, construct a right-angled triangle with legs 7 cm and 24 cm. Then construct squares on all three sides and verify the Pythagorean theorem.

Expected results:

  • Square on 7 cm side: 49 cm²
  • Square on 24 cm side: 576 cm²
  • Square on hypotenuse (25 cm): 625 cm²
  • Verification: 49 + 576 = 625

Question 2

Construct a right-angled triangle with one leg 9 cm and hypotenuse 15 cm. Find the other leg, then construct squares on all sides and verify the theorem.

Solution steps:

  1. Use the theorem to find missing leg: √(15² - 9²) = √(225 - 81) = √144 = 12 cm
  2. Construct squares on 9 cm, 12 cm, and 15 cm sides
  3. Calculate areas: 81 cm² + 144 cm² = 225 cm²

Solving Problems

Application Problems

The Pythagorean theorem can be used to solve various practical problems involving right-angled triangles.

Finding the Hypotenuse

Given two legs, find the hypotenuse:

\[ c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \]

Example: A ladder leans against a wall. If the base is 3m from the wall and reaches 4m up, how long is the ladder?

Solution: \( c = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5 \) meters

Finding a Leg

Given one leg and the hypotenuse, find the other leg:

\[ a = \sqrt{c^2 - b^2} \]

Example: A right triangle has hypotenuse 13 cm and one leg 5 cm. Find the other leg.

Solution: \( a = \sqrt{13^2 - 5^2} = \sqrt{169 - 25} = \sqrt{144} = 12 \) cm

Word Problems

Apply the theorem to real-world scenarios like distances, diagonals, etc.

Example: You walk 300m east then 400m north. How far are you from your starting point?

Solution: \( d = \sqrt{300^2 + 400^2} = \sqrt{90,000 + 160,000} = \sqrt{250,000} = 500 \) meters

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A television screen measures 24 inches by 18 inches. What is the length of its diagonal?

Solution:

\[ d = \sqrt{24^2 + 18^2} = \sqrt{576 + 324} = \sqrt{900} = 30 \text{ inches} \]

Question 2

A rectangular park is 120m long and 50m wide. How much shorter is the walk diagonally across the park than walking along two sides?

Solution:

  1. Calculate diagonal: \( \sqrt{120^2 + 50^2} = \sqrt{14,400 + 2,500} = \sqrt{16,900} = 130 \) m
  2. Calculate perimeter walk: 120 + 50 = 170 m
  3. Difference: 170 - 130 = 40 m shorter

Question 3

A 5m ladder leans against a wall with its base 1.8m from the wall. How high up the wall does the ladder reach?

Solution:

\[ h = \sqrt{5^2 - 1.8^2} = \sqrt{25 - 3.24} = \sqrt{21.76} \approx 4.66 \text{ meters} \]

Question 4

Find the perimeter of a right-angled triangle with legs 6 cm and 8 cm.

Solution:

  1. Find hypotenuse: \( \sqrt{6^2 + 8^2} = \sqrt{36 + 64} = \sqrt{100} = 10 \) cm
  2. Calculate perimeter: 6 + 8 + 10 = 24 cm
