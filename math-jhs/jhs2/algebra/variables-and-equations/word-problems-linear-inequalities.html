Introduction

Linear inequalities are mathematical expressions that show the relationship between two quantities where one is not necessarily equal to the other. They use inequality symbols like < (less than), > (greater than), ≤ (less than or equal to), and ≥ (greater than or equal to).

In this lesson, we will learn how to translate word problems into linear inequalities in one variable, and vice versa. This skill is essential for solving real-world problems where exact equality may not be possible or necessary.

Examples of linear inequalities:

\[ x + 3 > 7 \] \[ 2y - 5 \leq 10 \] \[ \frac{z}{4} < 12 \]

Key Inequality Symbols:

  • < : Less than
  • > : Greater than
  • ≤ : Less than or equal to
  • ≥ : Greater than or equal to

Variable Representation:

We typically use letters like x, y, or z to represent unknown quantities in inequalities.

Translating Word Problems to Inequalities

Step-by-Step Process

To translate a word problem into a linear inequality:

  1. Identify the unknown quantity and assign it a variable (usually x).
  2. Look for key phrases that indicate inequality relationships.
  3. Write the mathematical expression based on the relationship.
  4. Simplify the inequality if necessary.

Example 1

Problem: A number increased by 5 is at least 12.

Solution:

  1. Let the number be \( x \)
  2. "Increased by 5" translates to \( x + 5 \)
  3. "At least" means \( \geq \)
  4. Final inequality: \( x + 5 \geq 12 \)

Example 2

Problem: Twice a number is less than 20.

Solution:

  1. Let the number be \( y \)
  2. "Twice a number" translates to \( 2y \)
  3. "Less than" means \( < \)
  4. Final inequality: \( 2y < 20 \)

Example 3

Problem: The product of 3 and a number is at most 15.

Solution:

  1. Let the number be \( z \)
  2. "Product of 3 and a number" translates to \( 3z \)
  3. "At most" means \( \leq \)
  4. Final inequality: \( 3z \leq 15 \)

Key Phrases and Their Mathematical Meanings

Word Phrase Inequality Symbol
Less than <
Greater than >
At least ≥
At most ≤
No more than ≤
No less than ≥
Maximum of ≤
Minimum of ≥

Translating Inequalities to Word Problems

Step-by-Step Process

To translate a linear inequality into a word problem:

  1. Identify the variable and what it represents.
  2. Translate the mathematical operations into words.
  3. Use appropriate phrases for the inequality symbol.
  4. Combine all parts into a coherent statement.

Example 1

Inequality: \( x - 3 \leq 7 \)

Solution:

  1. Let \( x \) represent a number
  2. "\( x - 3 \)" translates to "3 less than a number"
  3. "\( \leq \)" means "is at most" or "is no more than"
  4. Word problem: "Three less than a number is no more than 7."

Example 2

Inequality: \( 2y > 14 \)

Solution:

  1. Let \( y \) represent a number
  2. "\( 2y \)" translates to "twice a number"
  3. "\( > \)" means "is greater than"
  4. Word problem: "Twice a number is greater than 14."

Example 3

Inequality: \( \frac{z}{4} \geq 5 \)

Solution:

  1. Let \( z \) represent a number
  2. "\( \frac{z}{4} \)" translates to "a number divided by 4" or "one-fourth of a number"
  3. "\( \geq \)" means "is at least"
  4. Word problem: "One-fourth of a number is at least 5."

Real-life Applications

Practical Examples

Linear inequalities are used in many real-world situations. Here are some examples:

Budgeting

Scenario: You have Gh₵500 to spend on books and stationery. Books cost Gh₵80 each and stationery costs Gh₵20 per set. You want to buy at least 2 books.

Inequality:

Let \( x \) = number of books, \( y \) = number of stationery sets

\( 80x + 20y \leq 500 \) with \( x \geq 2 \)

Age Restrictions

Scenario: To ride a roller coaster, you must be at least 12 years old and less than 65 years old.

Inequality:

Let \( a \) = age of rider

\( 12 \leq a < 65 \)

Speed Limits

Scenario: The speed limit on a highway is between 50 km/h (minimum) and 100 km/h (maximum).

Inequality:

Let \( s \) = speed of vehicle

\( 50 \leq s \leq 100 \)

Practice Exercise

Question 1

You need to score at least 50 points to pass a test. Write an inequality to represent this situation.

Let \( p \) = test score

Inequality: \( p \geq 50 \)

Question 2

A mobile phone plan gives you 500 free minutes per month. Additional minutes cost Gh₵0.50 each. If you want to spend no more than Gh₵100 on calls this month, write an inequality to represent this situation.

Let \( m \) = additional minutes used

Cost for additional minutes: \( 0.5m \)

Inequality: \( 0.5m \leq 100 \)

Question 3

A rectangle has a length that is 5 cm more than its width. The perimeter must be less than 50 cm. Write an inequality to represent this situation.

Let \( w \) = width, then length = \( w + 5 \)

Perimeter = \( 2(w + (w + 5)) = 2(2w + 5) = 4w + 10 \)

Inequality: \( 4w + 10 < 50 \)

Question 4

To qualify for a scholarship, a student's average in 5 subjects must be at least 80. The student has scored 85, 78, 92, and 88 in four subjects. Write an inequality to find the minimum score needed in the fifth subject.

Let \( x \) = fifth subject score

Average = \( \frac{85 + 78 + 92 + 88 + x}{5} \)

Inequality: \( \frac{343 + x}{5} \geq 80 \)

Question 5

A shop sells two types of pens: Type A costs Gh₵3 and Type B costs Gh₵5. You want to buy at least 5 pens but spend no more than Gh₵30. Write a system of inequalities to represent this situation.

Let \( a \) = number of Type A pens, \( b \) = number of Type B pens

Total pens: \( a + b \geq 5 \)

Total cost: \( 3a + 5b \leq 30 \)
