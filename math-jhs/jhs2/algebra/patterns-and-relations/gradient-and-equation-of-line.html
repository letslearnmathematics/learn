Introduction

Gradient (also called slope) measures how steep a line is. It's a fundamental concept in coordinate geometry that helps us describe and analyze linear relationships.

In this lesson, we'll learn how to calculate gradient in different situations and use it to write equations of straight lines in both slope-intercept form (y = mx + c) and point-slope form.

Key Applications:

  • Determining steepness of roads or ramps
  • Analyzing trends in data
  • Solving problems in physics (like velocity)
  • Creating linear models in economics

Gradient Concept

Understanding Gradient

The gradient (or slope) of a line describes its steepness and direction:

  • Positive gradient: Line rises from left to right
  • Negative gradient: Line falls from left to right
  • Zero gradient: Horizontal line (no steepness)
  • Undefined gradient: Vertical line (infinite steepness)

Real-Life Examples

Road Inclines

A 10% gradient means the road rises 10m vertically for every 100m horizontally.

Roof Pitch

A roof with a steep gradient sheds water more effectively.

Hiking Trails

Trail difficulty is often determined by its average gradient.

Gradient Formula

The Gradient Formula

The gradient (m) between two points \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\) is calculated as:

\[ m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{\text{Change in y}}{\text{Change in x}} = \frac{\text{Rise}}{\text{Run}} \]

This represents the ratio of vertical change to horizontal change between two points on the line.

Important Notes

Consistency

Always subtract coordinates in the same order (point 2 - point 1) for both x and y.

Undefined Gradient

If \(x_2 - x_1 = 0\) (vertical line), the gradient is undefined.

Zero Gradient

If \(y_2 - y_1 = 0\) (horizontal line), the gradient is 0.

Gradient from Coordinates

Calculating Gradient Between Two Points

Given any two points on a line, we can calculate the gradient using the formula:

\[ m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \]

Example 1:

Find the gradient of the line passing through (2, 3) and (5, 11).

Solution:

\[ m = \frac{11 - 3}{5 - 2} = \frac{8}{3} \]

Example 2:

Find the gradient of the line through (-1, 4) and (3, -2).

Solution:

\[ m = \frac{-2 - 4}{3 - (-1)} = \frac{-6}{4} = -\frac{3}{2} \]

Gradient from Equation

Finding Gradient from y = mx + c

When the equation of a line is in the slope-intercept form:

\[ y = mx + c \]

The coefficient of x (m) is the gradient, and c is the y-intercept.

Examples

Example 1:

Find the gradient of the line \( y = 3x - 5 \).

Solution: Gradient (m) = 3

Example 2:

Find the gradient of \( 2y = 8x + 3 \).

Solution: First rewrite in y = mx + c form:

\[ y = 4x + \frac{3}{2} \]

Gradient (m) = 4

Example 3:

Find the gradient of \( 3x + 4y = 12 \).

Solution: Rearrange to slope-intercept form:

\[ 4y = -3x + 12 \] \[ y = -\frac{3}{4}x + 3 \]

Gradient (m) = -¾

Gradient from Graph

Determining Gradient Visually

To find gradient from a graph:

  1. Identify two clear points on the line
  2. Calculate the vertical change (rise) between them
  3. Calculate the horizontal change (run) between them
  4. Divide rise by run to get gradient

Example:

A line passes through (1, 1) and (4, 3) on a graph. Find its gradient.

Solution:

\[ \text{Rise} = 3 - 1 = 2 \] \[ \text{Run} = 4 - 1 = 3 \] \[ m = \frac{2}{3} \]

Slope-Intercept Form

y = mx + c Form

The slope-intercept form is the most common way to express a linear equation:

\[ y = mx + c \]

Where:

  • m is the gradient (slope)
  • c is the y-intercept (where the line crosses the y-axis)

Writing Equations

To write the equation when given gradient and y-intercept:

  1. Identify the gradient (m)
  2. Identify the y-intercept (c)
  3. Substitute into y = mx + c

Example 1:

Write the equation of a line with gradient 2 and y-intercept -3.

Solution: \( y = 2x - 3 \)

Example 2:

A line has gradient -½ and passes through (0, 4). Write its equation.

Solution: The y-intercept is 4, so \( y = -\frac{1}{2}x + 4 \)

Point-Slope Form

y - y₁ = m(x - x₁) Form

The point-slope form is useful when you know:

  • The gradient (m)
  • Any point \((x_1, y_1)\) on the line
\[ y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) \]

Writing Equations

To write the equation using point-slope form:

  1. Identify the gradient (m)
  2. Identify a point \((x_1, y_1)\) on the line
  3. Substitute into the point-slope formula
  4. Simplify to slope-intercept form if needed

Example 1:

Find the equation of a line with gradient 3 passing through (2, 5).

Solution:

\[ y - 5 = 3(x - 2) \] \[ y - 5 = 3x - 6 \] \[ y = 3x - 1 \]

Example 2:

A line has gradient -2 and passes through (-1, 4). Write its equation.

Solution:

\[ y - 4 = -2(x - (-1)) \] \[ y - 4 = -2(x + 1) \] \[ y - 4 = -2x - 2 \] \[ y = -2x + 2 \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Find the gradient of the line passing through (3, 7) and (5, 13).

Using the gradient formula:

\[ m = \frac{13 - 7}{5 - 3} = \frac{6}{2} = 3 \]

Question 2

Determine the gradient of the line with equation \( 4x + 2y = 6 \).

First rewrite in slope-intercept form:

\[ 2y = -4x + 6 \] \[ y = -2x + 3 \]

Gradient (m) = -2

Question 3

Write the equation of a line with gradient -3 and y-intercept 5.

Using slope-intercept form:

\[ y = -3x + 5 \]

Question 4

Find the equation of the line passing through (1, 2) with gradient 4.

Using point-slope form:

\[ y - 2 = 4(x - 1) \] \[ y - 2 = 4x - 4 \] \[ y = 4x - 2 \]

Question 5

A line passes through (0, 3) and (2, 7). Write its equation in slope-intercept form.

First find gradient:

\[ m = \frac{7 - 3}{2 - 0} = \frac{4}{2} = 2 \]

y-intercept is 3 (from point (0,3))

Equation: \( y = 2x + 3 \)
