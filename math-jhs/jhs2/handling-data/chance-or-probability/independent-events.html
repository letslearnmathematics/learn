Introduction

Probability is the measure of how likely an event is to occur. When dealing with multiple events, we need to understand whether they are independent or dependent. Independent events are those where the outcome of one event does not affect the outcome of another event.

In this lesson, we will explore probability experiments involving two independent events, such as drawing colored bottle tops from a bag with replacement, and learn how to list the elements of the sample space.

Sample Space

Understanding Sample Space

The sample space of an experiment is the set of all possible outcomes of that experiment.

Example 1

In an experiment, Emmanuel was asked to pick one bottle top from a bag, three times, which contains 3 red, 2 green and 1 pink bottle tops.

i. List the elements of the sample space of the events.

ii. The sample space of the event of picking a red bottle top, R, with replacement is?

iii. The probability of picking a red bottle top is ........

Solution:

i. The sample space for one draw is {Red, Green, Pink}. For three draws with replacement, the sample space consists of all possible ordered triples:

{ (R,R,R), (R,R,G), (R,R,P), (R,G,R), (R,G,G), (R,G,P), (R,P,R), (R,P,G), (R,P,P),
(G,R,R), (G,R,G), (G,R,P), (G,G,R), (G,G,G), (G,G,P), (G,P,R), (G,P,G), (G,P,P),
(P,R,R), (P,R,G), (P,R,P), (P,G,R), (P,G,G), (P,G,P), (P,P,R), (P,P,G), (P,P,P) }

ii. When focusing only on red bottle tops with replacement, the sample space remains the same for each draw: {Red, Not Red}

iii. Probability of picking a red bottle top:

Total bottle tops = 3 (Red) + 2 (Green) + 1 (Pink) = 6

P(Red) = Number of red tops / Total tops = 3/6 = 1/2 or 0.5

Independent Events

Characteristics of Independent Events

Two events are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other occurring.

Example 2

Consider the following two events: (a) throwing of a fair six-sided die and (b) tossing a fair coin.

i. What is the sample space for (a) and for (b)?

ii. Does the occurrence of event (a) affect the occurrence of event (b)?

iii. What is the probability of an even number showing up in (a)? What is the probability of a head showing up in (b)?

iv. What is the relationship between the two events?

Solution:

i. Sample spaces:

For the die: {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}

For the coin: {Head, Tail}

ii. No, the outcome of the die throw does not affect the outcome of the coin toss.

iii. Probabilities:

Even numbers on die: 2, 4, 6 → P(Even) = 3/6 = 1/2

Head on coin: P(Head) = 1/2

iv. The two events are independent because the outcome of one does not influence the outcome of the other.

Probability Calculation

Calculating Probabilities of Independent Events

For independent events A and B, the probability of both occurring is:

\[ P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B) \]

Example 3

Ampofo and Serwa are two learners from a school. Ampofo walks to school daily and Serwa travels to school on a bus daily.

i. Does the event of Ampofo walking to school affect that of Serwa traveling by bus?

ii. Can the two events occur together?

Solution:

i. No, Ampofo's method of getting to school does not affect Serwa's method of transportation.

ii. Yes, the two events can occur together (Ampofo walks while Serwa takes the bus) because they are independent.

Real World Examples

Applying Probability Concepts

Independent events are common in everyday situations. Here are some examples:

Weather and Commute

Whether it rains today and whether your friend is late to school are typically independent events.

Games of Chance

In a game where you spin a wheel and draw a card, these are independent events because the wheel outcome doesn't affect the card drawn.

Manufacturing

If two machines in a factory produce items, and one breaks down, it typically doesn't affect the other machine's operation (assuming they're separate systems).

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A bag contains 4 blue marbles and 6 red marbles. A marble is drawn at random, its color noted, and then replaced. This is done twice. What is the probability that both marbles drawn are blue?

P(Blue) = 4/10 = 2/5

Since the draws are independent with replacement:

P(Both Blue) = (2/5) × (2/5) = 4/25

Question 2

A fair coin is tossed and a fair six-sided die is rolled. What is the probability of getting a head on the coin and an even number on the die?

P(Head) = 1/2

P(Even on die) = 3/6 = 1/2

Since these are independent events:

P(Head and Even) = (1/2) × (1/2) = 1/4

Question 3

In a school, 60% of students walk to school and 30% bring their lunch. Assuming these events are independent, what is the probability that a randomly selected student both walks to school and brings their lunch?

P(Walk) = 0.6

P(Brings Lunch) = 0.3

P(Walk and Brings Lunch) = 0.6 × 0.3 = 0.18 or 18%

Question 4

A box contains 5 white balls and 7 black balls. Two balls are drawn successively with replacement. What is the probability that both balls are white?

P(White) = 5/12

Since the draws are independent with replacement:

P(Both White) = (5/12) × (5/12) = 25/144

Question 5

In a game, you spin a spinner with equal sections labeled 1-4 and roll a die. What is the probability of spinning a 3 and rolling a number greater than 4?

P(Spin 3) = 1/4

P(Die > 4) = P(5 or 6) = 2/6 = 1/3

Since these are independent events:

P(3 and >4) = (1/4) × (1/3) = 1/12
