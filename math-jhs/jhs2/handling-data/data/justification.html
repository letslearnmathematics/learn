Introduction

Measures of central tendency are statistical values that represent the center or typical value of a dataset. The three main measures are:

Mean (Average):

The sum of all values divided by the number of values.

\[ \text{Mean} = \frac{\sum x}{n} \]

Median:

The middle value when data is ordered from smallest to largest.

Mode:

The most frequently occurring value in the dataset.

Choosing the most appropriate measure depends on the nature of your data and what you want to communicate. Each measure has strengths and weaknesses in different contexts.

When to Use the Mean

Appropriate Uses of the Mean

The mean is most appropriate when:

  1. The data is numerical and continuous (e.g., heights, weights, test scores)
  2. The distribution is approximately symmetrical (not skewed)
  3. There are no extreme outliers that would distort the average
  4. You need to include all values in your calculation
  5. You plan to do further statistical analysis (like calculating standard deviation)

Real-World Examples

Example 1: Test Scores

Calculating the average score for a class test where most students scored between 60-80%, with a few slightly higher or lower.

Why mean? The data is numerical, continuous, and relatively symmetric.

Example 2: Monthly Rainfall

Finding the average monthly rainfall over a year to understand typical weather patterns.

Why mean? We want to account for all rainfall measurements equally.

When Not to Use the Mean

The mean can be misleading when:

  • Data has extreme outliers (e.g., one billionaire in a neighborhood income survey)
  • The distribution is highly skewed
  • Data is categorical (e.g., favorite colors)
  • There are open-ended ranges (e.g., "50+ years old")

When to Use the Median

Appropriate Uses of the Median

The median is most appropriate when:

  1. The data has extreme values or outliers
  2. The distribution is skewed (not symmetrical)
  3. Data includes ordinal measurements (rankings where distances between values aren't equal)
  4. There are open-ended ranges in the data
  5. You want to find the typical value unaffected by extremes

Real-World Examples

Example 1: Household Income

Reporting income in a community where most earn $20,000-$50,000 but a few earn over $1,000,000.

Why median? The few high earners would distort the mean, but median shows the typical income.

Example 2: House Prices

Comparing housing markets where most homes cost $100,000-$300,000 but a few mansions cost millions.

Why median? Gives a better sense of what most buyers would pay.

When Not to Use the Median

The median may not be ideal when:

  • You need to account for all values in your analysis
  • The data is symmetrical with no outliers
  • You plan to do further statistical calculations that require the mean
  • The dataset is very small (median might not represent the data well)

When to Use the Mode

Appropriate Uses of the Mode

The mode is most appropriate when:

  1. Data is categorical (non-numerical categories)
  2. You want to know the most common or most popular item
  3. Working with discrete data with repeated values
  4. Looking for peaks in a distribution
  5. Dealing with nominal data (data that can't be ordered)

Real-World Examples

Example 1: Favorite Colors

Surveying a class to find the most popular color among students.

Why mode? Colors are categorical data - can't calculate mean or median.

Example 2: Shoe Sizes

A shoe store wants to know which size they should stock the most of.

Why mode? Identifies the size that occurs most frequently among customers.

When Not to Use the Mode

The mode may not be ideal when:

  • There is no repetition in the data (all values are unique)
  • There are multiple modes of similar frequency
  • You need to account for all values in the dataset
  • Data is continuous numerical with many possible values

Comparing Measures of Central Tendency

Decision Guide

Situation Best Measure Reason
Data is numerical and symmetrical Mean Uses all data points equally
Data has extreme outliers Median Not affected by extreme values
Categorical data (colors, brands) Mode Only measure that works for categories
Ordinal data (rankings, ratings) Median Maintains order but not affected by distance between ranks
Need to identify most common value Mode Directly shows highest frequency

Visual Comparison

Consider these three datasets and how each measure behaves:

Symmetrical Data

2, 3, 3, 4, 5, 5, 6

Mean = Median = Mode ≈ 4

Right-Skewed Data

2, 3, 3, 4, 5, 5, 15

Mean (5.29) > Median (4) > Mode (3 and 5)

Left-Skewed Data

Mean (5.43) < Median (6) < Mode (7)

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A teacher records the following test scores: 85, 90, 78, 92, 88, 95, 100, 85, 82, 90. Which measure of central tendency would best represent the class performance if she wants to calculate the average score?

Answer: The mean would be most appropriate here.

Reason: The data is numerical, symmetrical with no extreme outliers, and we want to account for all scores equally.

Question 2

A real estate agent has the following house prices (in thousands): 150, 175, 200, 225, 250, 300, 350, 400, 2000. Which measure should she use to describe the "typical" house price to potential buyers?

Answer: The median would be most appropriate here.

Reason: There's an extreme outlier ($2,000,000) that would distort the mean. The median gives a better sense of what most houses cost.

Question 3

A shoe store owner wants to know which size to order the most of. She has sales data showing sizes sold last month: 7, 7, 8, 8, 8, 8, 9, 9, 10, 10, 11. Which measure should she use?

Answer: The mode would be most appropriate here.

Reason: She wants to know the most frequently sold size, which is exactly what the mode identifies (size 8).

Question 4

A survey asks people to rate a movie on a scale of 1-5 stars: 1, 3, 3, 4, 4, 4, 4, 5, 5, 5. Which measure would best summarize the overall reception?

Answer: Either the median or mode could be appropriate here.

Reason: For ordinal data like ratings, the median (4) gives the middle rating, while the mode (4) shows the most common rating. The mean could be used but assumes equal distance between ratings.

Question 5

A company reports employee salaries: $30k, $32k, $35k, $38k, $40k, $42k, $45k, $50k, $300k. Which measure should they use in their annual report to represent "typical" employee earnings?

Answer: The median would be most appropriate here.

Reason: The CEO's high salary ($300k) is an extreme outlier that would make the mean much higher than what most employees earn. The median isn't affected by this outlier.
