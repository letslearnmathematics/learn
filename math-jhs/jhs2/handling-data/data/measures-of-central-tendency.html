Introduction

Measures of central tendency are values that describe the center or typical value of a dataset. The three main measures are mean, median, and mode.

Mean (Average)

The sum of all values divided by the number of values:

\[ \text{Mean} = \frac{\sum x}{n} \]

Median

The middle value when data is ordered from smallest to largest.

For even number of values: average of two middle numbers.

Mode

The value that appears most frequently in the dataset.

A dataset can have no mode or multiple modes.

From Bar Charts

Key Steps

To find mean, median and mode from a bar chart:

  1. Extract all data values from the bar chart
  2. List the values in order (helps find median)
  3. Calculate mean by summing values and dividing by count
  4. Find median by locating middle value(s)
  5. Identify mode as the value with tallest bar

Example 1: Simple Bar Chart

Data values: 5, 5, 7, 8, 8, 8, 9, 10, 10, 10, 10

Mean: \( \frac{5+5+7+8+8+8+9+10+10+10+10}{11} = \frac{90}{11} \approx 8.18 \)

Median: 6th value = 8

Mode: 10 (appears 4 times)

Example 2: Grouped Bar Chart

Data values: 12, 12, 12, 15, 15, 15, 15, 15, 18, 18, 20, 20, 20, 20, 20, 20, 20, 22, 22, 22

Mean: \( \frac{3\times12 + 5\times15 + 2\times18 + 7\times20 + 3\times22}{20} = \frac{356}{20} = 17.8 \)

Median: Average of 10th and 11th values = \( \frac{18+20}{2} = 19 \)

Mode: 20 (appears 7 times)

From Frequency Tables

Key Steps

To find mean, median and mode from a frequency table:

  1. Calculate total sum: multiply each value by its frequency and sum all products
  2. Find total count: sum all frequencies
  3. Mean = Total sum ÷ Total count
  4. For median: find cumulative frequencies to locate middle position
  5. Mode is the value with highest frequency

Example 1: Simple Frequency Table

Total sum: \( (5×2) + (6×4) + (7×6) + (8×5) + (9×3) = 10 + 24 + 42 + 40 + 27 = 143 \)

Total count: \( 2 + 4 + 6 + 5 + 3 = 20 \)

Mean: \( \frac{143}{20} = 7.15 \)

Median: Average of 10th and 11th values (both 7) = 7

Mode: 7 (highest frequency of 6)

Example 2: Grouped Frequency Table

Note: For grouped data, we use midpoints of each class interval

Midpoints: 25, 35, 45, 55, 65, 75

Total sum: \( (25×3) + (35×7) + (45×12) + (55×8) + (65×5) + (75×5) = 75 + 245 + 540 + 440 + 325 + 375 = 2000 \)

Total count: \( 3 + 7 + 12 + 8 + 5 + 5 = 40 \)

Mean: \( \frac{2000}{40} = 50 \)

Median class: 40-50 (contains 20th and 21st values)

Modal class: 40-50 (highest frequency of 12)

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Calculate the mean, median and mode for this frequency table.

Total sum: \( (1×5) + (2×8) + (3×12) + (4×10) + (5×5) = 5 + 16 + 36 + 40 + 25 = 122 \)

Total count: \( 5 + 8 + 12 + 10 + 5 = 40 \)

Mean: \( \frac{122}{40} = 3.05 \)

Median: Average of 20th and 21st values = \( \frac{3+3}{2} = 3 \)

Mode: 3 (highest frequency of 12)

Question 2

Find the mean, median class and modal class for this grouped frequency table.

Midpoints: 105, 115, 125, 135, 145

Total sum: \( (105×4) + (115×9) + (125×7) + (135×5) + (145×5) = 420 + 1035 + 875 + 675 + 725 = 3730 \)

Total count: \( 4 + 9 + 7 + 5 + 5 = 30 \)

Mean: \( \frac{3730}{30} \approx 124.33 \)

Median class: 120-130 (contains 15th and 16th values)

Modal class: 110-120 (highest frequency of 9)

Comparing Measures

When Measures Are Equal

In perfectly symmetrical distributions (like normal distributions), the mean, median and mode are all equal:

Example dataset: 4, 5, 5, 6, 6, 6, 6, 6, 7, 7, 8

Mean: \( \frac{4+5+5+6+6+6+6+6+7+7+8}{11} = \frac{66}{11} = 6 \)

Median: 6 (middle value)

Mode: 6 (appears 5 times)

When Measures Differ

Measures differ when data is skewed or has outliers:

Right-Skewed Data

Mean > Median > Mode

Example: Income data with few very high earners

Left-Skewed Data

Mean < Median < Mode

Example: Exam scores with few very low scores

Bimodal Data

Two distinct modes, mean and median between them

Example: Heights of mixed gender population

Choosing the Best Measure

Use Mean When

  • Data is symmetrical
  • No extreme outliers
  • Need to use the value in further calculations

Use Median When

  • Data is skewed
  • There are outliers
  • Working with ordinal data

Use Mode When

  • Working with categorical data
  • Need to know most common value
  • Data has multiple peaks

Practice Exercise

Question 1

For the dataset: 2, 3, 3, 4, 4, 4, 5, 5, 6, 6, 7, 50

a) Calculate mean, median and mode

b) Which measure best represents the "typical" value? Why?

a)

Mean: \( \frac{2+3+3+4+4+4+5+5+6+6+7+50}{12} = \frac{99}{12} = 8.25 \)

Median: \( \frac{4+5}{2} = 4.5 \)

Mode: 4 (appears 3 times)

b) The median (4.5) best represents the typical value because the mean is skewed higher by the outlier (50). The mode (4) is also reasonable but doesn't account for all values.

Question 2

Create a dataset where:

a) Mean = Median = Mode

b) Mean > Median > Mode

c) Mode > Median > Mean

Possible answers:

a) 3, 4, 4, 5, 5, 5, 5, 6, 6, 7 (Mean=Median=Mode=5)

b) 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 50 (Mean=9.5, Median=5.5, Mode=none or all)

c) 10, 10, 10, 9, 9, 8, 8, 7, 7, 1 (Mean=7.9, Median=8.5, Mode=10)
