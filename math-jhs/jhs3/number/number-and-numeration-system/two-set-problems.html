Introduction

Sets are fundamental mathematical objects that help us organize and categorize numbers or objects. In this lesson, we'll apply set concepts to solve problems involving rational numbers and real-life situations.

We'll focus on two key operations with sets:

  • Union (∪): Combines all elements from both sets
  • Intersection (∩): Contains only elements common to both sets

Given sets A and B:

\[ \begin{align*} A &= \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5\} \\ B &= \{4, 5, 6, 7\} \\ A \cup B &= \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7\} \\ A \cap B &= \{4, 5\} \end{align*} \]

Union and Intersection

Understanding the Concepts

The union and intersection of sets can be visualized using Venn diagrams:

Union (A ∪ B)

All elements that are in A, or in B, or in both:

\[ n(A \cup B) = n(A) + n(B) - n(A \cap B) \]

Intersection (A ∩ B)

Only elements that are in both A and B:

If sets are disjoint: \( A \cap B = \emptyset \)

Key Formula

The fundamental relationship between union and intersection:

\[ n(A \cup B) = n(A) + n(B) - n(A \cap B) \]

This formula helps solve many real-world problems involving two sets.

Real Life Problems

Farmer's Problem

Let's solve the given problem about farmers growing crops:

Problem: There are 80 farmers in a certain village who grow either maize or beans. Fifty of them grow beans while sixty grow maize. If each farmer grows at least one of the two crops, represent the information on a Venn diagram and hence find the number of farmers who grow:

  1. Both crops
  2. Only one crop

Solution:

Let:

  • M = set of farmers growing maize
  • B = set of farmers growing beans

Given:

  • Total farmers: \( n(M \cup B) = 80 \)
  • Farmers growing beans: \( n(B) = 50 \)
  • Farmers growing maize: \( n(M) = 60 \)
  • Each farmer grows at least one crop (no one grows neither)

Using the union formula:

\[ \begin{align*} n(M \cup B) &= n(M) + n(B) - n(M \cap B) \\ 80 &= 60 + 50 - n(M \cap B) \\ n(M \cap B) &= 110 - 80 = 30 \end{align*} \]

Therefore:

  1. Farmers growing both crops: \( n(M \cap B) = 30 \)
  2. Farmers growing only one crop:
    • Only maize: \( n(M) - n(M \cap B) = 60 - 30 = 30 \)
    • Only beans: \( n(B) - n(M \cap B) = 50 - 30 = 20 \)
    • Total growing only one crop: \( 30 + 20 = 50 \)

Practice Exercise

Question 1

In a class of 50 students, 30 study French, 25 study German, and 10 study both languages. How many students study:

  1. Only French?
  2. Only German?
  3. At least one language?

Solution:

Let F = French students, G = German students

Given: n(F) = 30, n(G) = 25, n(F ∩ G) = 10, Total = 50

  1. Only French: n(F) - n(F ∩ G) = 30 - 10 = 20
  2. Only German: n(G) - n(F ∩ G) = 25 - 10 = 15
  3. At least one language: n(F ∪ G) = n(F) + n(G) - n(F ∩ G) = 30 + 25 - 10 = 45

Question 2

A survey of 100 people found that 60 read the Daily Graphic, 45 read the Ghanaian Times, and 20 read neither. How many people read:

  1. Both newspapers?
  2. Exactly one newspaper?

Solution:

Let D = Daily Graphic readers, T = Ghanaian Times readers

Given: n(D) = 60, n(T) = 45, n(neither) = 20, Total = 100

Number reading at least one paper: 100 - 20 = 80

Using union formula:

n(D ∪ T) = n(D) + n(T) - n(D ∩ T)

80 = 60 + 45 - n(D ∩ T)

n(D ∩ T) = 105 - 80 = 25

  1. Both newspapers: 25
  2. Exactly one newspaper:
    • Only Daily Graphic: 60 - 25 = 35
    • Only Ghanaian Times: 45 - 25 = 20
    • Total: 35 + 20 = 55

Question 3

In a sports club with 120 members, 70 play football, 50 play basketball, and 15 play both sports. How many members:

  1. Play only football?
  2. Play only basketball?
  3. Play neither sport?

Solution:

Let F = football players, B = basketball players

Given: n(F) = 70, n(B) = 50, n(F ∩ B) = 15, Total = 120

  1. Only football: n(F) - n(F ∩ B) = 70 - 15 = 55
  2. Only basketball: n(B) - n(F ∩ B) = 50 - 15 = 35
  3. Neither sport:

    First find n(F ∪ B) = 70 + 50 - 15 = 105

    Then neither = 120 - 105 = 15
