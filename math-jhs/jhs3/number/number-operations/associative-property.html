Introduction

The associative property is a fundamental property of numbers that states that the way in which numbers are grouped in addition or multiplication does not change their sum or product.

This property applies to both addition and multiplication, but not to subtraction or division. Understanding this property helps simplify calculations and solve complex problems more efficiently.

Key Features:

  • Only applies to addition and multiplication
  • Changing grouping doesn't change the result
  • Works with three or more numbers

Associative Property of Addition

The Property

For any three numbers a, b, and c, the associative property of addition states:

\[ a + (b + c) = (a + b) + c \]

Example: Let's use the numbers 2, 3, and 5

\[ 2 + (3 + 5) = (2 + 3) + 5 \] \[ 2 + 8 = 5 + 5 \] \[ 10 = 10 \]

Why It Works

The associative property works because addition is about combining quantities, and the order in which we combine them doesn't affect the total amount.

Think of it like grouping different sets of objects - no matter how you group them, the total number remains the same.

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Show that the associative property holds for the numbers 4, 7, and 9.

Using the associative property:

\[ 4 + (7 + 9) = (4 + 7) + 9 \] \[ 4 + 16 = 11 + 9 \] \[ 20 = 20 \]

Both sides equal 20, so the property holds.

Question 2

Verify the associative property with 12, 15, and 18.

Applying the property:

\[ 12 + (15 + 18) = (12 + 15) + 18 \] \[ 12 + 33 = 27 + 18 \] \[ 45 = 45 \]

The property is verified as both sides are equal.

Question 3

Does the associative property hold for subtraction? Test with 10, 5, and 2.

Testing with subtraction:

\[ 10 - (5 - 2) = (10 - 5) - 2 \] \[ 10 - 3 = 5 - 2 \] \[ 7 = 3 \]

This is false, so the associative property does not hold for subtraction.

Associative Property of Multiplication

The Property

For any three numbers a, b, and c, the associative property of multiplication states:

\[ (a \times b) \times c = a \times (b \times c) \]

Example: Let's use the numbers 2, 3, and 4

\[ (2 \times 3) \times 4 = 2 \times (3 \times 4) \] \[ 6 \times 4 = 2 \times 12 \] \[ 24 = 24 \]

Why It Works

The associative property of multiplication works because multiplication is repeated addition, and the grouping of factors doesn't change the total product.

Imagine arranging objects in rectangular arrays - whether you group rows or columns first, the total number of objects remains the same.

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Demonstrate the associative property with the numbers 5, 6, and 2.

Applying the property:

\[ (5 \times 6) \times 2 = 5 \times (6 \times 2) \] \[ 30 \times 2 = 5 \times 12 \] \[ 60 = 60 \]

The property holds as both sides are equal.

Question 2

Verify the property using 10, 3, and 7.

Testing the property:

\[ (10 \times 3) \times 7 = 10 \times (3 \times 7) \] \[ 30 \times 7 = 10 \times 21 \] \[ 210 = 210 \]

The property is verified.

Question 3

Does the associative property apply to division? Test with 24, 6, and 2.

Testing with division:

\[ (24 \div 6) \div 2 = 24 \div (6 \div 2) \] \[ 4 \div 2 = 24 \div 3 \] \[ 2 = 8 \]

This is false, so the associative property does not hold for division.

Practical Applications

Simplifying Calculations

The associative property can be used to simplify complex calculations by regrouping numbers in ways that make them easier to compute.

Example 1: Calculate 25 × 17 × 4

Using the associative property:

\[ 25 \times 17 \times 4 = 25 \times (17 \times 4) \] \[ = 25 \times 68 \] (hard to compute)

Better grouping:

\[ (25 \times 4) \times 17 \] \[ = 100 \times 17 \] \[ = 1700 \]

Example 2: Calculate 13 + 29 + 7

Using the associative property:

\[ 13 + 29 + 7 = (13 + 7) + 29 \] \[ = 20 + 29 \] \[ = 49 \]

Mental Math Strategies

The associative property is fundamental to many mental math techniques. By regrouping numbers strategically, we can perform calculations more efficiently in our heads.

Addition Strategy:

Look for numbers that add up to multiples of 10 first:

\[ 7 + 4 + 3 = (7 + 3) + 4 = 10 + 4 = 14 \]

Multiplication Strategy:

Multiply numbers that give easy intermediate results first:

\[ 5 \times 7 \times 2 = (5 \times 2) \times 7 = 10 \times 7 = 70 \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Use the associative property to simplify: 18 + 25 + 5

Regroup the numbers:

\[ 18 + 25 + 5 = 18 + (25 + 5) \] \[ = 18 + 30 \] \[ = 48 \]

Question 2

Simplify using the associative property: 4 × 23 × 25

Regroup the factors:

\[ 4 \times 23 \times 25 = (4 \times 25) \times 23 \] \[ = 100 \times 23 \] \[ = 2300 \]

Question 3

Calculate mentally: 15 + 37 + 5 + 13

Use the associative property to group convenient pairs:

\[ (15 + 5) + (37 + 13) \] \[ = 20 + 50 \] \[ = 70 \]
