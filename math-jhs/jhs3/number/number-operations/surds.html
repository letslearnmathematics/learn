Introduction

Surds are irrational numbers that cannot be simplified to remove a square root (or cube root etc). They are numbers left in root form (√) to express their exact value.

Surds are commonly used when it's necessary to work with exact values rather than decimal approximations. They appear frequently in geometry, algebra, and trigonometry.

Examples of surds:

\[ \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{5}, \sqrt[3]{7}, \sqrt{10} \]

Not Surds:

These are not surds because they can be simplified to rational numbers:

\[ \sqrt{4} = 2, \sqrt{9} = 3, \sqrt[3]{8} = 2 \]

Irrational Nature:

Surds are irrational numbers - they cannot be expressed as exact fractions and their decimal representations never terminate or repeat.

Simple Surds

Definition

A simple surd is a single surd term that cannot be broken down into simpler surd terms. It consists of a single irrational root expression.

Characteristics

Simple surds have the following characteristics:

Single Term

Contains only one surd term

\[ \sqrt{2}, \sqrt[3]{5}, \sqrt{7} \]

No Addition/Subtraction

Does not involve addition or subtraction with other terms

Cannot be Simplified

Cannot be simplified to remove the root or broken into simpler surds

Examples

Square Roots

Examples of simple surds with square roots:

\[ \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{5}, \sqrt{7}, \sqrt{11} \]

Higher Roots

Examples with cube and higher roots:

\[ \sqrt[3]{2}, \sqrt[4]{3}, \sqrt[5]{7} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Which of the following are simple surds?

a) √4

b) √5

c) 2 + √3

d) √7

Only b) √5 and d) √7 are simple surds.

a) √4 = 2 (rational number)

c) 2 + √3 is a compound surd (has addition)

Question 2

Identify the simple surds from the list:

a) √9

b) √13

c) 1 - √2

d) ∛5

b) √13 and d) ∛5 are simple surds.

a) √9 = 3 (rational number)

c) 1 - √2 is a compound surd (has subtraction)

Question 3

Explain why √8 is not a simple surd.

√8 can be simplified to 2√2, so it's not in its simplest surd form. A simple surd cannot be simplified further.

Compound Surds

Definition

A compound surd is an expression that contains two or more surd terms combined through addition or subtraction. It can also involve a combination of rational numbers and surds.

Characteristics

Compound surds have the following characteristics:

Multiple Terms

Contains two or more terms with surds

\[ \sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}, 5 - \sqrt{7} \]

Addition/Subtraction

Involves addition or subtraction operations between terms

Can be Simplified

Some compound surds can be simplified using algebraic identities

Examples

Two Surd Terms

Examples with two surd terms:

\[ \sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3} \] \[ \sqrt{5} - \sqrt{7} \]

Rational + Surd

Examples with rational numbers and surds:

\[ 3 + \sqrt{2} \] \[ 5 - \sqrt[3]{7} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Which of the following are compound surds?

a) √3 + √5

b) 2√7

c) 1 - √2

d) √11

a) √3 + √5 and c) 1 - √2 are compound surds.

b) 2√7 is a simple surd (single term)

d) √11 is a simple surd (single term)

Question 2

Identify the compound surds from the list:

a) √2 + √3 + √5

b) 3√2

c) 4 - ∛3

d) √17

a) √2 + √3 + √5 and c) 4 - ∛3 are compound surds.

b) 3√2 is a simple surd (single term)

d) √17 is a simple surd (single term)

Question 3

Explain why (√8 + √2) is a compound surd but can be simplified to a simple surd.

√8 + √2 = 2√2 + √2 = 3√2

While it appears as a compound surd initially, it simplifies to a simple surd. This is a special case.

Identifying Surds

Key Differences

The main differences between simple and compound surds:

Feature Simple Surd Compound Surd
Number of Terms Single term Two or more terms
Operations No addition/subtraction Contains addition/subtraction
Examples √2, √3, ∛5 √2 + √3, 5 - √7

Identification Steps

  1. Look for root (√) symbols in the expression
  2. Check if the expression under the root is not a perfect square/cube
  3. Count the number of terms with roots
  4. Check for addition/subtraction between terms
  5. Single term = simple surd, multiple terms = compound surd

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing √4 (rational) with √5 (surd)
  • Thinking 2√3 is compound (it's simple)
  • Forgetting that multiplication creates simple surds (√2 × √3 = √6)
  • Overlooking that some compound surds can simplify to simple surds

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Classify each expression as simple surd, compound surd, or not a surd:

a) √6

b) √9

c) √2 + √5

d) 3√7

a) √6 - simple surd

b) √9 = 3 - not a surd

c) √2 + √5 - compound surd

d) 3√7 - simple surd

Question 2

Which of these are compound surds that can be simplified to simple surds?

a) √12 + √3

b) √8 + √18

c) √2 + √3

d) √50 - √2

a) √12 + √3 = 2√3 + √3 = 3√3 (simplifies)

b) √8 + √18 = 2√2 + 3√2 = 5√2 (simplifies)

c) √2 + √3 (cannot simplify further)

d) √50 - √2 = 5√2 - √2 = 4√2 (simplifies)

Question 3

Create two examples each of:

a) Simple surds

b) Compound surds that remain compound

c) Compound surds that simplify to simple surds

Possible answers:

a) √5, ∛7

b) √2 + √3, 5 - √7

c) √18 + √2 = 3√2 + √2 = 4√2

√27 - √3 = 3√3 - √3 = 2√3
