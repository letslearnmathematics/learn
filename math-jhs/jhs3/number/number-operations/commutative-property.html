Introduction

The commutative property is a fundamental property in mathematics that applies to certain operations. It states that the order in which two numbers are combined does not affect the result.

This property holds true for addition and multiplication, but not for subtraction or division.

Key Idea:

For any two numbers a and b:

  • In addition: \( a + b = b + a \)
  • In multiplication: \( a \times b = b \times a \)

Non-Examples:

These operations are NOT commutative:

  • Subtraction: \( a - b \neq b - a \)
  • Division: \( a \div b \neq b \div a \)

Commutative Property of Addition

The Property

For any two numbers a and b:

\[ a + b = b + a \]

This means the order in which you add two numbers doesn't change the sum.

Examples

Numerical Examples:

\[ \begin{align*} 5 + 3 &= 3 + 5 = 8 \\ 12 + 7 &= 7 + 12 = 19 \\ 1.5 + 2.3 &= 2.3 + 1.5 = 3.8 \\ \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} &= \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{4} \end{align*} \]

Algebraic Example:

If \( x = 4 \) and \( y = 9 \): \[ x + y = 4 + 9 = 13 \] \[ y + x = 9 + 4 = 13 \] Therefore, \( x + y = y + x \)

Why It Matters

The commutative property allows us to rearrange numbers when adding to make calculations easier:

Example: \( 17 + 84 + 3 \) can be rearranged as \( 17 + 3 + 84 = 20 + 84 = 104 \)

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Show that \( 15 + 22 = 22 + 15 \)

\[ \begin{align*} 15 + 22 &= 37 \\ 22 + 15 &= 37 \\ \therefore 15 + 22 &= 22 + 15 \end{align*} \]

Question 2

Use the commutative property to simplify: \( 45 + 13 + 5 \)

\[ \begin{align*} 45 + 13 + 5 &= 45 + 5 + 13 \quad \text{(Rearranged using commutative property)} \\ &= 50 + 13 \\ &= 63 \end{align*} \]

Question 3

Does the commutative property hold for these fractions? Show your work: \( \frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{6} \)

\[ \begin{align*} \frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{6} &= \frac{4}{6} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{5}{6} \\ \frac{1}{6} + \frac{2}{3} &= \frac{1}{6} + \frac{4}{6} = \frac{5}{6} \\ \therefore \frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{6} &= \frac{1}{6} + \frac{2}{3} \end{align*} \]

Commutative Property of Multiplication

The Property

For any two numbers a and b:

\[ a \times b = b \times a \]

This means the order in which you multiply two numbers doesn't change the product.

Examples

Numerical Examples:

\[ \begin{align*} 4 \times 5 &= 5 \times 4 = 20 \\ 10 \times 3 &= 3 \times 10 = 30 \\ 2.5 \times 4 &= 4 \times 2.5 = 10 \\ \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{3} &= \frac{1}{3} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{6} \end{align*} \]

Algebraic Example:

If \( x = 7 \) and \( y = 8 \): \[ x \times y = 7 \times 8 = 56 \] \[ y \times x = 8 \times 7 = 56 \] Therefore, \( x \times y = y \times x \)

Why It Matters

The commutative property allows us to rearrange numbers when multiplying to make calculations easier:

Example: \( 25 \times 7 \times 4 \) can be rearranged as \( 25 \times 4 \times 7 = 100 \times 7 = 700 \)

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Show that \( 12 \times 5 = 5 \times 12 \)

\[ \begin{align*} 12 \times 5 &= 60 \\ 5 \times 12 &= 60 \\ \therefore 12 \times 5 &= 5 \times 12 \end{align*} \]

Question 2

Use the commutative property to simplify: \( 8 \times 15 \times 5 \)

\[ \begin{align*} 8 \times 15 \times 5 &= 8 \times 5 \times 15 \quad \text{(Rearranged using commutative property)} \\ &= 40 \times 15 \\ &= 600 \end{align*} \]

Question 3

Does the commutative property hold for these fractions? Show your work: \( \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{2}{5} \)

\[ \begin{align*} \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{2}{5} &= \frac{6}{20} = \frac{3}{10} \\ \frac{2}{5} \times \frac{3}{4} &= \frac{6}{20} = \frac{3}{10} \\ \therefore \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{2}{5} &= \frac{2}{5} \times \frac{3}{4} \end{align*} \]

Real-World Applications

Practical Uses

The commutative property is used daily in various real-world situations:

  • Counting objects: Whether you count a group of items from left to right or right to left, the total remains the same.
  • Money transactions: Adding up purchases in any order gives the same total amount.
  • Area calculations: The area of a rectangle is the same whether you multiply length × width or width × length.
  • Recipe scaling: When doubling ingredients, the order of multiplication doesn't affect the final quantities.

Examples

Counting Example:

If you have 3 apples and 5 oranges, you have: \[ 3 + 5 = 8 \text{ fruits} \] \[ 5 + 3 = 8 \text{ fruits} \] The order of counting doesn't change the total.

Area Example:

A rectangle with length 6m and width 4m: \[ \text{Area} = 6 \times 4 = 24 \text{ m}^2 \] \[ \text{Area} = 4 \times 6 = 24 \text{ m}^2 \] Both calculations give the same area.

Practice Exercise

Question 1

You bought 4 books at ₵15 each and 3 pens at ₵5 each. Use the commutative property to show two ways to calculate your total spending.

Method 1: \[ (4 \times 15) + (3 \times 5) = 60 + 15 = ₵75 \] Method 2: \[ (3 \times 5) + (4 \times 15) = 15 + 60 = ₵75 \] Both methods give the same total.

Question 2

A rectangular garden is 12m long and 8m wide. Show two ways to calculate its area using the commutative property.

Method 1: \[ 12 \times 8 = 96 \text{ m}^2 \] Method 2: \[ 8 \times 12 = 96 \text{ m}^2 \] Both calculations give the same area.

Question 3

Explain why the commutative property doesn't apply to subtraction using a real-world example.

Example: If you have ₵20 and spend ₵5: \[ 20 - 5 = 15 \text{ (you have ₵15 left)} \] But \( 5 - 20 = -15 \) doesn't make sense in this context.
The order matters in subtraction.
