Introduction

Proportional relationships describe situations where two quantities change at a constant rate relative to each other. In mathematics, we say that two variables are proportional if their ratio remains constant.

A common example of proportional relationships is when the total cost of items is directly related to the number of items purchased at a fixed price. We can represent these relationships using simple equations that show how the quantities are related.

For example, if one pencil costs ¢2, then:

  • 2 pencils cost ¢4
  • 3 pencils cost ¢6
  • 10 pencils cost ¢20

This shows a proportional relationship between number of pencils and total cost.

Direct Proportion

Understanding Direct Proportion

Two quantities are in direct proportion when they increase or decrease at the same rate. This means if one quantity doubles, the other also doubles; if one is halved, the other is halved.

The key characteristic of direct proportion is that the ratio between the two quantities remains constant.

The Constant of Proportionality

In any proportional relationship, the constant ratio between the two variables is called the constant of proportionality.

For the pencil example:

\[ \text{Constant of proportionality} = \frac{\text{Total cost}}{\text{Number of pencils}} = \frac{2}{1} = \frac{4}{2} = \frac{6}{3} = 2 \]

This constant value (2) is actually the price per pencil.

Cost Equation

The Proportional Relationship Equation

When total cost (t) is proportional to the number of items (n) purchased at a constant price (p), we can express this relationship with the equation:

\[ t = p \times n \]

Where:

  • t = total cost
  • p = price per item (constant)
  • n = number of items

How to Use the Equation

  1. Identify the constant price per item (p)
  2. Identify the number of items (n)
  3. Multiply p × n to find total cost (t)

Example: If apples cost ¢5 each, how much do 8 apples cost?

Solution:

\[ \begin{align*} p &= 5 \\ n &= 8 \\ t &= p \times n = 5 \times 8 = 40 \\ \end{align*} \]

Total cost = ¢40

Rearranging the Equation

The equation can be rearranged to find any of the three variables:

\[ \begin{align*} t &= p \times n \\ p &= \frac{t}{n} \\ n &= \frac{t}{p} \\ \end{align*} \]

Example: If 10 notebooks cost ¢150, what's the price per notebook?

Solution:

\[ p = \frac{t}{n} = \frac{150}{10} = 15 \]

Price per notebook = ¢15

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A loaf of bread costs ¢12. Write an equation for the total cost (t) of n loaves of bread.

The equation is:

\[ t = 12 \times n \]

Where:

  • t = total cost
  • 12 = price per loaf (¢12)
  • n = number of loaves

Question 2

If one exercise book costs ¢8, how much will 15 exercise books cost?

Using the equation t = p × n:

\[ \begin{align*} p &= 8 \\ n &= 15 \\ t &= 8 \times 15 = 120 \\ \end{align*} \]

Total cost = ¢120

Question 3

A shop sells pens at a constant price. If 5 pens cost ¢25, how much will 8 pens cost?

First find the price per pen (p):

\[ p = \frac{t}{n} = \frac{25}{5} = 5 \]

Now calculate cost for 8 pens:

\[ t = 5 \times 8 = 40 \]

8 pens will cost ¢40

Question 4

If 3 kg of rice costs ¢36, how many kg of rice can you buy with ¢120?

First find price per kg (p):

\[ p = \frac{36}{3} = 12 \]

Now find how many kg for ¢120:

\[ n = \frac{120}{12} = 10 \]

You can buy 10 kg of rice with ¢120

Question 5

A taxi charges a fixed rate per km. If a 15 km trip costs ¢45, how much would a 25 km trip cost?

First find rate per km (p):

\[ p = \frac{45}{15} = 3 \]

Now calculate cost for 25 km:

\[ t = 3 \times 25 = 75 \]

A 25 km trip would cost ¢75

Word Problems

Solving Real-World Problems

Many real-world situations involve proportional relationships. The key to solving these problems is:

  1. Identify the two quantities that are in proportion
  2. Determine the constant of proportionality
  3. Set up the equation t = p × n
  4. Solve for the unknown quantity

Shopping Example

Problem: A store sells notebooks in packs of 3 for ¢60. How much would 7 notebooks cost?

Solution:

First find price per notebook:

\[ p = \frac{60}{3} = 20 \]

Now calculate cost for 7 notebooks:

\[ t = 20 \times 7 = 140 \]

7 notebooks would cost ¢140

Transportation Example

Problem: A taxi charges ¢5 per km. If Kwame has ¢75, how far can he travel?

Solution:

We know:

\[ \begin{align*} p &= 5 \\ t &= 75 \\ n &= \frac{t}{p} = \frac{75}{5} = 15 \\ \end{align*} \]

Kwame can travel 15 km with ¢75

Word Problem Practice

Question 1

A printing shop charges ¢3 per page. If a document has 45 pages, how much will it cost to print?

Using t = p × n:

\[ t = 3 \times 45 = 135 \]

Printing will cost ¢135

Question 2

If 8 meters of fabric cost ¢96, how much would 15 meters cost?

First find price per meter:

\[ p = \frac{96}{8} = 12 \]

Now calculate for 15 meters:

\[ t = 12 \times 15 = 180 \]

15 meters would cost ¢180

Question 3

A water tank fills at a rate of 30 liters per minute. How much water will be in the tank after 12 minutes?

Using t = p × n (where t = total liters, p = liters per minute, n = minutes):

\[ t = 30 \times 12 = 360 \]

The tank will have 360 liters after 12 minutes
