Introduction

Fraction word problems require applying basic operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division) to solve real-world situations involving fractions and percentages. These problems test your understanding of fractions and your ability to translate word descriptions into mathematical expressions.

In this lesson, we will review several word problems involving fractions and percentages. Each problem will be solved step-by-step to demonstrate the thought process and mathematical operations required.

Test Score Problem

Problem Statement

i. A test is made up of 20 questions, how many questions must you answer correctly to get a score of 80%?

Solution Approach

To find how many questions you need to answer correctly to achieve 80%:

  1. Understand that 80% means 80 out of 100
  2. Calculate 80% of the total questions (20)
  3. Multiply total questions by the percentage (as a decimal)

Step-by-Step Solution:

1. Convert percentage to decimal: \( 80\% = 0.80 \)

2. Multiply by total questions: \( 0.80 \times 20 = 16 \)

3. Answer: You need to answer 16 questions correctly to score 80%.

Percentage Reduction

Problem Statement

ii. By what percentage was a television set reduced if it was marked GH₵2,250 and sold for GH₵2,025?

Solution Approach

To find the percentage reduction:

  1. Calculate the amount of reduction (original price - sale price)
  2. Divide the reduction by the original price
  3. Multiply by 100 to convert to percentage

Step-by-Step Solution:

1. Calculate reduction amount: GH₵2,250 - GH₵2,025 = GH₵225

2. Find fraction reduced: \( \frac{225}{2250} = 0.10 \)

3. Convert to percentage: \( 0.10 \times 100 = 10\% \)

4. Answer: The television was reduced by 10%.

Election Results

Problem Statement

iii. In an election involving two contestants, one candidate claimed 52% of the votes, while the other candidate claimed 2,681 votes. If 5000 people voted, how do you know the election results are invalid?

Solution Approach

To verify if the election results are valid:

  1. Calculate votes for first candidate (52% of total)
  2. Calculate votes for second candidate (given as 2,681)
  3. Add both votes and compare with total voters
  4. Alternatively, calculate what percentage 2,681 represents

Step-by-Step Solution:

Method 1:

1. Calculate first candidate's votes: \( 0.52 \times 5000 = 2600 \) votes

2. Second candidate's votes: 2,681 votes

3. Total votes counted: \( 2600 + 2681 = 5281 \)

4. This exceeds total voters (5000), so results are invalid

Method 2:

1. First candidate has 52%, so second should have \( 100\% - 52\% = 48\% \)

2. Calculate 48% of 5000: \( 0.48 \times 5000 = 2400 \) votes

3. But second candidate claims 2,681 votes (which is \( \frac{2681}{5000} \times 100 = 53.62\% \))

4. The percentages add to \( 52\% + 53.62\% = 105.62\% \), which is impossible

5. Answer: The results are invalid because the total votes exceed 5000 or percentages exceed 100%.

Orange Drink Comparison

Problem Statement

iv. Esi and Fusena prepared orange drink by mixing orange squash and water. Esi's drink was made of \(\frac{3}{8}\) orange squash and Fusena's was made up of \(\frac{2}{8}\) orange squash. Whose drink tasted stronger of orange?

Solution Approach

To determine which drink has a stronger orange taste:

  1. Compare the fractions of orange squash in each drink
  2. The drink with the larger fraction of orange squash will taste stronger
  3. Since denominators are equal, compare numerators directly

Step-by-Step Solution:

1. Esi's drink: \( \frac{3}{8} \) orange squash

2. Fusena's drink: \( \frac{2}{8} \) orange squash

3. Compare fractions: \( \frac{3}{8} > \frac{2}{8} \) because 3 > 2

4. Answer: Esi's drink tasted stronger of orange because it has more orange squash.

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A textbook has 480 pages. If a student has read \(\frac{5}{12}\) of the book, how many pages has she read?

Solution:

1. Calculate fraction of pages read: \( \frac{5}{12} \times 480 \)

2. \( \frac{5 \times 480}{12} = \frac{2400}{12} = 200 \) pages

Answer: She has read 200 pages.

Question 2

In a class of 45 students, \(\frac{2}{5}\) are boys. How many girls are in the class?

Solution:

1. Calculate number of boys: \( \frac{2}{5} \times 45 = 18 \) boys

2. Subtract from total: \( 45 - 18 = 27 \) girls

Answer: There are 27 girls in the class.

Question 3

A shopkeeper sold \(\frac{3}{4}\) of his stock of 600 oranges. How many oranges remain unsold?

Solution:

1. Calculate sold oranges: \( \frac{3}{4} \times 600 = 450 \) oranges

2. Subtract from total: \( 600 - 450 = 150 \) oranges

Answer: 150 oranges remain unsold.

Question 4

A recipe requires \(\frac{1}{2}\) cup of sugar. If you want to make \(\frac{3}{4}\) of the recipe, how much sugar do you need?

Solution:

1. Multiply original amount by fraction: \( \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{3}{4} = \frac{3}{8} \) cup

Answer: You need \(\frac{3}{8}\) cup of sugar.

Question 5

A water tank is \(\frac{5}{8}\) full. After using 150 liters, it's \(\frac{1}{4}\) full. What is the total capacity of the tank?

Solution:

1. Difference in fractions: \( \frac{5}{8} - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{5}{8} - \frac{2}{8} = \frac{3}{8} \)

2. This \(\frac{3}{8}\) represents 150 liters

3. Total capacity: \( 150 \div \frac{3}{8} = 150 \times \frac{8}{3} = 400 \) liters

Answer: The tank's capacity is 400 liters.
