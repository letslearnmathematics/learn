Introduction

Linear equations are powerful tools that help us solve real-world problems. They can model relationships between quantities where one quantity depends on another in a straight-line relationship.

In this lesson, we'll learn how to translate word problems into mathematical equations, solve them, and interpret the solutions in practical contexts.

The general form of a linear equation is:

\[ ax + b = c \]

Where:

  • \( x \) is the variable we're solving for
  • \( a \), \( b \), and \( c \) are constants

Problem-Solving Steps:

  1. Read and understand the problem
  2. Identify what's given and what's unknown
  3. Define variables to represent unknowns
  4. Write an equation that relates the quantities
  5. Solve the equation
  6. Check if the solution makes sense

Word Problems

Key Concepts

Word problems describe real-life situations mathematically. To solve them:

  1. Identify the unknown quantity (let's call it \( x \))
  2. Express other quantities in terms of \( x \)
  3. Set up an equation based on the relationships described
  4. Solve for \( x \)
  5. Interpret the solution in the original context

Example Problem

A taxi charges a flat fee of Gh₵5 plus Gh₵2 per kilometer. If a ride costs Gh₵25, how far did the taxi travel?

Solution:

Let \( x \) = number of kilometers traveled

Total cost = Flat fee + (Rate × Distance)

\[ 5 + 2x = 25 \] \[ 2x = 25 - 5 \] \[ 2x = 20 \] \[ x = 10 \]

The taxi traveled 10 kilometers.

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A phone company charges Gh₵0.50 per minute for calls plus a Gh₵1 connection fee. If Kwame's bill was Gh₵6.50, how many minutes did he talk?

Solution:

Let \( x \) = number of minutes talked

\[ 1 + 0.5x = 6.5 \] \[ 0.5x = 5.5 \] \[ x = 11 \]

Kwame talked for 11 minutes.

Question 2

The sum of three consecutive numbers is 72. Find the numbers.

Solution:

Let \( x \) = first number

Then next numbers are \( x+1 \) and \( x+2 \)

\[ x + (x+1) + (x+2) = 72 \] \[ 3x + 3 = 72 \] \[ 3x = 69 \] \[ x = 23 \]

The numbers are 23, 24, and 25.

Question 3

A rectangle's length is 5cm more than its width. If the perimeter is 50cm, find the dimensions.

Solution:

Let \( x \) = width

Then length = \( x + 5 \)

Perimeter = \( 2 \times (\text{length} + \text{width}) \)

\[ 2(x + x + 5) = 50 \] \[ 2(2x + 5) = 50 \] \[ 4x + 10 = 50 \] \[ 4x = 40 \] \[ x = 10 \]

Width = 10cm, Length = 15cm.

Age Problems

Key Concepts

Age problems involve relationships between people's ages at different times. Key strategies:

  • Define variables for current ages
  • Express future/past ages in terms of these variables
  • Account for the same amount of time passing for all people
  • Set up equations based on given relationships

Example Problem

Kwame is twice as old as Ama. Five years ago, Kwame was three times as old as Ama. Find their current ages.

Solution:

Let \( x \) = Ama's current age

Then Kwame's current age = \( 2x \)

Five years ago:

Ama's age = \( x - 5 \)

Kwame's age = \( 2x - 5 \)

\[ 2x - 5 = 3(x - 5) \] \[ 2x - 5 = 3x - 15 \] \[ -5 + 15 = 3x - 2x \] \[ 10 = x \]

Ama is 10 years old, Kwame is 20 years old.

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Yaw is 4 years older than Afia. In 5 years, the sum of their ages will be 30. Find their current ages.

Solution:

Let \( x \) = Afia's current age

Then Yaw's current age = \( x + 4 \)

In 5 years:

Afia's age = \( x + 5 \)

Yaw's age = \( x + 4 + 5 = x + 9 \)

\[ (x + 5) + (x + 9) = 30 \] \[ 2x + 14 = 30 \] \[ 2x = 16 \] \[ x = 8 \]

Afia is 8 years old, Yaw is 12 years old.

Question 2

A father is three times as old as his son. In 12 years, he will be twice as old as his son. Find their current ages.

Solution:

Let \( x \) = son's current age

Then father's current age = \( 3x \)

In 12 years:

Son's age = \( x + 12 \)

Father's age = \( 3x + 12 \)

\[ 3x + 12 = 2(x + 12) \] \[ 3x + 12 = 2x + 24 \] \[ x = 12 \]

Son is 12 years old, father is 36 years old.

Money Problems

Key Concepts

Money problems involve transactions, costs, profits, and financial relationships. Common scenarios include:

  • Calculating costs with fixed and variable components
  • Determining quantities based on total cost
  • Finding break-even points
  • Comparing different pricing options

Example Problem

A shop sells pens at Gh₵2 each and pencils at Gh₵1 each. Kofi bought a total of 10 items (some pens and some pencils) for Gh₵16. How many pens did he buy?

Solution:

Let \( x \) = number of pens

Then number of pencils = \( 10 - x \)

Total cost:

\[ 2x + 1(10 - x) = 16 \] \[ 2x + 10 - x = 16 \] \[ x + 10 = 16 \] \[ x = 6 \]

Kofi bought 6 pens and 4 pencils.

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A concert ticket costs Gh₵50 for adults and Gh₵30 for children. A family bought 8 tickets for Gh₵340. How many adult tickets did they buy?

Solution:

Let \( x \) = number of adult tickets

Then number of child tickets = \( 8 - x \)

\[ 50x + 30(8 - x) = 340 \] \[ 50x + 240 - 30x = 340 \] \[ 20x = 100 \] \[ x = 5 \]

They bought 5 adult tickets and 3 child tickets.

Question 2

A trader bought some oranges at Gh₵2 each and sold them at Gh₵3 each. If he made a total profit of Gh₵40, how many oranges did he buy?

Solution:

Let \( x \) = number of oranges

Profit per orange = Gh₵3 - Gh₵2 = Gh₵1

\[ 1 \times x = 40 \] \[ x = 40 \]

The trader bought 40 oranges.

Inequality Problems

Key Concepts

Inequalities are used when problems involve minimums, maximums, or ranges of values. Common inequality symbols:

  • \( < \) less than
  • \( > \) greater than
  • \( \leq \) less than or equal to
  • \( \geq \) greater than or equal to

Solving inequalities follows the same rules as equations, except when multiplying or dividing by a negative number (which reverses the inequality sign).

Example Problem

A school requires students to score at least 50% in math and English to pass. If a student scored 65% in math, what range of English scores will allow them to pass, if the average of both subjects must be at least 50%?

Solution:

Let \( x \) = English score

Average score:

\[ \frac{65 + x}{2} \geq 50 \] \[ 65 + x \geq 100 \] \[ x \geq 35 \]

The student needs at least 35% in English.

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A phone plan costs Gh₵20 per month plus Gh₵0.50 per minute. If Kofi wants to spend no more than Gh₵50 this month, what's the maximum minutes he can use?

Solution:

Let \( x \) = number of minutes

\[ 20 + 0.5x \leq 50 \] \[ 0.5x \leq 30 \] \[ x \leq 60 \]

Kofi can use a maximum of 60 minutes.

Question 2

To join a gym, there's a Gh₵100 registration fee plus Gh₵30 monthly fee. Ama has Gh₵400 saved. For how many months can she afford membership?

Solution:

Let \( x \) = number of months

\[ 100 + 30x \leq 400 \] \[ 30x \leq 300 \] \[ x \leq 10 \]

Ama can afford up to 10 months of membership.
