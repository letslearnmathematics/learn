Introduction

A linear inequality is similar to a linear equation but uses inequality signs (<, >, ≤, ≥) instead of an equals sign. It describes a range of possible solutions rather than a single solution.

Examples of linear inequalities:

\[ 3x + 2 > 8 \] \[ \frac{1}{2}y - 4 \leq 7 \] \[ -2x + 5 \geq 3x - 1 \]

Inequality Symbols:

  • < : less than
  • > : greater than
  • ≤ : less than or equal to
  • ≥ : greater than or equal to

Solution Set:

All real numbers that make the inequality true when substituted for the variable.

Inequality Properties

Key Properties

When solving inequalities, these properties help maintain the inequality's truth:

Addition Property

You can add the same number to both sides without changing the inequality.

If \( a > b \), then \( a + c > b + c \)

Example: \( x - 3 < 5 \) becomes \( x < 8 \) after adding 3 to both sides

Subtraction Property

You can subtract the same number from both sides without changing the inequality.

If \( a \geq b \), then \( a - c \geq b - c \)

Example: \( x + 4 \geq 9 \) becomes \( x \geq 5 \) after subtracting 4 from both sides

Multiplication Property

When multiplying both sides by a positive number, the inequality remains the same.

If \( a < b \) and \( c > 0 \), then \( ac < bc \)

Example: \( \frac{x}{2} > 3 \) becomes \( x > 6 \) after multiplying both sides by 2

When multiplying by a negative number, you must reverse the inequality sign.

Division Property

When dividing both sides by a positive number, the inequality remains the same.

If \( a \leq b \) and \( c > 0 \), then \( \frac{a}{c} \leq \frac{b}{c} \)

Example: \( 4x \leq 12 \) becomes \( x \leq 3 \) after dividing both sides by 4

When dividing by a negative number, you must reverse the inequality sign.

Solving Inequalities

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Simplify both sides: Clear parentheses and combine like terms
  2. Isolate the variable term: Use addition/subtraction to get variable terms on one side and constants on the other
  3. Solve for the variable: Use multiplication/division to get the variable by itself
  4. Check your solution: Verify by substituting back into the original inequality

Example 1: Solve \( 2x + 5 < 13 \)

Step 1: Subtract 5 from both sides

\[ 2x < 8 \]

Step 2: Divide both sides by 2

\[ x < 4 \]

Solution: All real numbers less than 4

Example 2: Solve \( -3x + 7 \geq 16 \)

Step 1: Subtract 7 from both sides

\[ -3x \geq 9 \]

Step 2: Divide both sides by -3 (remember to reverse the inequality!)

\[ x \leq -3 \]

Solution: All real numbers less than or equal to -3

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Solve the inequality: \( 5x - 3 \leq 12 \)

Step 1: Add 3 to both sides

\[ 5x \leq 15 \]

Step 2: Divide both sides by 5

\[ x \leq 3 \]

Solution: All real numbers less than or equal to 3

Question 2

Solve the inequality: \( \frac{2}{3}x + 4 > 10 \)

Step 1: Subtract 4 from both sides

\[ \frac{2}{3}x > 6 \]

Step 2: Multiply both sides by 3/2

\[ x > 9 \]

Solution: All real numbers greater than 9

Question 3

Solve the inequality: \( -4x + 7 < 23 \)

Step 1: Subtract 7 from both sides

\[ -4x < 16 \]

Step 2: Divide both sides by -4 (remember to reverse the inequality!)

\[ x > -4 \]

Solution: All real numbers greater than -4

Question 4

Solve the inequality: \( 3(x - 2) \geq 5x + 4 \)

Step 1: Expand the left side

\[ 3x - 6 \geq 5x + 4 \]

Step 2: Subtract 3x from both sides

\[ -6 \geq 2x + 4 \]

Step 3: Subtract 4 from both sides

\[ -10 \geq 2x \]

Step 4: Divide both sides by 2

\[ -5 \geq x \]

Solution: All real numbers less than or equal to -5 (can also be written as \( x \leq -5 \))

Question 5

Solve the inequality: \( \frac{x+3}{2} - \frac{x-1}{4} \leq 1 \)

Step 1: Find common denominator (4) and combine terms

\[ \frac{2(x+3)}{4} - \frac{x-1}{4} \leq 1 \] \[ \frac{2x+6 - x + 1}{4} \leq 1 \] \[ \frac{x + 7}{4} \leq 1 \]

Step 2: Multiply both sides by 4

\[ x + 7 \leq 4 \]

Step 3: Subtract 7 from both sides

\[ x \leq -3 \]

Solution: All real numbers less than or equal to -3

Word Problems

Many real-world situations can be modeled with inequalities. The key steps are:

  1. Identify what the variable represents
  2. Translate the words into an inequality
  3. Solve the inequality
  4. Interpret the solution in the original context

Example 1: Budget Constraint

Kwame wants to buy some notebooks that cost ¢12 each and pens that cost ¢5 each. He has at most ¢50 to spend. If he buys 3 notebooks, how many pens can he buy?

Solution:

Let \( p \) = number of pens

Cost of notebooks: \( 3 \times 12 = 36 \) cedis

Cost of pens: \( 5p \) cedis

Total cost must be ≤ 50 cedis:

\[ 36 + 5p \leq 50 \]

Subtract 36 from both sides:

\[ 5p \leq 14 \]

Divide both sides by 5:

\[ p \leq 2.8 \]

Since Kwame can't buy a fraction of a pen, he can buy at most 2 pens.

Example 2: Grade Requirement

Ama needs an average of at least 80% on her five tests to get a B. Her first four test scores are 78, 85, 75, and 82. What score does she need on her fifth test?

Solution:

Let \( x \) = fifth test score

Average must be ≥ 80:

\[ \frac{78 + 85 + 75 + 82 + x}{5} \geq 80 \]

Combine like terms:

\[ \frac{320 + x}{5} \geq 80 \]

Multiply both sides by 5:

\[ 320 + x \geq 400 \]

Subtract 320 from both sides:

\[ x \geq 80 \]

Ama needs at least 80% on her fifth test.

Graphing Solutions

The solutions to inequalities can be represented on a number line:

Open Circle (○)

Used for < or > inequalities (not including the endpoint)

Example: \( x > 2 \)

Closed Circle (●)

Used for ≤ or ≥ inequalities (including the endpoint)

Example: \( x \leq -1 \)

Compound Inequalities

When a variable is between two values:

Example: \( -3 \leq x < 2 \)

Question 1

Solve and graph: \( 2x - 5 < 3 \)

Step 1: Add 5 to both sides

\[ 2x < 8 \]

Step 2: Divide both sides by 2

\[ x < 4 \]

Graph: Open circle at 4 with arrow to the left

Question 2

A phone plan charges ¢50 per month plus ¢0.25 per text message. If you want to spend no more than ¢100 per month, how many texts can you send?

Let \( t \) = number of text messages

Monthly cost must be ≤ 100:

\[ 50 + 0.25t \leq 100 \]

Subtract 50 from both sides:

\[ 0.25t \leq 50 \]

Divide both sides by 0.25:

\[ t \leq 200 \]

You can send at most 200 text messages per month.

Question 3

Solve and graph: \( -2 \leq \frac{x+1}{3} < 4 \)

Break into two inequalities:

Part 1: \( -2 \leq \frac{x+1}{3} \)

Multiply both sides by 3:

\[ -6 \leq x + 1 \]

Subtract 1 from both sides:

\[ -7 \leq x \]

Part 2: \( \frac{x+1}{3} < 4 \)

Multiply both sides by 3:

\[ x + 1 < 12 \]

Subtract 1 from both sides:

\[ x < 11 \]

Combined solution: \( -7 \leq x < 11 \)

Graph: Closed circle at -7, open circle at 11, shaded between
