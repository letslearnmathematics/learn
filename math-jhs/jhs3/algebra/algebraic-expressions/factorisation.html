Introduction

Factorising is the process of breaking down an expression into simpler parts (factors) that when multiplied together give the original expression. When dealing with binomials (expressions with two terms), there are several methods we can use to factorise them.

In this lesson, we'll learn three main methods for factorising simple binomial expressions:

  1. Common Factor Method
  2. Difference of Squares
  3. Grouping Method

Common Factor Method

Main Idea

The common factor method involves identifying the greatest common factor (GCF) of all terms in the binomial and factoring it out.

The general form is:
\[ ax + ay = a(x + y) \] where \( a \) is the common factor.

Steps to Factorise

  1. Identify the GCF of all terms
  2. Divide each term by the GCF
  3. Write the expression as the GCF multiplied by the remaining terms

Example 1: Factorise \( 6x + 9 \)

\[ \begin{align*} \text{GCF of 6 and 9 is 3} \\ 6x + 9 = 3(2x) + 3(3) \\ = 3(2x + 3) \end{align*} \]

Example 2: Factorise \( 4x^2 - 8x \)

\[ \begin{align*} \text{GCF of 4 and 8 is 4} \\ \text{GCF of } x^2 \text{ and } x \text{ is } x \\ 4x^2 - 8x = 4x(x) - 4x(2) \\ = 4x(x - 2) \end{align*} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Factorise \( 12y + 18 \)

\[ \begin{align*} \text{GCF of 12 and 18 is 6} \\ 12y + 18 = 6(2y) + 6(3) \\ = 6(2y + 3) \end{align*} \]

Question 2

Factorise \( 15a^2 - 25a \)

\[ \begin{align*} \text{GCF of 15 and 25 is 5} \\ \text{GCF of } a^2 \text{ and } a \text{ is } a \\ 15a^2 - 25a = 5a(3a) - 5a(5) \\ = 5a(3a - 5) \end{align*} \]

Question 3

Factorise \( 24x^3 + 16x^2 \)

\[ \begin{align*} \text{GCF of 24 and 16 is 8} \\ \text{GCF of } x^3 \text{ and } x^2 \text{ is } x^2 \\ 24x^3 + 16x^2 = 8x^2(3x) + 8x^2(2) \\ = 8x^2(3x + 2) \end{align*} \]

Difference of Squares

Main Idea

The difference of squares is a special factoring pattern that applies to binomials where:

  1. Both terms are perfect squares
  2. The terms are subtracted

The general form is:
\[ a^2 - b^2 = (a + b)(a - b) \]

Steps to Factorise

  1. Identify if both terms are perfect squares
  2. Confirm the operation is subtraction
  3. Take the square root of each term
  4. Write as (square root + square root)(square root - square root)

Example 1: Factorise \( x^2 - 16 \)

\[ \begin{align*} x^2 \text{ is } (x)^2 \\ 16 \text{ is } (4)^2 \\ x^2 - 16 = (x + 4)(x - 4) \end{align*} \]

Example 2: Factorise \( 9y^2 - 25 \)

\[ \begin{align*} 9y^2 \text{ is } (3y)^2 \\ 25 \text{ is } (5)^2 \\ 9y^2 - 25 = (3y + 5)(3y - 5) \end{align*} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Factorise \( 4a^2 - 9 \)

\[ \begin{align*} 4a^2 \text{ is } (2a)^2 \\ 9 \text{ is } (3)^2 \\ 4a^2 - 9 = (2a + 3)(2a - 3) \end{align*} \]

Question 2

Factorise \( 16x^2 - 49y^2 \)

\[ \begin{align*} 16x^2 \text{ is } (4x)^2 \\ 49y^2 \text{ is } (7y)^2 \\ 16x^2 - 49y^2 = (4x + 7y)(4x - 7y) \end{align*} \]

Question 3

Factorise \( 100 - 81b^2 \)

\[ \begin{align*} 100 \text{ is } (10)^2 \\ 81b^2 \text{ is } (9b)^2 \\ 100 - 81b^2 = (10 + 9b)(10 - 9b) \end{align*} \]

Grouping Method

Main Idea

The grouping method is useful when a binomial can be split into parts that have common factors. This method involves:

  1. Grouping terms with common factors
  2. Factoring out the GCF from each group
  3. Looking for a common binomial factor

Steps to Factorise

  1. Group terms that have common factors
  2. Factor out the GCF from each group
  3. If a common binomial remains, factor it out

Example 1: Factorise \( ax + ay + bx + by \)

\[ \begin{align*} (ax + ay) + (bx + by) \\ = a(x + y) + b(x + y) \\ = (x + y)(a + b) \end{align*} \]

Example 2: Factorise \( 3x + 3y + x^2 + xy \)

\[ \begin{align*} (3x + 3y) + (x^2 + xy) \\ = 3(x + y) + x(x + y) \\ = (x + y)(3 + x) \end{align*} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Factorise \( 2m + 2n + am + an \)

\[ \begin{align*} (2m + 2n) + (am + an) \\ = 2(m + n) + a(m + n) \\ = (m + n)(2 + a) \end{align*} \]

Question 2

Factorise \( 5x + 5y + x^2 + xy \)

\[ \begin{align*} (5x + 5y) + (x^2 + xy) \\ = 5(x + y) + x(x + y) \\ = (x + y)(5 + x) \end{align*} \]

Question 3

Factorise \( ab + ac + b^2 + bc \)

\[ \begin{align*} (ab + ac) + (b^2 + bc) \\ = a(b + c) + b(b + c) \\ = (b + c)(a + b) \end{align*} \]
