Introduction

Changing the subject of a formula means rearranging the formula so that a different variable is on its own on one side of the equation. The subject of a formula is the variable that is being worked out.

For example, in the formula for the area of a rectangle:

\[ A = l \times w \]

Where:

  • \( A \) is the area (subject)
  • \( l \) is the length
  • \( w \) is the width

If we want to make \( l \) the subject, we would rearrange the formula to:

\[ l = \frac{A}{w} \]

This process is very useful in mathematics and science when we need to solve for different variables in equations.

Basic Rules

Key Principles

When changing the subject of a formula, we follow these basic rules:

  1. Whatever you do to one side of the equation, you must do to the other side
  2. Use inverse operations to isolate the variable you want to make the subject
  3. The order of operations matters (reverse BODMAS/PEMDAS)

Addition/Subtraction

To remove a term from one side, add or subtract it from both sides.

Example: Make \( x \) the subject in \( y = x + 5 \)

Subtract 5 from both sides:

\[ y - 5 = x \]

Or:

\[ x = y - 5 \]

Multiplication/Division

To remove a coefficient, divide both sides by it (or multiply by its reciprocal).

Example: Make \( x \) the subject in \( y = 3x \)

Divide both sides by 3:

\[ \frac{y}{3} = x \]

Or:

\[ x = \frac{y}{3} \]

Squares/Roots

To remove a square, take the square root of both sides (and vice versa).

Example: Make \( x \) the subject in \( y = x^2 \)

Take square roots of both sides:

\[ \sqrt{y} = x \]

Or:

\[ x = \sqrt{y} \]

Worked Examples

Example 1: Simple Linear Equation

Make \( x \) the subject in the formula:

\[ y = 2x + 3 \]

Solution:

  1. Subtract 3 from both sides: \[ y - 3 = 2x \]
  2. Divide both sides by 2: \[ \frac{y - 3}{2} = x \]
  3. Final answer: \[ x = \frac{y - 3}{2} \]

Example 2: Formula with Fractions

Make \( r \) the subject in the formula:

\[ V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \]

Solution:

  1. Multiply both sides by 3: \[ 3V = 4\pi r^3 \]
  2. Divide both sides by \( 4\pi \): \[ \frac{3V}{4\pi} = r^3 \]
  3. Take the cube root of both sides: \[ r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{3V}{4\pi}} \]

Example 3: Quadratic Formula

Make \( b \) the subject in the formula:

\[ a = \sqrt{b^2 + c^2} \]

Solution:

  1. Square both sides: \[ a^2 = b^2 + c^2 \]
  2. Subtract \( c^2 \) from both sides: \[ a^2 - c^2 = b^2 \]
  3. Take square roots of both sides: \[ b = \sqrt{a^2 - c^2} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Make \( x \) the subject in the formula: \( y = 5x - 2 \)

  1. Add 2 to both sides: \[ y + 2 = 5x \]
  2. Divide both sides by 5: \[ x = \frac{y + 2}{5} \]

Question 2

Make \( h \) the subject in the formula: \( A = \frac{1}{2}bh \)

  1. Multiply both sides by 2: \[ 2A = bh \]
  2. Divide both sides by \( b \): \[ h = \frac{2A}{b} \]

Question 3

Make \( r \) the subject in the formula: \( C = 2\pi r \)

  1. Divide both sides by \( 2\pi \): \[ r = \frac{C}{2\pi} \]

Question 4

Make \( u \) the subject in the formula: \( v^2 = u^2 + 2as \)

  1. Subtract \( 2as \) from both sides: \[ v^2 - 2as = u^2 \]
  2. Take square roots of both sides: \[ u = \sqrt{v^2 - 2as} \]

Question 5

Make \( t \) the subject in the formula: \( s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \)

This is a quadratic in terms of \( t \):

  1. Rearrange: \[ \frac{1}{2}at^2 + ut - s = 0 \]
  2. Multiply through by 2: \[ at^2 + 2ut - 2s = 0 \]
  3. Use quadratic formula: \[ t = \frac{-2u \pm \sqrt{(2u)^2 - 4 \times a \times (-2s)}}{2a} \]
  4. Simplify: \[ t = \frac{-2u \pm \sqrt{4u^2 + 8as}}{2a} \] \[ t = \frac{-u \pm \sqrt{u^2 + 2as}}{a} \]

Real-life Applications

Science Applications

Changing the subject of a formula is commonly used in science:

  • Physics: Rearranging kinematic equations to solve for different variables
  • Chemistry: Rearranging the ideal gas law \( PV = nRT \) to solve for different variables
  • Biology: Calculating population growth rates by rearranging formulas

Example: From the ideal gas law \( PV = nRT \), make \( T \) the subject:

\[ T = \frac{PV}{nR} \]

Everyday Applications

Practical uses in daily life:

  • Cooking: Adjusting recipe quantities by rearranging proportion formulas
  • Finance: Rearranging interest formulas to solve for different variables
  • Construction: Calculating dimensions by rearranging area and volume formulas

Example: From the simple interest formula \( I = PRT \), make \( P \) the subject:

\[ P = \frac{I}{RT} \]

This lets you calculate the principal needed to earn a certain interest amount.
