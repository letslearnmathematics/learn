Introduction

Algebraic expressions are mathematical phrases that can contain numbers, variables, and operation symbols. We can use these expressions to model real-world situations and solve problems by simplifying and factorising them.

In this lesson, we'll learn how to:

  • Simplify algebraic expressions by combining like terms
  • Factorise expressions by finding common factors
  • Apply these skills to solve practical problems

For example, if we have the expression:

\[ 3x + 2y - x + 4y \]

We can simplify it by combining like terms:

\[ (3x - x) + (2y + 4y) = 2x + 6y \]

Simplifying Expressions

Key Concepts

Simplifying expressions makes them easier to work with by:

  1. Combining like terms (terms with the same variables)
  2. Removing parentheses using the distributive property
  3. Reducing fractions when possible

Combining Like Terms

Like terms have the same variables raised to the same powers.

Simplify: \( 5x + 3y - 2x + 4 \)

Solution: Combine the x terms and constant terms separately:
\( (5x - 2x) + 3y + 4 = 3x + 3y + 4 \)

Distributive Property

Multiply terms inside parentheses by the factor outside.

Simplify: \( 3(x + 4) - 2(2x - 1) \)

Solution:
\( = 3x + 12 - 4x + 2 \)
\( = (3x - 4x) + (12 + 2) \)
\( = -x + 14 \)

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Simplify: \( 4a + 3b - 2a + 5 \)

Combine like terms:

\( (4a - 2a) + 3b + 5 = 2a + 3b + 5 \)

Question 2

Simplify: \( 5(x + 3) - 2(x - 1) \)

Use distributive property:

\( = 5x + 15 - 2x + 2 \)

Combine like terms:

\( = (5x - 2x) + (15 + 2) = 3x + 17 \)

Question 3

Simplify: \( 2y^2 + 3y - y^2 + 5 \)

Combine like terms:

\( (2y^2 - y^2) + 3y + 5 = y^2 + 3y + 5 \)

Factorising Expressions

Key Concepts

Factorising is the reverse of expanding. It means writing an expression as a product of its factors.

Common factorisation methods include:

  1. Taking out common factors
  2. Factorising quadratics
  3. Difference of two squares

Common Factors

Find the greatest common factor of all terms.

Factorise: \( 6x + 9 \)

Solution: The GCF is 3
\( = 3(2x + 3) \)

Factorising Quadratics

Find two numbers that multiply to give the constant term and add to give the coefficient of x.

Factorise: \( x^2 + 5x + 6 \)

Solution: Find two numbers that multiply to 6 and add to 5
\( = (x + 2)(x + 3) \)

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Factorise: \( 4x + 8 \)

Find the GCF (4):

\( = 4(x + 2) \)

Question 2

Factorise: \( x^2 + 7x + 12 \)

Find two numbers that multiply to 12 and add to 7:

\( = (x + 3)(x + 4) \)

Question 3

Factorise: \( 3x^2 - 12x \)

Find the GCF (3x):

\( = 3x(x - 4) \)

Real World Applications

Applying Algebra to Real Problems

Algebraic expressions can model many real-world situations. By simplifying and factorising, we can solve practical problems more efficiently.

Area Problems

Algebra can help calculate areas and perimeters.

A rectangular garden has length \( (x + 5) \) meters and width \( x \) meters. Express the area and simplify.

Solution: Area = length × width
\( = x(x + 5) = x^2 + 5x \) square meters

Cost Problems

Algebra can model costs and profits.

If apples cost \( c \) cedis each and you buy \( n \) apples plus 3 oranges at 50 pesewas each, write an expression for the total cost.

Solution:
Cost of apples: \( n \times c = nc \)
Cost of oranges: \( 3 \times 0.50 = 1.50 \)
Total cost: \( nc + 1.50 \) cedis

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A rectangle has length \( (2x + 3) \) cm and width \( x \) cm. Find and simplify expressions for: a) The perimeter b) The area

a) Perimeter = 2(length + width)

\( = 2[(2x + 3) + x] = 2(3x + 3) = 6x + 6 \) cm

b) Area = length × width

\( = (2x + 3)(x) = 2x^2 + 3x \) cm²

Question 2

If pencils cost \( p \) cedis each and pens cost \( 2p \) cedis each, write an expression for the cost of: a) 5 pencils and 3 pens b) Simplify your expression

a) Cost = \( 5 \times p + 3 \times 2p = 5p + 6p \)

b) Simplified: \( 11p \) cedis

Question 3

The area of a square is \( x^2 + 6x + 9 \) cm². Find an expression for its side length.

Factorise the area expression:

\( x^2 + 6x + 9 = (x + 3)^2 \)

Therefore, side length = \( x + 3 \) cm
