Introduction

Vectors are quantities that have both magnitude (size) and direction. In this lesson, we'll learn about two special relationships between vectors: parallel vectors and perpendicular vectors.

Understanding these relationships is important for solving problems in physics, engineering, and advanced mathematics. We'll explore the conditions that make vectors parallel or perpendicular to each other, and how to use these conditions to solve problems.

A vector can be represented as:

\[ \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} \]

or as a directed line segment between two points.

Parallel Vectors

Conditions for Parallel Vectors

Two vectors are parallel if one is a scalar multiple of the other. This means:

For vectors \(\vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \end{pmatrix}\), they are parallel if:

\[ \vec{a} = k\vec{b} \]

for some scalar \( k \neq 0 \).

How to Check for Parallel Vectors

To determine if two vectors are parallel:

  1. Check if one vector is a scalar multiple of the other
  2. Verify that the ratios of corresponding components are equal: \[ \frac{a_1}{b_1} = \frac{a_2}{b_2} \]

Example: Are \(\vec{u} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix}\) parallel?

Solution: Yes, because \(\vec{v} = 2\vec{u}\) (each component is multiplied by 2).

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Determine if the vectors \(\vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} -6 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}\) are parallel.

Check if one is a scalar multiple of the other:

\(\vec{b} = -2 \times \vec{a}\) because:

\(-2 \times 3 = -6\) and \(-2 \times -2 = 4\)

Therefore, the vectors are parallel.

Question 2

Find the value of \(k\) that makes \(\vec{u} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ k \end{pmatrix}\) parallel to \(\vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 12 \\ 9 \end{pmatrix}\).

For parallel vectors, the ratios must be equal:

\[ \frac{4}{12} = \frac{k}{9} \] \[ \frac{1}{3} = \frac{k}{9} \] \[ k = 3 \]

Question 3

Given that \(\vec{p} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\vec{q} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\) are parallel, find \(x\).

Set up the proportion:

\[ \frac{5}{x} = \frac{-1}{2} \]

Cross-multiply:

\[ -x = 10 \] \[ x = -10 \]

Perpendicular Vectors

Conditions for Perpendicular Vectors

Two vectors are perpendicular (orthogonal) if their dot product is zero. For vectors \(\vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \end{pmatrix}\):

\[ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 = 0 \]

This condition comes from the fact that perpendicular vectors meet at 90°.

How to Check for Perpendicular Vectors

To determine if two vectors are perpendicular:

  1. Calculate the dot product of the two vectors
  2. Check if the result equals zero

Example: Are \(\vec{u} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\) perpendicular?

Solution: Calculate the dot product:

\[ \vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = (2)(-1) + (1)(2) = -2 + 2 = 0 \]

Since the dot product is zero, the vectors are perpendicular.

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Determine if \(\vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} -8 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix}\) are perpendicular.

Calculate the dot product:

\[ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = (3)(-8) + (4)(6) = -24 + 24 = 0 \]

Since the dot product is zero, the vectors are perpendicular.

Question 2

Find the value of \(k\) that makes \(\vec{u} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ k \end{pmatrix}\) perpendicular to \(\vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}\).

Set the dot product to zero:

\[ \vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = (5)(3) + (k)(-2) = 0 \] \[ 15 - 2k = 0 \] \[ 2k = 15 \] \[ k = 7.5 \]

Question 3

Given \(\vec{p} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}\), find a vector \(\vec{q}\) that is perpendicular to \(\vec{p}\).

Let \(\vec{q} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}\). The dot product must be zero:

\[ 2x - y = 0 \]

Choose \(x = 1\), then \(y = 2\). One possible solution is:

\[ \vec{q} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} \]

(Note: There are infinitely many possible solutions)

Solving Problems

Combined Problems

Many problems require you to determine if vectors are parallel, perpendicular, or neither. Here's how to approach such problems:

  1. First check if the vectors are parallel by seeing if one is a scalar multiple of the other
  2. If not parallel, calculate the dot product to check for perpendicularity
  3. If neither condition is met, the vectors are neither parallel nor perpendicular

Problem-Solving Strategy

Example: Determine the relationship between \(\vec{u} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} -6 \\ -9 \end{pmatrix}\).

Solution:

1. Check for parallel: \(\vec{v} = -3\vec{u}\) (each component multiplied by -3)

2. Therefore, the vectors are parallel (and not perpendicular)

Example: Determine the relationship between \(\vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}\).

Solution:

1. Check for parallel: \(\frac{4}{-1} \neq \frac{1}{4}\), so not parallel

2. Check dot product: \((4)(-1) + (1)(4) = -4 + 4 = 0\)

3. Therefore, the vectors are perpendicular

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Determine if \(\vec{m} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\vec{n} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -10 \end{pmatrix}\) are parallel, perpendicular, or neither.

1. Check for parallel: \(\vec{n} = -2\vec{m}\) (each component multiplied by -2)

2. Therefore, the vectors are parallel

Question 2

Determine if \(\vec{p} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\vec{q} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ -5 \end{pmatrix}\) are parallel, perpendicular, or neither.

1. Check for parallel: \(\frac{3}{0}\) is undefined, so not parallel

2. Check dot product: \((3)(0) + (0)(-5) = 0 + 0 = 0\)

3. Therefore, the vectors are perpendicular

Question 3

Find a vector that is both parallel to \(\vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\) and perpendicular to \(\vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}\).

1. Any parallel vector to \(\vec{a}\) is \(k\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\)

2. For perpendicularity with \(\vec{b}\), the dot product must be zero:

\[ (k)(2) + (2k)(-1) = 0 \] \[ 2k - 2k = 0 \]

3. This holds for all \(k\), so all scalar multiples of \(\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\) satisfy both conditions
