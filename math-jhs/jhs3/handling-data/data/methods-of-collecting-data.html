Introduction

Data collection is the process of gathering and measuring information on variables of interest in a systematic way. The method you choose to collect data depends on the type of information you need to answer your research question.

There are two main types of data collection methods:

Quantitative Methods

Collect numerical data that can be measured and analyzed statistically

Qualitative Methods

Collect non-numerical data that describes qualities or characteristics

Example: If you want to know "How many students in JHS3 enjoy mathematics?", you would use a quantitative method. If you want to know "Why do students enjoy or not enjoy mathematics?", you would use a qualitative method.

Quantitative Data Collection Methods

Common Quantitative Methods

Quantitative methods produce numerical data that can be analyzed statistically:

Surveys

Structured questionnaires with closed-ended questions that produce numerical data.

Example: "On a scale of 1-5, how much do you enjoy mathematics?"

Experiments

Controlled tests where variables are manipulated to measure effects.

Example: Measuring test scores of students who used different study methods.

Systematic Observation

Counting and recording specific behaviors or events numerically.

Example: Counting how many students arrive late to class each day.

When to Use Quantitative Methods

  • When you need to measure variables precisely
  • When you want to generalize findings to a larger population
  • When you need to test hypotheses or establish relationships between variables
  • When you need data that can be analyzed statistically

Qualitative Data Collection Methods

Common Qualitative Methods

Qualitative methods produce descriptive data about people's experiences, opinions, and behaviors:

Interviews

One-on-one conversations with open-ended questions to gather in-depth information.

Example: "Can you describe your experience learning mathematics?"

Focus Groups

Group discussions that explore participants' views and experiences.

Example: Discussion with students about challenges in mathematics.

Participant Observation

Observing and recording behaviors in natural settings with detailed notes.

Example: Observing how students interact during group math activities.

When to Use Qualitative Methods

  • When you want to understand experiences, meanings, and perspectives
  • When exploring complex issues that can't be reduced to numbers
  • When developing theories or generating new ideas
  • When studying processes or how things change over time

Selecting Appropriate Methods

Key Considerations

When choosing a data collection method, consider these factors:

Research Question

What exactly do you need to find out? Quantitative for "how many/much", qualitative for "why/how".

Resources Available

Time, budget, equipment, and personnel constraints.

Population Characteristics

Age, literacy level, accessibility of your target group.

Data Analysis Needs

Statistical analysis vs. thematic analysis.

Mixed Methods Approach

Sometimes combining both quantitative and qualitative methods provides the most complete understanding:

Example: First conduct a survey to measure how many students struggle with algebra (quantitative), then conduct interviews to understand why they struggle (qualitative).

Justifying Your Method Choice

How to Justify

A good justification explains why your chosen method is the most appropriate for your research question.

Elements of a Good Justification

  1. Clearly state your research question
  2. Identify the type of data needed to answer it
  3. Explain why your chosen method is best suited to collect that data
  4. Address potential limitations and how you'll mitigate them
  5. Consider alternative methods and why yours is superior

Example Justification:

"For our research question 'How does peer tutoring affect mathematics performance in JHS3?', we selected a mixed methods approach. We'll use test scores (quantitative) to measure performance changes and conduct interviews (qualitative) to understand students' experiences. This combination allows us to both measure the impact and explore how it occurs."

Practice Exercise

Question 1

You want to investigate "What percentage of JHS3 students use online resources for mathematics learning?" Which data collection method would be most appropriate and why?

Solution: A survey would be most appropriate because:

  • The question requires numerical data (percentage)
  • Surveys can efficiently collect data from many students
  • Closed-ended questions can quantify usage patterns

Question 2

Your research question is "How do students feel about group work in mathematics class?" Select and justify a data collection method.

Solution: Focus groups or interviews would be appropriate because:

  • The question explores feelings and experiences (qualitative)
  • Group discussions can reveal diverse perspectives
  • Open-ended questions allow students to express their views in detail

Question 3

Compare the advantages of using surveys versus interviews for collecting data about students' study habits.

Solution:

Surveys:

  • Can reach more participants quickly
  • Easier to analyze statistically
  • Anonymous responses may increase honesty

Interviews:

  • Provide deeper, more detailed information
  • Allow follow-up questions based on responses
  • Can explore complex behaviors more thoroughly
