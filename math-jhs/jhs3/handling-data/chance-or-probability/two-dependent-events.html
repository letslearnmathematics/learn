Introduction

Dependent events in probability are events where the outcome of one event affects the outcome of another. This means the probability of the second event changes based on what happened in the first event.

A common example is drawing objects from a container without replacement. When you remove an item, it changes the composition of what remains, affecting the probabilities of subsequent draws.

In this lesson, we'll perform an experiment with colored bottle tops to understand how dependent events work.

Key Terms

Without replacement: When items are not returned to the container after being drawn.

Sample space: All possible outcomes of an experiment.

Probability: The likelihood of an event occurring, calculated as: \[ P(\text{Event}) = \frac{\text{Number of favorable outcomes}}{\text{Total possible outcomes}} \]

The Experiment

Materials Needed

  1. A bag or container
  2. 5 red bottle tops
  3. 3 blue bottle tops
  4. 2 green bottle tops
  5. Paper and pencil for recording results

Experimental Procedure

  1. Place all 10 bottle tops (5 red, 3 blue, 2 green) into the bag
  2. Without looking, draw one bottle top and record its color
  3. Do NOT return the bottle top to the bag
  4. Draw a second bottle top and record its color
  5. Repeat this process 20 times, recording each pair of colors
  6. After each trial, return all bottle tops to the bag and mix them

Recording Results

Blue then Green

Probability Calculations

Theoretical Probability

Let's calculate the theoretical probabilities for our experiment. Remember that since we're not replacing the first bottle top, the second probability is affected by the first draw.

Example 1: Probability of drawing Red then Blue

\[ \begin{align*} P(\text{Red first}) &= \frac{5}{10} = \frac{1}{2} \\ P(\text{Blue second}) &= \frac{3}{9} = \frac{1}{3} \quad \text{(since one red is already removed)} \\ P(\text{Red then Blue}) &= \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{6} \approx 16.67\% \end{align*} \]

Example 2: Probability of drawing two Reds in a row

\[ \begin{align*} P(\text{Red first}) &= \frac{5}{10} = \frac{1}{2} \\ P(\text{Red second}) &= \frac{4}{9} \quad \text{(one red already removed)} \\ P(\text{Red then Red}) &= \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{4}{9} = \frac{4}{18} = \frac{2}{9} \approx 22.22\% \end{align*} \]

Comparing Results

After completing your 20 trials, compare your experimental probabilities with the theoretical probabilities we calculated. They should be somewhat similar, though not exactly the same due to the randomness of the experiment.

The more trials you perform (e.g., 100 instead of 20), the closer your experimental results should get to the theoretical probabilities.

Tree Diagrams

Visualizing Dependent Events

Tree diagrams are helpful tools for visualizing and calculating probabilities of dependent events. Each branch represents a possible outcome, with probabilities written along the branches.

First Draw:

  • Red: 5/10 = 0.5
  • Blue: 3/10 = 0.3
  • Green: 2/10 = 0.2

Second Draw (after Red was drawn first):

  • Red: 4/9 ≈ 0.444
  • Blue: 3/9 ≈ 0.333
  • Green: 2/9 ≈ 0.222

Second Draw (after Blue was drawn first):

  • Red: 5/9 ≈ 0.555
  • Blue: 2/9 ≈ 0.222
  • Green: 2/9 ≈ 0.222

Calculating Path Probabilities

To find the probability of a specific sequence (path) in the tree diagram, multiply the probabilities along that path.

For example, the probability of drawing Blue then Green: \[ P(\text{Blue then Green}) = \frac{3}{10} \times \frac{2}{9} = \frac{6}{90} = \frac{1}{15} \approx 6.67\% \]

The sum of all possible path probabilities should equal 1 (or 100%).

Practice Exercise

Question 1

If you have a bag with 4 red marbles and 6 blue marbles, what is the probability of drawing two red marbles in a row without replacement?

\[ \begin{align*} P(\text{Red first}) &= \frac{4}{10} = 0.4 \\ P(\text{Red second}) &= \frac{3}{9} \approx 0.333 \\ P(\text{Red then Red}) &= 0.4 \times 0.333 \approx 0.133 \text{ or } 13.3\% \end{align*} \]

Question 2

Using the same bag (4 red, 6 blue), what is the probability of drawing one red and one blue marble in any order without replacement?

There are two possible sequences:

1. Red first, then blue:

\[ P = \frac{4}{10} \times \frac{6}{9} = \frac{24}{90} \approx 0.267 \]

2. Blue first, then red:

\[ P = \frac{6}{10} \times \frac{4}{9} = \frac{24}{90} \approx 0.267 \]

Total probability = 0.267 + 0.267 ≈ 0.533 or 53.3%

Question 3

A box contains 5 white balls and 7 black balls. Two balls are drawn successively without replacement. What is the probability that both balls are black?

\[ \begin{align*} P(\text{Black first}) &= \frac{7}{12} \\ P(\text{Black second}) &= \frac{6}{11} \\ P(\text{Both black}) &= \frac{7}{12} \times \frac{6}{11} = \frac{42}{132} = \frac{7}{22} \approx 31.8\% \end{align*} \]
