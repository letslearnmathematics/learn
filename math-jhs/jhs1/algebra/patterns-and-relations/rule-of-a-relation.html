Introduction

In mathematics, a relation describes how numbers or quantities are connected to each other. We can describe these connections using mathematical language like "one more than", "twice as much as", or "three less than".

Example: If every input number is 5 more than the output number, we can say the rule is "output is input minus 5" or \( y = x - 5 \).

Understanding Relations

A relation shows how two sets of numbers are connected. We can represent relations in different ways:

  • Tables: Showing input and output values
  • Words: Describing the relationship in sentences
  • Equations: Using mathematical expressions
Input (x) Output (y)
1 3
2 5
3 7

The rule is: "Output is one more than twice the input" or \( y = 2x + 1 \)

Describing Relations

Identifying Patterns

To describe a relation, first look for the pattern between input and output values:

  1. Examine how the output changes when the input changes
  2. Look for consistent operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication)
  3. Describe the relationship in words

Example 1:

Input (x) Output (y)
2 5
3 7
4 9

Rule: "Output is one less than twice the input" or \( y = 2x - 1 \)

Example 2:

Input (x) Output (y)
5 2
6 3
7 4

Rule: "Output is three less than the input" or \( y = x - 3 \)

Common Relationship Types

Here are some common types of relationships:

Relationship Type Example Rule Example Values
One more than \( y = x + 1 \) 3 → 4, 5 → 6
One less than \( y = x - 1 \) 3 → 2, 5 → 4
Twice as much \( y = 2x \) 3 → 6, 5 → 10
Half as much \( y = \frac{x}{2} \) 4 → 2, 6 → 3
Three more than twice \( y = 2x + 3 \) 2 → 7, 3 → 9

Mathematical Language

Translating Words to Math

We can translate English phrases into mathematical expressions:

Addition Phrases

  • "5 more than a number" → \( x + 5 \)
  • "A number increased by 3" → \( x + 3 \)
  • "The sum of a number and 7" → \( x + 7 \)

Subtraction Phrases

  • "4 less than a number" → \( x - 4 \)
  • "A number decreased by 2" → \( x - 2 \)
  • "The difference between a number and 5" → \( x - 5 \)

Multiplication Phrases

  • "Twice a number" → \( 2x \)
  • "Half of a number" → \( \frac{x}{2} \)
  • "The product of a number and 3" → \( 3x \)

Combining Operations

Some relationships combine multiple operations:

Phrase Expression
"Three more than twice a number" \( 2x + 3 \)
"Five less than half a number" \( \frac{x}{2} - 5 \)
"One more than three times a number" \( 3x + 1 \)

Practice identifying these patterns in word problems and tables.

Applications

Real-World Examples

Understanding mathematical relations helps solve real-world problems:

Age Problems

"John is 3 years older than Mary" can be written as \( J = M + 3 \)

If Mary is 10, then John is \( 10 + 3 = 13 \) years old.

Pricing Problems

"A shirt costs twice as much as a hat" can be written as \( S = 2H \)

If a hat costs \$5, the shirt costs \( 2 \times 5 = \$10 \).

Measurement Problems

"The length is 5cm more than the width" can be written as \( L = W + 5 \)

If width is 8cm, length is \( 8 + 5 = 13 \)cm.
