Introduction

Dividing a quantity by a fraction is a fundamental skill in mathematics that helps us solve problems involving sharing, measurement, and proportional relationships. When we divide by a fraction, we're essentially asking "how many parts of this size fit into the whole quantity?"

\[ \text{Example: } 6 \div \frac{1}{2} = 12 \]

This means there are 12 half-parts in 6 wholes.

Key Idea

Dividing by a fraction is the same as multiplying by its reciprocal (the fraction flipped upside down):

\[ a \div \frac{b}{c} = a \times \frac{c}{b} \]
  • Reciprocal: The inverse of a fraction (e.g., reciprocal of \(\frac{2}{3}\) is \(\frac{3}{2}\))
  • Whole numbers: Treat as fractions with denominator 1 (e.g., 5 = \(\frac{5}{1}\))

Understanding the Concept

Why Does This Work?

Dividing by a fraction is equivalent to multiplying by its reciprocal because:

  1. Division asks "how many parts fit into the whole?"
  2. When dividing by a fraction, we're asking how many of those fractional parts make up the whole
  3. This is the same as multiplying by the reciprocal

Visual Example: 4 ÷ ½

Imagine 4 whole pizzas. How many half-pizzas (½ slices) are there?

Each whole pizza has 2 half-pizzas, so 4 pizzas × 2 halves = 8 halves

\[ 4 \div \frac{1}{2} = 4 \times \frac{2}{1} = 8 \]

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting to find the reciprocal: Many students try to divide directly without flipping the fraction
  • Flipping the first number: Only the divisor (the fraction you're dividing by) gets flipped
  • Not simplifying: Always simplify your final answer if possible

Incorrect: \( 8 \div \frac{2}{3} = 8 \times \frac{2}{3} \) (didn't flip)

Correct: \( 8 \div \frac{2}{3} = 8 \times \frac{3}{2} = 12 \)

Step-by-Step Process

Method for Dividing by Fractions

Follow these steps to divide any quantity by a fraction:

  1. Identify the reciprocal of the divisor (the fraction you're dividing by)
  2. Change the division sign to multiplication
  3. Multiply the first number by the reciprocal
  4. Simplify the resulting fraction if possible

Example 1: \( 5 \div \frac{1}{4} \)

  1. Reciprocal of \(\frac{1}{4}\) is \(\frac{4}{1}\)
  2. Change to multiplication: \( 5 \times \frac{4}{1} \)
  3. Multiply: \( \frac{5}{1} \times \frac{4}{1} = \frac{20}{1} \)
  4. Simplify: 20
\[ 5 \div \frac{1}{4} = 20 \]

Example 2: \( \frac{3}{4} \div \frac{2}{5} \)

  1. Reciprocal of \(\frac{2}{5}\) is \(\frac{5}{2}\)
  2. Change to multiplication: \( \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{5}{2} \)
  3. Multiply: \( \frac{15}{8} \)
  4. Simplify: \( 1\frac{7}{8} \) (convert to mixed number)
\[ \frac{3}{4} \div \frac{2}{5} = 1\frac{7}{8} \]

Real-World Applications

Practical Examples

Dividing by fractions is useful in many everyday situations:

Recipe Adjustments

If a recipe calls for 2 cups of flour but you only have \(\frac{1}{3}\) cup measures:

\[ 2 \div \frac{1}{3} = 6 \text{ (you need 6 scoops)} \]

Construction Projects

If a 12-foot board needs to be cut into \(\frac{3}{4}\)-foot pieces:

\[ 12 \div \frac{3}{4} = 16 \text{ (you get 16 pieces)} \]

Time Management

If you have 5 hours to complete tasks that each take \(\frac{1}{2}\) hour:

\[ 5 \div \frac{1}{2} = 10 \text{ (you can complete 10 tasks)} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Calculate \( 4 \div \frac{1}{5} \)

\[ 4 \times \frac{5}{1} = 20 \]

Question 2

Find \( \frac{2}{3} \div \frac{4}{9} \)

\[ \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{9}{4} = \frac{18}{12} = 1\frac{1}{2} \]

Question 3

Solve \( 7 \div \frac{2}{3} \)

\[ 7 \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{21}{2} = 10\frac{1}{2} \]

Question 4

Calculate \( \frac{5}{8} \div \frac{2}{3} \)

\[ \frac{5}{8} \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{15}{16} \]

Question 5

If you have 10 meters of ribbon and need pieces that are \(\frac{2}{5}\) meters long, how many pieces can you get?

\[ 10 \div \frac{2}{5} = 10 \times \frac{5}{2} = 25 \text{ pieces} \]

Question 6

A recipe calls for \(\frac{3}{4}\) cup of sugar. If you want to make \(\frac{1}{3}\) of the recipe, how much sugar do you need?

This is actually a multiplication problem: \( \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{1}{3} = \frac{3}{12} = \frac{1}{4} \) cup

But if the question was "how many \(\frac{1}{3}\) portions are in \(\frac{3}{4}\) cup?", then:

\[ \frac{3}{4} \div \frac{1}{3} = \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{3}{1} = \frac{9}{4} = 2\frac{1}{4} \text{ portions} \]
