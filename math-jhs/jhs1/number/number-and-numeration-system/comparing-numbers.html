Skip Counting

Video Lesson

Watch this comprehensive explanation on the lesson with worked examples.

Main Idea

Skip counting is a way that we can perform addition or subtraction of whole numbers.

Consider the numbers fifty (50) and one hundred (100). On the real number line, skipping from 50 to 100 can be thought of as adding 50 to 50 in order to obtain 100.

Similarly, moving from 100 to 50, can be thought of as subtracting 50 from 100 to obtain 50.

Intervals

The distance between 2 numbers on the number line is termed as the interval between the two numbers. In our example below, we can therefore say that we are skipping in an interval of 50 to the right, or to the left.

Movement to the left

Movement to the left on the real number line means subtraction

\[ 100 - 50 = 50 \]

Movement to the right

Movement to the right on the real number line means addition

\[ 50 + 50 = 100 \]

Main Idea

Place value refers to the value of a digit based on its position within a given number. Each digit in a number has a different place value, depending on its position (or place) from right to left.

Comparing two numbers

To compare two large numbers, sometimes it is better to compare the place value of the individual digits of the numbers.

Step 1

Number of Digits

When comparing 2 numbers using their place values, we first look at the number of digits within the numbers. The higher the number of digits, the bigger the number.

For example, 2,000 is bigger than 300 because 2,000 has 4 digits while the number 300 has only 3 digits.

Step 2

The place value chart

Each digit in a number has a different place value, depending on its position from right to left.

The values increase as you move from from right to left on the place value chart as shown above.

Comparison Operators

Main Idea

The symbols discussed below are called comparison symbols or comparison operator, as they are symbols we use to compare two or more numbers or values.

Greater than \((>)\)

Given any number on the number line, all the numbers to the right of that number are bigger than the given number.

It also implies that all the numbers to the left of the number are smaller than the given number.

\[5 > 4\]

Read as, "5 is greater than 4."

Less than \((<)\)

Similarly, the value of the numbers decrease as we move to the left of any number on the number line.

We can therefore say, that the numbers to the left of any given number on the real number line are smaller than the number in question.

\[2 < 3\]

Read as, "2 is less than 3."

Equal to \((=)\)

When two quantities have the same value, we say that one is equal to the other.

We use the equal to symbol to indicate this comparison.

\[4 + 1 = 5\]

Read as, "the sum of 4 and 1 is equal to 5."
