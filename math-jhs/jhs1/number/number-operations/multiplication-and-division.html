Introduction

Multiplication and division are essential operations that help us solve real-world problems efficiently. Whether calculating areas, distributing items equally, or scaling quantities, mastering these operations is crucial for mathematical success.

In this lesson, you will learn:

  • How to multiply using partitioning and expanded form
  • The place value method and lattice method for multiplication
  • How the distributive property simplifies calculations
  • Strategies for division and divisibility tests
  • Common pitfalls to avoid

Multiplication Using Partitioning

Breaking Numbers Apart

Partitioning splits numbers into tens, hundreds, etc., making multiplication easier to manage.

Example 1: Multiply 23 × 14

Step 1: Partition both numbers:

\[ 23 = 20 + 3 \] \[ 14 = 10 + 4 \]

Step 2: Multiply each part (like a grid):

\[ 20 \times 10 = 200 \] \[ 20 \times 4 = 80 \] \[ 3 \times 10 = 30 \] \[ 3 \times 4 = 12 \]

Step 3: Add all partial products:

\[ 200 + 80 + 30 + 12 = 322 \]

Final Answer: 23 × 14 = 322

Why Partitioning Works

This method leverages the distributive property of multiplication over addition:

\[ a \times (b + c) = (a \times b) + (a \times c) \]

Visual

Clearly shows how each digit contributes

Flexible

Works for any number size

Conceptual

Builds number sense

Place Value (Vertical) Method

Traditional Multiplication

Step-by-Step:

  1. Write numbers vertically, aligning by place value
  2. Multiply the top number by each digit of the bottom number (right to left)
  3. Add zero placeholders as you move left
  4. Sum all partial products

Visual Multiplication

Step 3: Add diagonally from right to left:

\[ 5 \ (\text{units}) \] \[ 2 + 0 + 3 = 5 \ (\text{tens}) \] \[ 1 + 2 = 3 \ (\text{hundreds}) \]

Final Answer: 47 × 35 = 1,645

Distributive Property

Breaking Down Complex Problems

The distributive property allows us to multiply a sum by multiplying each addend separately:

\[ a \times (b + c) = (a \times b) + (a \times c) \]

Example: Multiply 6 × 143

Step 1: Break down 143:

\[ 143 = 100 + 40 + 3 \]

Step 2: Multiply each part by 6:

\[ 6 \times 100 = 600 \] \[ 6 \times 40 = 240 \] \[ 6 \times 3 = 18 \]

Step 3: Add the results:

\[ 600 + 240 + 18 = 858 \]

Final Answer: 6 × 143 = 858

Division & Divisibility

Division Using Partitioning

Break the dividend into manageable parts that are easily divisible by the divisor.

Example: Divide 1,232 ÷ 16

Step 1: Partition 1,232:

\[ 1,232 = 1,200 + 32 \]

Step 2: Divide each part by 16:

\[ 1,200 ÷ 16 = 75 \] \[ 32 ÷ 16 = 2 \]

Step 3: Add the results:

\[ 75 + 2 = 77 \]

Final Answer: 1,232 ÷ 16 = 77

Divisibility Rules

Divisible by 2

Ends in 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8

e.g., 58 → 8 is even

Divisible by 3

Sum of digits ÷ 3

123 → 1+2+3=6, 6÷3=2 ✓

Divisible by 5

Ends in 0 or 5

e.g., 120, 345

Divisible by 10

Ends in 0

e.g., 1,000

Practice Exercises

Question 1

Multiply 45 × 18 using partitioning.

Step 1: Partition both numbers:

\[ 45 = 40 + 5 \] \[ 18 = 10 + 8 \]

Step 2: Multiply each part:

\[ 40 \times 10 = 400 \] \[ 40 \times 8 = 320 \] \[ 5 \times 10 = 50 \] \[ 5 \times 8 = 40 \]

Step 3: Add all partial products:

\[ 400 + 320 + 50 + 40 = 810 \]

Final Answer: 45 × 18 = 810

Question 2

Divide 828 ÷ 12 using partitioning.

Step 1: Partition 828:

\[ 828 = 720 + 108 \]

Step 2: Divide each part by 12:

\[ 720 ÷ 12 = 60 \] \[ 108 ÷ 12 = 9 \]

Step 3: Add the results:

\[ 60 + 9 = 69 \]

Final Answer: 828 ÷ 12 = 69

Common Mistakes

Misaligned Place Values

Forgetting to shift left when multiplying by tens.

Wrong:

\[ \begin{array}{r} 23 \\ \underline{\times \, 15} \\ 115 \\ +\ 23 \quad \text{(should be 230)} \\ \hline 138 \quad \text{(✗ should be 345)} \end{array} \]

Ignoring Zero Placeholders

Skipping zeros in partial products.

Wrong:

\[ 30 \times 12 = 36 \quad \text{(✗ forgot the 0)} \]

Pro Tips

  • Always estimate first to check reasonableness
  • Use graph paper to align place values
  • Verify division by multiplying back
