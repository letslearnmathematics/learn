Introduction

In mathematics, we often encounter situations where we multiply the same number by itself multiple times. This concept is called repeated factors and leads us to the idea of powers or exponents.

For example:

\[ 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8 \]

This can be written more compactly as \(2^3 = 8\)

In this lesson, we will:

  1. Model repeated factors using counting objects
  2. Understand what powers of numbers mean
  3. Learn about the features of powers like \(2^3\)

Powers of Numbers

What is a Power?

A power is a short way to write repeated multiplication of the same number. It consists of two parts:

  • The base (the number being multiplied)
  • The exponent (how many times the base is multiplied)

For example:

\[ 5 \times 5 \times 5 = 5^3 \]

Read as "five to the power of three" or "five cubed"

Special Names

Squared (Power of 2)

When the exponent is 2, we say the number is "squared":

\[ 4^2 = 4 \times 4 = 16 \]

"Four squared equals sixteen"

Cubed (Power of 3)

When the exponent is 3, we say the number is "cubed":

\[ 3^3 = 3 \times 3 \times 3 = 27 \]

"Three cubed equals twenty-seven"

Features of Powers

Understanding \(2^3\)

Let's examine the power \(2^3\) in detail to understand its components:

\[ 2^3 = 8 \]

The Base (2)

The base is the number being multiplied by itself.

In \(2^3\), the base is 2.

This means we're working with the number 2.

The Exponent/Index (3)

The exponent tells us how many times to multiply the base by itself.

In \(2^3\), the exponent is 3.

This means we multiply 2 by itself 3 times:

\[ 2 \times 2 \times 2 \]

The Result (8)

The result of the multiplication is called the power value.

For \(2^3\):

\[ 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8 \]

So \(2^3 = 8\)
