Introduction

In geometry, we often need to work with angles - copying them exactly or dividing them into two equal parts. These skills are essential for more complex constructions and real-world applications.

You will need:

  • A sharp pencil
  • A ruler
  • A pair of compasses
  • A clean sheet of paper

Understanding Angles

An angle is formed when two straight lines meet at a point. The size of an angle is measured in degrees (°):

  • Acute angle: less than 90°
  • Right angle: exactly 90°
  • Obtuse angle: between 90° and 180°
  • Straight angle: exactly 180°

We represent angles using the ∠ symbol. For example, ∠ABC means the angle at point B between lines BA and BC.

Copying an Angle

Step-by-Step Construction

To copy a given angle ∠ABC using compass and ruler:

  1. Draw a base line and mark a point P where you want the copied angle's vertex
  2. With compass at B, draw an arc that crosses both arms of ∠ABC (mark these points D and E)
  3. Without changing compass width, draw the same arc centered at P (mark intersection Q)
  4. Measure distance DE with your compass
  5. With this distance, draw an arc from Q that intersects the first arc (mark intersection R)
  6. Draw a line from P through R to complete the copied angle ∠RPQ ≅ ∠ABC

Verification

To verify your copied angle is correct:

  1. Measure the original angle with a protractor
  2. Measure the copied angle
  3. Both measurements should be equal

Remember: The compass width must not change when transferring distances during construction.

Bisecting an Angle

Step-by-Step Construction

To bisect a given angle ∠ABC (divide it into two equal angles):

  1. With compass at B, draw an arc that crosses both arms (mark points D and E)
  2. With compass at D, draw an arc inside the angle
  3. With same compass width, draw an arc from E that intersects the previous arc (mark intersection F)
  4. Draw a line from B through F - this is the angle bisector

Visual Example:

Animation showing angle bisecting process

Properties of Angle Bisectors

The angle bisector:

  • Divides the angle into two equal parts
  • Is equidistant from both arms of the angle
  • Creates two congruent angles

For a 60° angle, the bisector creates two 30° angles.

Applications

Practical Uses

These constructions are used in many real-world situations:

Architecture and Design

Copying angles is essential when creating symmetrical designs or replicating existing structures.

Navigation

Bisecting angles helps in determining midpoints between two directions.

Engineering

Precise angle copying is needed in mechanical designs and machine parts.

Question 2

Bisect a 72° angle. What will be the measure of each resulting angle?

Each resulting angle will be 36° (72° ÷ 2 = 36°)

Question 3

What happens if you change the compass width when copying an angle?

The copied angle will be incorrect because the proportions won't match the original angle.
