Introduction

A circle is a perfectly round shape where all points on its edge are equally distant from its center. Understanding circles is important in many areas of mathematics and real-life applications like wheels, clocks, and plates.

Key Concepts

Today we will learn about:

  • The different parts of a circle
  • How to measure these parts
  • The special relationship between diameter and circumference
  • How to use this relationship to solve problems

Parts of a Circle

Main Parts

Let's identify and name the important parts of a circle:

Radius (r)

The distance from the center to any point on the circle

Diameter (d)

The distance across the circle through the center (2 × radius)

\[ d = 2r \]

Circumference (C)

The distance around the circle (the perimeter)

Measuring Circular Objects

Let's measure some circular objects (like bottles, cans, or plates):

  1. Measure the diameter (d) across the center
  2. Measure the circumference (C) by wrapping a string around it
  3. Calculate the radius (r = d ÷ 2)

Diameter-Circumference Relationship

The Special Ratio

When we divide the circumference (C) by the diameter (d) of any circle, we always get the same number:

\[ \pi = \frac{C}{d} \approx 3.14159... \]

This special number is called pi (π). It's approximately equal to 3.14 or 22/7.

Circumference Formulas

From this relationship, we get two formulas for circumference:

\[ C = \pi d \] \[ C = 2\pi r \]

The second formula comes from the fact that diameter (d) = 2 × radius (r).

Historical Note

Ancient mathematicians noticed this relationship thousands of years ago. The Babylonians and Egyptians used approximations of π in their calculations.

Babylonians: π ≈ 3.125

Egyptians: π ≈ 3.1605

Today we know π to billions of digits!

Solving Problems

Example Problems

Let's solve some problems using the circumference formulas:

Example 1

A circle has diameter 10 cm. Find its circumference.

\[ C = \pi d = \pi \times 10 \approx 31.4 \text{ cm} \]

Example 2

A circle has radius 7 m. Find its circumference.

\[ C = 2\pi r = 2 \times \pi \times 7 \approx 44 \text{ m} \]

Example 3

A circular track has circumference 100 m. Find its diameter.

\[ C = \pi d \] \[ 100 = \pi d \] \[ d = \frac{100}{\pi} \approx 31.83 \text{ m} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

A circle has diameter 14 cm. Calculate its circumference (use π = 22/7).

\[ C = \pi d = \frac{22}{7} \times 14 = 44 \text{ cm} \]

Question 2

Find the circumference of a circle with radius 5 m (use π = 3.14).

\[ C = 2\pi r = 2 \times 3.14 \times 5 = 31.4 \text{ m} \]

Question 3

The circumference of a bicycle wheel is 176 cm. Find its diameter (use π = 22/7).

\[ C = \pi d \] \[ 176 = \frac{22}{7} \times d \] \[ d = 176 \times \frac{7}{22} = 56 \text{ cm} \]

Question 4

If the radius of a circular pond is 21 m, what is its circumference (use π = 22/7)?

\[ C = 2\pi r = 2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 21 = 132 \text{ m} \]

Question 5

A circular table has circumference 3.14 m. Find its radius (use π = 3.14).

\[ C = 2\pi r \] \[ 3.14 = 2 \times 3.14 \times r \] \[ r = \frac{3.14}{2 \times 3.14} = 0.5 \text{ m} \]

Question 6

Which is longer: The circumference of a circle with diameter 10 cm or the perimeter of a square with side 8 cm?

Circle circumference: \( C = \pi \times 10 \approx 31.4 \text{ cm} \)

Square perimeter: \( P = 4 \times 8 = 32 \text{ cm} \)

The square's perimeter is slightly longer.
