Introduction

Data collection is the process of gathering information to answer questions or solve problems. There are two main types of data:

Quantitative Data

Numerical data that can be measured or counted.

Examples: Height, weight, test scores, number of students

Qualitative Data

Descriptive data about qualities or characteristics.

Examples: Favorite color, opinions, feelings, preferences

Why Data Collection Matters

Collecting good data helps us:

  • Make informed decisions
  • Solve problems effectively
  • Understand patterns and trends
  • Support arguments with evidence

Group Discussion Activity

Discussion Scenarios

In small groups, discuss and write down how you would make decisions in these situations, what facts you would consider, and how you would collect these facts:

1. The type of drinks to buy for a class party

Consider: Popular preferences, budget, quantity needed

2. The make of football boots to buy for the school team

Consider: Player preferences, durability, cost, brand reputation

3. Do people who eat more fufu develop pot belly?

Consider: Dietary habits, exercise routines, body measurements

4. The number of desks in each classroom

Consider: Class sizes, room dimensions, current inventory

5. The amount of money B6 students spend on bus fare to school every month

Consider: Distance traveled, fare rates, frequency of travel

6. Buying a mobile phone from an online shop

Consider: Features needed, budget, customer reviews, return policies

Data Collection Methods

Common Methods

Different situations require different data collection methods. Here are the main methods:

Questionnaires

Written questions for many people to answer.

Best for: Gathering opinions from many people quickly

Interviews

Face-to-face or phone conversations with people.

Best for: Detailed personal information

Observation

Watching and recording what happens.

Best for: Studying behavior in natural settings

Experiments

Testing ideas under controlled conditions.

Best for: Determining cause-and-effect relationships

Surveys

Combination of questionnaires and interviews.

Best for: Gathering both quantitative and qualitative data

Databases

Using existing records and information.

Best for: Historical or large-scale data

Electronic Media/Internet

Using online sources and digital tools.

Best for: Quick access to wide-ranging information

Choosing the Right Method

When selecting a data collection method, consider:

  1. Purpose: What question are you trying to answer?
  2. Resources: What time, money, and tools do you have?
  3. Accuracy: How precise does the data need to be?
  4. Subjects: Who or what are you collecting data about?
  5. Ethics: Is the method respectful and fair?

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Your school wants to know students' favorite subjects. Which data collection method would be most appropriate?

Questionnaire or survey - can efficiently gather preferences from many students.

Question 2

A scientist wants to test if a new fertilizer helps plants grow faster. Which method should they use?

Experiment - need to control conditions and measure growth differences.

Question 3

You want to know how many cars pass your school between 7-8am each morning. What method would work best?

Observation - need to directly count the cars during that time period.

Question 4

A company wants detailed feedback about their new product from customers. Which method should they choose?

Interviews - allow for in-depth, personalized responses.

Question 5

You need to find the average rainfall in your region over the past 10 years. Where would you get this data?

Database - meteorological records would have this historical data.

Question 6

For each scenario below, identify whether quantitative or qualitative data would be more appropriate:

  1. Measuring student heights
  2. Recording favorite music genres
  3. Counting absent students each day
  4. Describing classroom atmosphere
  1. Quantitative (numerical measurements)
  2. Qualitative (categories/preferences)
  3. Quantitative (countable numbers)
  4. Qualitative (descriptive qualities)
