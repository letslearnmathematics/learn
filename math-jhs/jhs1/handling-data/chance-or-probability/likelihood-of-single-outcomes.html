Introduction

In probability, we can classify how likely an outcome is to occur using three main terms: impossible, possible, and certain.

Term Meaning Example
Impossible Will never happen Getting a 7 when rolling a die
Possible Might happen Getting a 3 when rolling a die
Certain Will always happen Getting a number between 1-6 when rolling a die

Understanding Probability Terms

Let's understand these terms better:

  • Impossible: An outcome that cannot occur in any situation (probability = 0)
  • Possible: An outcome that has a chance of occurring (probability between 0 and 1)
  • Certain: An outcome that is guaranteed to happen (probability = 1)

Example: When flipping a coin:

  • Getting "heads" is possible
  • Getting "tails" is possible
  • Getting "neither heads nor tails" is impossible
  • Getting "either heads or tails" is certain

Single Die Outcomes

Outcomes of Throwing One Die

A standard die has 6 faces numbered 1 through 6. Let's classify some outcomes:

A. Obtaining the number 1

This is possible because the die has a face with number 1.

Probability: \(\frac{1}{6}\) (1 chance out of 6 possible outcomes)

B. Obtaining the number 7

This is impossible because a standard die only has numbers 1 through 6.

Probability: 0 (no chance of occurring)

C. Obtaining the number 4

This is possible because the die has a face with number 4.

Probability: \(\frac{1}{6}\) (1 chance out of 6 possible outcomes)

Key Points to Remember

  1. For a standard die, numbers 1 through 6 are all possible outcomes
  2. Any number outside 1-6 is impossible
  3. Getting some number between 1-6 is certain (it must happen)
  4. All numbers 1-6 have equal probability (\(\frac{1}{6}\))

Two Dice Outcomes

Outcomes of Throwing Two Dice

When throwing two dice, the possible totals range from 2 to 12. Let's classify some outcomes:

A. Obtaining a total of 12

This is possible but rare (only one combination: 6+6).

Probability: \(\frac{1}{36}\) (1 chance out of 36 possible outcomes)

B. Obtaining a total of 2

This is possible but rare (only one combination: 1+1).

Probability: \(\frac{1}{36}\) (1 chance out of 36 possible outcomes)

C. Obtaining a total of 13

This is impossible because the maximum total with two dice is 12 (6+6).

Probability: 0 (no chance of occurring)

Possible Totals with Two Dice

Here are all possible totals when throwing two standard dice:

Total Possible Combinations Probability
2 1+1 \(\frac{1}{36}\)
3 1+2, 2+1 \(\frac{2}{36}\)
4 1+3, 2+2, 3+1 \(\frac{3}{36}\)
5 1+4, 2+3, 3+2, 4+1 \(\frac{4}{36}\)
6 1+5, 2+4, 3+3, 4+2, 5+1 \(\frac{5}{36}\)
7 1+6, 2+5, 3+4, 4+3, 5+2, 6+1 \(\frac{6}{36}\)
8 2+6, 3+5, 4+4, 5+3, 6+2 \(\frac{5}{36}\)
9 3+6, 4+5, 5+4, 6+3 \(\frac{4}{36}\)
10 4+6, 5+5, 6+4 \(\frac{3}{36}\)
11 5+6, 6+5 \(\frac{2}{36}\)
12 6+6 \(\frac{1}{36}\)

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Classify the outcome: Getting a 5 when rolling a single die.

This is possible (probability = \(\frac{1}{6}\))

Question 2

Classify the outcome: Getting a total of 1 when rolling two dice.

This is impossible (minimum total is 2)

Question 3

Classify the outcome: Getting an even number when rolling a single die.

This is possible (can get 2, 4, or 6; probability = \(\frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}\))

Question 4

Classify the outcome: Getting a total between 2 and 12 when rolling two dice.

This is certain (all possible totals fall in this range)

Question 5

Classify the outcome: Getting a total of 7 when rolling two dice.

This is possible (probability = \(\frac{6}{36} = \frac{1}{6}\))

Question 6

Classify the outcome: Getting a number greater than 6 when rolling a single die.

This is impossible (maximum number is 6)
