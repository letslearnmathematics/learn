Introduction to Experimental Law

Experimental law in mathematics involves establishing mathematical relationships between variables based on experimental data. This is crucial in scientific research and engineering applications.

Key Concepts

  • Variables: Quantities that change in an experiment (independent and dependent)
  • Relationship: How one variable changes with respect to another
  • Constants: Fixed values in the relationship

Common Relationships

  • Direct proportion: \( y \propto x \)
  • Inverse proportion: \( y \propto \frac{1}{x} \)
  • Exponential relationships: \( y = ka^x \)
  • Logarithmic relationships: \( y = k\log x \)

General Approach

  1. Collect experimental data (x and y values)
  2. Plot graphs to identify patterns
  3. Test different relationships (linear, quadratic, etc.)
  4. Determine constants in the relationship
  5. Verify the established law with additional data

Direct Proportion

When two quantities are directly proportional, their ratio remains constant. This creates a straight-line graph through the origin.

\[ y \propto x \implies y = kx \]

Where k is the constant of proportionality.

From Data Table

Since \( \frac{y}{x} = 3 \) for all points, \( y = 3x \).

Gradient (slope) = constant of proportionality

Example Application

Hooke's Law states that the extension of a spring is directly proportional to the force applied.

\[ F \propto x \implies F = kx \]

Where F is force, x is extension, and k is the spring constant.

Inverse Proportion

When two quantities are inversely proportional, their product remains constant. The graph is a hyperbola.

\[ y \propto \frac{1}{x} \implies y = \frac{k}{x} \]

Where k is the constant of proportionality.

From Data Table

Check if \( y \times x \) is constant for all data points.

xyy×x
11212
2612
3412
4312

Since \( y \times x = 12 \) for all points, \( y = \frac{12}{x} \).

From Graph

A hyperbola indicates inverse proportion.

Plotting \( y \) against \( \frac{1}{x} \) gives a straight line through origin.

Example Application

Boyle's Law states that for a fixed amount of gas at constant temperature, pressure is inversely proportional to volume.

\[ P \propto \frac{1}{V} \implies P = \frac{k}{V} \]

Where P is pressure, V is volume, and k is a constant.

Joint Variation

Joint variation occurs when a quantity varies directly with the product of two or more other quantities.

\[ z \propto xy \implies z = kxy \]

Where k is the constant of proportionality.

Identifying Joint Variation

  1. Check if \( \frac{z}{xy} \) is constant for all data points
  2. Alternatively, keep one variable constant and check direct proportion with the other

Example Problem

The electrical resistance (R) of a wire varies directly with its length (L) and inversely with the square of its diameter (d). Establish the relationship.

\[ R \propto \frac{L}{d^2} \implies R = k\frac{L}{d^2} \]

Where k is the resistivity of the material.

Practical Method

  1. Vary one quantity while keeping others constant
  2. Determine the relationship between the variables
  3. Combine the individual relationships
  4. Determine the proportionality constant using given data

Exponential Relationships

Exponential relationships occur when a quantity changes by a constant factor over equal intervals.

\[ y = ka^x \]

Where k is the initial value and a is the growth/decay factor.

Since the ratio is constant, \( y = 5 \times 2^x \).

From Graph

An exponential curve that increases or decreases rapidly.

Plotting \( \log y \) against \( x \) gives a straight line.

Example Application

Radioactive decay follows an exponential relationship:

\[ N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t} \]

Where \( N_0 \) is initial quantity, \( \lambda \) is decay constant, and \( t \) is time.

Logarithmic Relationships

Logarithmic relationships occur when the rate of change decreases as the independent variable increases.

\[ y = a + b\ln x \]

Where a and b are constants.

Since \( y \) increases linearly with \( \ln x \), \( y = 2 + 3\ln x \).

A logarithmic curve that increases rapidly then levels off.

Example Application

The Weber-Fechner law in psychology states that perceived intensity is proportional to the logarithm of actual intensity.

\[ P = k\ln S \]

Where P is perceived intensity and S is stimulus intensity.

Practical Methods for Determining Laws

Step-by-Step Approach

1

Collect Data

Record accurate measurements of the variables under investigation.

2

Plot Graphs

Plot y against x, y against 1/x, log y against x, etc. to identify patterns.

3

Identify Linear Form

Find the transformation that produces a straight-line graph.

4

Determine Constants

Use the gradient and intercept to find constants in the relationship.

5

Verify the Law

Test the established law with additional experimental data.

Common Transformations

Suspected Relationship Graph to Plot What to Expect
\( y = kx^n \) log y vs log x Straight line with gradient n
\( y = ka^x \) log y vs x Straight line with gradient log a
\( y = k/x \) y vs 1/x Straight line through origin
\( y = a + b\ln x \) y vs ln x Straight line with gradient b

Worked Example

Given the following data, determine the relationship between x and y:

x1234
y83272128

Solution:

  1. Test for direct proportion: \( \frac{y}{x} \) values are 8, 16, 24, 32 → not constant
  2. Test for \( y = kx^n \) by plotting log y vs log x:
    3. log x00.3010.4770.602
    log y0.9031.5051.8572.107
  3. Gradient ≈ 2 (power relationship), intercept ≈ 0.903 (log k)
  4. Thus \( y = 8x^2 \) (since \( 10^{0.903} ≈ 8 \))
