Laws of Indices

Indices (or exponents) are a shorthand way of showing how many times a number is multiplied by itself. The general form is:

\[ a^n = \underbrace{a \times a \times \cdots \times a}_{n \text{ times}} \]

Where a is the base and n is the index/exponent.

Fundamental Laws of Indices

1. Multiplication Law

\[ a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n} \]

Example:

\[ 2^3 \times 2^4 = 2^{3+4} = 2^7 = 128 \]

2. Division Law

\[ \frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n} \]

Example:

\[ \frac{5^6}{5^2} = 5^{6-2} = 5^4 = 625 \]

3. Power of a Power

\[ (a^m)^n = a^{m \times n} \]

Example:

\[ (3^2)^4 = 3^{2 \times 4} = 3^8 = 6561 \]

4. Zero Exponent

\[ a^0 = 1 \quad (a \neq 0) \]

Example:

\[ 7^0 = 1 \]

5. Negative Exponent

\[ a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n} \]

Example:

\[ 2^{-3} = \frac{1}{2^3} = \frac{1}{8} \]

6. Fractional Exponent

\[ a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a} \] \[ a^{m/n} = (\sqrt[n]{a})^m \]

Example:

\[ 8^{2/3} = (\sqrt[3]{8})^2 = 2^2 = 4 \]

Special Cases

Same Exponent

\[ a^n \times b^n = (a \times b)^n \]
\[ 2^3 \times 5^3 = (2 \times 5)^3 = 10^3 = 1000 \]

Roots as Indices

\[ \sqrt{a} = a^{1/2} \] \[ \sqrt[n]{a} = a^{1/n} \]
\[ \sqrt{25} = 25^{1/2} = 5 \]

Equations Involving Indices

To solve equations with indices, we often express both sides with the same base and then equate the exponents.

Method 1: Same Base

If \( a^m = a^n \) then \( m = n \) (for \( a \neq 0, 1 \))

Example:

\[ 3^{2x+1} = 3^{x-3} \] \[ \Rightarrow 2x + 1 = x - 3 \] \[ \Rightarrow x = -4 \]

Method 2: Different Bases

Express both sides as powers of the same base

Example:

\[ 8^{x} = 16^{x-1} \] \[ (2^3)^x = (2^4)^{x-1} \] \[ 2^{3x} = 2^{4x-4} \] \[ \Rightarrow 3x = 4x - 4 \] \[ \Rightarrow x = 4 \]

Method 3: Substitution

Use substitution for complex equations

Example:

\[ 2^{2x} - 5 \times 2^x + 4 = 0 \] Let \( y = 2^x \): \[ y^2 - 5y + 4 = 0 \] \[ (y-1)(y-4) = 0 \] \[ y = 1 \text{ or } 4 \] \[ 2^x = 1 \Rightarrow x = 0 \] \[ 2^x = 4 \Rightarrow x = 2 \]

Common Pitfalls

Base = 1

Remember \( 1^n = 1 \) for any n. The equation \( 1^x = 1^5 \) has infinite solutions!

Base = 0

\( 0^n = 0 \) for \( n > 0 \), but \( 0^0 \) is undefined.

Negative Bases

Be careful with even roots of negative numbers: \( (-1)^{1/2} \) is not real.

Laws of Logarithms

A logarithm answers the question: "To what power must the base be raised to get this number?"

\[ \log_b a = c \iff b^c = a \]

Where b is the base, a is the argument, and c is the logarithm.

Fundamental Laws of Logarithms

1. Product Law

\[ \log_b (xy) = \log_b x + \log_b y \]

Example:

\[ \log_2 (8 \times 4) = \log_2 8 + \log_2 4 = 3 + 2 = 5 \]

2. Quotient Law

\[ \log_b \left(\frac{x}{y}\right) = \log_b x - \log_b y \]

Example:

\[ \log_{10} \left(\frac{1000}{10}\right) = \log_{10} 1000 - \log_{10} 10 = 3 - 1 = 2 \]

3. Power Law

\[ \log_b (x^n) = n \log_b x \]

Example:

\[ \log_3 (81^2) = 2 \log_3 81 = 2 \times 4 = 8 \]

4. Change of Base

\[ \log_b a = \frac{\log_k a}{\log_k b} \]

Example:

\[ \log_2 5 = \frac{\log_{10} 5}{\log_{10} 2} \approx \frac{0.6990}{0.3010} \approx 2.3219 \]

5. Logarithm of 1

\[ \log_b 1 = 0 \]

Because \( b^0 = 1 \) for any base \( b \).

6. Logarithm of Base

\[ \log_b b = 1 \]

Because \( b^1 = b \).

Special Logarithmic Forms

Common Logarithm

\[ \log_{10} x \text{ (Base 10, often written as } \log x) \]

Natural Logarithm

\[ \log_e x \text{ (Base e â‰ˆ 2.718, written as } \ln x) \]

Equations Involving Logarithms

Logarithmic equations can be solved by converting them to exponential form or using logarithmic identities.

Method 1: Exponential Conversion

Convert from logarithmic to exponential form

Example:

\[ \log_2 (x + 3) = 4 \] \[ \Rightarrow x + 3 = 2^4 \] \[ x + 3 = 16 \] \[ x = 13 \]

Method 2: Condensing Logs

Combine multiple log terms into one

Example:

\[ \log x + \log (x-3) = 1 \] \[ \log [x(x-3)] = 1 \] \[ x(x-3) = 10^1 \] \[ x^2 - 3x - 10 = 0 \] \[ (x-5)(x+2) = 0 \] \[ x = 5 \text{ (since } x > 0) \]

Method 3: Expanding Logs

Break complex log expressions into simpler parts

Example:

\[ \log \left(\frac{x^2 \sqrt{y}}{z}\right) = 2\log x + \frac{1}{2}\log y - \log z \]

Important Considerations

  • Domain Restrictions: \( \log_b a \) is only defined when \( a > 0 \) and \( b > 0, b \neq 1 \)
  • Check Solutions: Always verify that solutions satisfy the original equation's domain
  • Graphical Interpretation: Logarithmic functions are only defined for positive real numbers

Change of Base Formula

The change of base formula allows us to evaluate logarithms with any base using logarithms of another base.

\[ \log_b a = \frac{\log_k a}{\log_k b} \]

Where k is any positive number (commonly 10 or e).

Practical Applications:

  • Calculators typically only have \( \log_{10} \) and \( \ln \) buttons
  • Allows conversion between different logarithmic bases
  • Useful for solving equations with different bases

Examples:

Evaluate \( \log_3 7 \):

\[ \log_3 7 = \frac{\log_{10} 7}{\log_{10} 3} \approx \frac{0.8451}{0.4771} \approx 1.7712 \]

Solve \( \log_4 x = \log_2 5 \):

\[ \frac{\log_2 x}{\log_2 4} = \log_2 5 \] \[ \frac{\log_2 x}{2} = \log_2 5 \] \[ \log_2 x = 2\log_2 5 \] \[ x = 5^2 = 25 \]

Applications of Indices & Logarithms

Indices and logarithms have wide-ranging applications in science, engineering, and finance.

1. Exponential Growth/Decay

\[ P(t) = P_0 e^{kt} \]

Population growth, radioactive decay, etc.

2. pH Scale (Chemistry)

\[ \text{pH} = -\log_{10} [\text{H}^+] \]

Measures acidity/alkalinity

3. Richter Scale (Seismology)

\[ M = \log_{10} A - \log_{10} A_0 \]

Measures earthquake magnitude

4. Compound Interest

\[ A = P \left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} \]

Calculating investment growth

Real-World Example

A bacteria culture doubles every 3 hours. If initially there are 500 bacteria, how long will it take to reach 50,000?

Solution:

\[ P(t) = P_0 \times 2^{t/3} \] \[ 50,000 = 500 \times 2^{t/3} \] \[ 100 = 2^{t/3} \] \[ \log_{10} 100 = \frac{t}{3} \log_{10} 2 \] \[ 2 = \frac{t}{3} \times 0.3010 \] \[ t \approx 19.93 \text{ hours} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Simplify \( \frac{3^{2x+1} \times 9^{x-1}}{27^x} \)

\[ = \frac{3^{2x+1} \times (3^2)^{x-1}}{(3^3)^x} \] \[ = \frac{3^{2x+1} \times 3^{2x-2}}{3^{3x}} \] \[ = 3^{(2x+1+2x-2)-3x} = 3^{x-1} \]

Question 2

Solve for x: \( \log_3 (x+1) + \log_3 (x-1) = 1 \)

\[ \log_3 [(x+1)(x-1)] = 1 \] \[ x^2 - 1 = 3^1 \] \[ x^2 = 4 \] \[ x = 2 \text{ (x > 1)} \]

Question 3

Evaluate \( \log_2 5 \times \log_5 8 \)

\[ = \frac{\ln 5}{\ln 2} \times \frac{\ln 8}{\ln 5} = \frac{\ln 8}{\ln 2} \] \[ = \frac{\ln 2^3}{\ln 2} = \frac{3\ln 2}{\ln 2} = 3 \]
