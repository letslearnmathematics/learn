Equation of a Circle

A circle is the set of all points in a plane that are equidistant from a fixed point called the center. The distance from the center to any point on the circle is called the radius.

Standard Form

\[ (x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2 \]

Where (h, k) is the center and r is the radius.

Key Forms of Circle Equations

1. Center at Origin

\[ x^2 + y^2 = r^2 \]

Example:

\[ x^2 + y^2 = 9 \]

Center at (0,0), radius = 3

2. General Form

\[ x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0 \]

Example:

\[ x^2 + y^2 - 4x + 6y - 12 = 0 \]

Can be converted to standard form by completing the square

3. Parametric Form

\[ x = h + r\cosθ \] \[ y = k + r\sinθ \]

Example:

For circle (x-2)² + (y+1)² = 16:

\[ x = 2 + 4\cosθ \] \[ y = -1 + 4\sinθ \]

Derivation of Circle Equation

Using the distance formula between center (h,k) and any point (x,y) on the circle:

\[ \sqrt{(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2} = r \]

Squaring both sides gives the standard form:

\[ (x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2 \]

Finding Center & Radius

Given the general form of a circle's equation, we can find its center and radius by completing the square.

Method 1: Completing the Square

Convert from general to standard form

Example:

\[ x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 4y - 3 = 0 \]

Group x and y terms:

\[ (x^2 - 6x) + (y^2 + 4y) = 3 \]

Complete the square:

\[ (x^2 - 6x + 9) + (y^2 + 4y + 4) = 3 + 9 + 4 \] \[ (x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 16 \]

Center: (3, -2), Radius: 4

Method 2: Using Coefficients

For general form \( x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0 \):

  • Center: (-g, -f)
  • Radius: \( \sqrt{g^2 + f^2 - c} \)

Example:

\[ x^2 + y^2 + 8x - 10y + 5 = 0 \]

Here 2g=8 ⇒ g=4, 2f=-10 ⇒ f=-5

Center: (-4, 5)

Radius: \( \sqrt{4^2 + (-5)^2 - 5} = \sqrt{16 + 25 - 5} = 6 \)

Special Cases

Point Circle

When radius = 0, the circle degenerates to a single point

\[ (x-2)^2 + (y+3)^2 = 0 \]

Represents just the point (2, -3)

Imaginary Circle

When \( g^2 + f^2 - c < 0 \), no real points satisfy the equation

\[ x^2 + y^2 - 4x + 6y + 15 = 0 \]

Radius would be \( \sqrt{4 + 9 - 15} = \sqrt{-2} \) (imaginary)

Tangent & Normal to a Circle

A tangent to a circle is a straight line that touches the circle at exactly one point, called the point of tangency. The normal is the line perpendicular to the tangent at the point of contact.

1. Equation of Tangent

At point (x₁, y₁) on circle \( (x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2 \):

\[ (x₁ - h)(x - h) + (y₁ - k)(y - k) = r^2 \]

Example:

Find tangent to \( (x-2)^2 + (y+1)^2 = 25 \) at (5,3)

\[ (5-2)(x-2) + (3-(-1))(y-(-1)) = 25 \] \[ 3(x-2) + 4(y+1) = 25 \] \[ 3x + 4y - 6 + 4 = 25 \] \[ 3x + 4y = 27 \]

2. Condition for Tangency

A line \( y = mx + c \) is tangent to \( x^2 + y^2 = r^2 \) if:

\[ c^2 = r^2(1 + m^2) \]

Example:

Show \( y = 2x + 5 \) is tangent to \( x^2 + y^2 = 5 \)

Here m=2, c=5, r²=5

\[ 5^2 = 5(1 + 2^2) \] \[ 25 = 5(5) \] \[ 25 = 25 \] ✓

3. Length of Tangent

From external point (x₁,y₁) to circle \( x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0 \):

\[ L = \sqrt{x₁^2 + y₁^2 + 2gx₁ + 2fy₁ + c} \]

Example:

Find tangent length from (3,4) to \( x^2 + y^2 = 25 \)

\[ L = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2 - 25} = \sqrt{9 + 16 - 25} = 0 \]

Point is on the circle (tangent length is 0)

Equation of Normal

The normal to a circle at any point is the line joining that point to the center of the circle.

For circle \( (x-1)^2 + (y-2)^2 = 9 \) at point (4,2):

Center is (1,2), point is (4,2)

Slope of normal (and radius): \( \frac{2-2}{4-1} = 0 \)

Equation: y = 2 (horizontal line)

Loci in Circle Geometry

A locus (plural: loci) is the set of all points that satisfy a given condition. In circle geometry, we often find equations of loci that maintain certain relationships to given circles.

1. Constant Distance from Center

The locus of points at a fixed distance from a center is a circle.

Example:

Find locus of points 5 units from (2,-3)

\[ (x-2)^2 + (y+3)^2 = 25 \]

2. Equal Distance from Two Points

The perpendicular bisector of the line segment joining the points.

Example:

Find locus equidistant from (1,2) and (3,4)

Midpoint: (2,3), Slope: \( \frac{4-2}{3-1} = 1 \)

Perpendicular slope: -1

\[ y - 3 = -1(x - 2) \] \[ y = -x + 5 \]

3. Power of a Point

For a fixed point P(x₁,y₁), the locus of points Q such that PQ is tangent to a given circle.

Example:

Given circle \( x^2 + y^2 = 25 \), find locus of points P from which tangents to circle are perpendicular.

This forms another circle (called the director circle) with same center and radius \( r\sqrt{2} \):

\[ x^2 + y^2 = 50 \]

Common Locus Problems

  • Moving point on circle: As a point moves around a circle, its coordinates can be expressed parametrically
  • Chord midpoint locus: The locus of midpoints of parallel chords in a circle is a diameter perpendicular to the chords
  • Angle condition: Points subtending a right angle on a diameter form a circle (Thales' theorem)

Intersection Problems

We can find points of intersection between circles and lines or between two circles by solving their equations simultaneously.

1. Circle-Line Intersection

Substitute linear equation into circle equation and solve quadratic

Example:

Find intersection of \( x^2 + y^2 = 25 \) and \( y = 2x + 1 \)

\[ x^2 + (2x + 1)^2 = 25 \] \[ x^2 + 4x^2 + 4x + 1 = 25 \] \[ 5x^2 + 4x - 24 = 0 \]

Solve using quadratic formula to find x, then y values

2. Circle-Circle Intersection

Subtract equations to eliminate quadratic terms, then solve with one original equation

Example:

Find intersection of \( x^2 + y^2 = 25 \) and \( (x-3)^2 + y^2 = 16 \)

Subtract second from first:

\[ x^2 - (x^2 - 6x + 9) = 9 \] \[ 6x - 9 = 9 \] \[ x = 3 \]

Substitute back: \( 9 + y^2 = 25 \) ⇒ \( y = ±4 \)

Points: (3,4) and (3,-4)

3. Radical Axis

The line formed by subtracting two circle equations (contains all points with equal power relative to both circles)

For circles \( C_1 \) and \( C_2 \), radical axis is \( C_1 - C_2 = 0 \)

Intersection Cases

Two distinct points

When distance between centers is less than sum and greater than difference of radii

One point (tangent)

When distance equals sum (external) or difference (internal) of radii

No intersection

When distance is greater than sum or less than difference of radii

Applications of Circle Geometry

Circle geometry has numerous practical applications in science, engineering, and technology.

1. Navigation Systems

GPS uses trilateration based on circular loci from satellites

2. Optics

Lens surfaces are often circular, requiring tangent calculations

3. Mechanical Engineering

Gear design involves precise circle geometry

4. Architecture

Circular structures require precise geometric calculations

Real-World Example

A satellite orbits Earth such that it's always 1000km from point A on Earth's surface and 2000km from point B. Find the locus of possible satellite positions (Earth radius ≈ 6371km).

Solution:

This involves solving for the intersection of two spheres (3D circles), resulting in another circle (the locus).

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Find the center and radius of the circle \( x^2 + y^2 - 8x + 10y - 8 = 0 \)

Complete the square:

\[ (x^2 - 8x) + (y^2 + 10y) = 8 \] \[ (x^2 - 8x + 16) + (y^2 + 10y + 25) = 8 + 16 + 25 \] \[ (x - 4)^2 + (y + 5)^2 = 49 \]

Center: (4, -5), Radius: 7

Question 2

Find the equation of the tangent to the circle \( (x-2)^2 + (y+1)^2 = 10 \) at the point (3,0)

Using tangent formula:

\[ (3-2)(x-2) + (0-(-1))(y-(-1)) = 10 \] \[ 1(x-2) + 1(y+1) = 10 \] \[ x + y - 1 = 10 \] \[ x + y = 11 \]

Question 3

Find the locus of points equidistant from (1,3) and the line y = -1

Let (x,y) be a point on the locus:

\[ \sqrt{(x-1)^2 + (y-3)^2} = |y + 1| \]

Square both sides:

\[ (x-1)^2 + (y-3)^2 = (y+1)^2 \] \[ (x-1)^2 + y^2 - 6y + 9 = y^2 + 2y + 1 \] \[ (x-1)^2 - 8y + 8 = 0 \] \[ (x-1)^2 = 8(y - 1) \]

This is a parabola with vertex at (1,1)
