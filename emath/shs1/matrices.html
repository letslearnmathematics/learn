Definition of Matrices

A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers, symbols, or expressions arranged in rows and columns. The individual items in a matrix are called its elements or entries.

Example of a 2×3 matrix:

[
1 2 3
4 5 6
]

This can be written as: \( A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \)

Notation:

Matrices are typically denoted by capital letters (A, B, C, etc.)

The element in the i-th row and j-th column is denoted as \( a_{ij} \)

Applications:

Matrices are used in various fields including computer graphics, engineering, physics, economics, and statistics.

Order & Types of Matrices

Order of a Matrix

The order or dimension of a matrix is defined by the number of rows and columns it contains, written as "rows × columns".

For matrix \( B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 7 \\ 3 & 5 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \):

Order: 3 × 2 (read as "three by two")

Types of Matrices

1. Row Matrix

A matrix with only one row: \( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \)

Order: 1 × n

2. Column Matrix

A matrix with only one column: \( \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 7 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \)

Order: m × 1

3. Square Matrix

Number of rows equals number of columns: \( \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 5 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \)

Order: n × n

4. Diagonal Matrix

A square matrix where all elements outside the main diagonal are zero:

\( \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \)

5. Identity Matrix

A diagonal matrix with all diagonal elements equal to 1:

\( I_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

Acts as the multiplicative identity in matrix multiplication.

6. Zero Matrix

A matrix where all elements are zero:

\( \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \)

Acts as the additive identity in matrix addition.

Determinants of a 2×2 Matrix

The determinant is a scalar value that can be computed from the elements of a square matrix. It provides important information about the matrix, including whether it's invertible.

Calculating Determinant of 2×2 Matrix

For matrix \( A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \):

\[ \det(A) = ad - bc \]

Example:

Find the determinant of \( B = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 \\ 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \):

\[ \det(B) = (3 \times 4) - (2 \times 1) = 12 - 2 = 10 \]

Significance:

  • If determinant = 0, the matrix is singular (no inverse exists)
  • If determinant ≠ 0, the matrix is non-singular (inverse exists)
  • The absolute value represents the scale factor of the linear transformation

Arithmetic Operations of Matrices

1. Matrix Addition

Matrices of the same order can be added by adding corresponding elements.

Example:

\( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1+5 & 3+2 \\ 2+1 & 4+0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & 5 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \)

Properties:

  • Commutative: \( A + B = B + A \)
  • Associative: \( (A + B) + C = A + (B + C) \)
  • Additive Identity: \( A + 0 = A \) (where 0 is the zero matrix)

2. Matrix Subtraction

Matrices of the same order can be subtracted by subtracting corresponding elements.

Example:

\( \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 7 \\ 3 & 2 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5-1 & 7-3 \\ 3-0 & 2-4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 4 \\ 3 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \)

3. Scalar Multiplication

A matrix can be multiplied by a scalar (a single number) by multiplying every element by that scalar.

Example:

\( 3 \times \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 0 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3×2 & 3×(-1) \\ 3×0 & 3×4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & -3 \\ 0 & 12 \end{bmatrix} \)

4. Matrix Multiplication

Matrix multiplication is more complex. The number of columns in the first matrix must equal the number of rows in the second matrix.

If \( A \) is m×n and \( B \) is n×p, then \( AB \) will be m×p.

Element \( c_{ij} \) is computed as:

\[ c_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^n a_{ik}b_{kj} \]

Example:

\( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (1×5)+(2×7) & (1×6)+(2×8) \\ (3×5)+(4×7) & (3×6)+(4×8) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 19 & 22 \\ 43 & 50 \end{bmatrix} \)

Important Notes:

  • Matrix multiplication is not commutative: \( AB \neq BA \) in general
  • Matrix multiplication is associative: \( A(BC) = (AB)C \)
  • Matrix multiplication is distributive over addition: \( A(B + C) = AB + AC \)

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Given \( A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \) and \( B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 5 \\ 2 & -3 \end{bmatrix} \), find \( A + B \).

\[ A + B = \begin{bmatrix} 2+0 & -1+5 \\ 3+2 & 4+(-3) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 5 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Question 2

Calculate the determinant of \( C = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 7 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \).

\[ \det(C) = (4 \times 2) - (7 \times 1) = 8 - 7 = 1 \]

Question 3

Multiply \( D = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \) by \( E = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 4 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \).

\[ DE = \begin{bmatrix} (1×-1)+(3×2) & (1×4)+(3×1) \\ (2×-1)+(0×2) & (2×4)+(0×1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 7 \\ -2 & 8 \end{bmatrix} \]
