Vector Basics

A vector is a mathematical quantity that has both magnitude (size) and direction. Unlike scalars which have only magnitude, vectors are represented by arrows where:

Magnitude:

Length of the arrow represents the vector's magnitude (denoted as \( \|\vec{v}\| \) or \( |\vec{v}| \))

Direction:

Arrow's orientation indicates the direction of the vector

Examples of vector quantities:

  • Displacement: 5 km Northeast
  • Velocity: 20 m/s at 30° to the horizontal
  • Force: 10 N vertically upwards

Notation

Vectors can be represented in several ways:

Arrow Notation

\[ \vec{AB} \text{ or } \vec{a} \]

Boldface

\[ \mathbf{a} \text{ or } \mathbf{AB} \]

Underline

\[ \underline{a} \text{ or } \underline{AB} \]

Equal Vectors

Two vectors are equal if they have:

  • Same magnitude
  • Same direction

If \( \vec{AB} = \vec{CD} \), then:

  • Length AB = Length CD
  • AB is parallel to CD
  • They point in the same direction

Vector Representation

Standard Basis Form

In 2D space, any vector can be expressed using the standard basis vectors \( \mathbf{i} \) and \( \mathbf{j} \):

\[ \vec{v} = a\mathbf{i} + b\mathbf{j} = \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \end{pmatrix} \]

Where:

  • \( \mathbf{i} \) is the unit vector in the x-direction
  • \( \mathbf{j} \) is the unit vector in the y-direction
  • \( a \) and \( b \) are scalar components

Magnitude:

\[ \|\vec{v}\| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \]

Direction:

\[ \theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{b}{a}\right) \]

Position Vectors

A position vector represents the location of a point relative to an origin:

If point \( P \) has coordinates \( (x, y) \), its position vector is:

\[ \vec{OP} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} \]

Where \( O \) is the origin \( (0, 0) \).

Unit Vectors

A unit vector has magnitude 1 and indicates direction:

The unit vector in the direction of \( \vec{v} \) is:

\[ \hat{v} = \frac{\vec{v}}{\|\vec{v}\|} \]

Standard unit vectors:

\[ \mathbf{i} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{j} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \]

Vector Operations

Vector Addition

To add two vectors component-wise:

If \( \vec{u} = \begin{pmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \end{pmatrix} \) and \( \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{pmatrix} \):

\[ \vec{u} + \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} u_1 + v_1 \\ u_2 + v_2 \end{pmatrix} \]

Graphical addition uses the "tip-to-tail" method

Vector Subtraction

Subtraction is equivalent to adding the negative vector:

\[ \vec{u} - \vec{v} = \vec{u} + (-\vec{v}) = \begin{pmatrix} u_1 - v_1 \\ u_2 - v_2 \end{pmatrix} \]

Scalar Multiplication

Multiplying a vector by a scalar changes its magnitude:

For scalar \( k \) and vector \( \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{pmatrix} \):

\[ k\vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} kv_1 \\ kv_2 \end{pmatrix} \]

If \( k > 0 \):

Same direction, scaled magnitude

If \( k < 0 \):

Opposite direction, scaled magnitude

Resultant of Vectors

The resultant vector is the single vector that has the same effect as multiple vectors combined:

For vectors \( \vec{u} \) and \( \vec{v} \):

\[ \vec{R} = \vec{u} + \vec{v} \]

Magnitude of resultant:

\[ \|\vec{R}\| = \sqrt{\|\vec{u}\|^2 + \|\vec{v}\|^2 + 2\|\vec{u}\|\|\vec{v}\|\cos\theta} \]

Where \( \theta \) is the angle between \( \vec{u} \) and \( \vec{v} \).

Laws of Vector Addition

Parallelogram Law

If two vectors are adjacent sides of a parallelogram, their sum is the diagonal:

For vectors \( \vec{a} \) and \( \vec{b} \):

\[ \vec{a} + \vec{b} = \text{Diagonal of parallelogram} \]

Polygon Law

For adding multiple vectors by placing them tip-to-tail:

For vectors \( \vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \ldots, \vec{v}_n \):

\[ \vec{R} = \vec{v}_1 + \vec{v}_2 + \cdots + \vec{v}_n \]

The resultant joins the tail of the first to the tip of the last.

Resolution of Vectors

Breaking a vector into perpendicular components:

For vector \( \vec{v} \) at angle \( \theta \):

\[ v_x = \|\vec{v}\|\cos\theta \] \[ v_y = \|\vec{v}\|\sin\theta \]

Properties of Vectors

Commutative Property

Order of addition doesn't matter:

\[ \vec{a} + \vec{b} = \vec{b} + \vec{a} \]

Associative Property

Grouping doesn't affect addition:

\[ (\vec{a} + \vec{b}) + \vec{c} = \vec{a} + (\vec{b} + \vec{c}) \]

Additive Identity

The zero vector \( \vec{0} \) leaves vectors unchanged:

\[ \vec{a} + \vec{0} = \vec{a} \]

Additive Inverse

Every vector has an opposite that sums to zero:

\[ \vec{a} + (-\vec{a}) = \vec{0} \]

Parallel Vectors

Two vectors are parallel if one is a scalar multiple of the other:

\[ \vec{a} \parallel \vec{b} \iff \vec{a} = k\vec{b} \text{ for some scalar } k \]

Vector Products

Dot (Scalar) Product

The dot product produces a scalar value:

For vectors \( \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{pmatrix} \) and \( \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \end{pmatrix} \):

\[ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 \]

Geometrically:

\[ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|\cos\theta \]

Where \( \theta \) is the angle between them.

Orthogonal Vectors:

If \( \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0 \), the vectors are perpendicular

Angle Between Vectors:

\[ \cos\theta = \frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{\|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|} \]

Cross Product (Preview)

In 3D space, the cross product produces a vector perpendicular to both input vectors:

For 2D vectors \( \vec{a} \) and \( \vec{b} \), the magnitude is:

\[ \|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}\| = \|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|\sin\theta \]

(Note: Full cross product is covered in SHS3 3D vectors)

Geometry Applications

Section Formula

Finding a point that divides a line segment in a given ratio:

If point \( P \) divides \( AB \) in ratio \( m:n \), its position vector is:

\[ \vec{OP} = \frac{n\vec{OA} + m\vec{OB}}{m + n} \]

Collinear Points

Points are collinear if their position vectors satisfy:

Points \( A, B, C \) are collinear if:

\[ \vec{AB} = k\vec{AC} \text{ for some scalar } k \]

Parallelogram Properties

Using vectors to prove geometric properties:

For parallelogram \( ABCD \):

  • \( \vec{AB} = \vec{DC} \)
  • \( \vec{AD} = \vec{BC} \)
  • Diagonals: \( \vec{AC} = \vec{AB} + \vec{AD} \)
  • Diagonals bisect each other

Centroid of Triangle

The intersection point of medians:

For triangle with vertices \( A, B, C \):

\[ \vec{OG} = \frac{\vec{OA} + \vec{OB} + \vec{OC}}{3} \]

Practice Exercise

Question 1

Given \( \vec{a} = 3\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j} \) and \( \vec{b} = -\mathbf{i} + 4\mathbf{j} \), find \( \vec{a} + \vec{b} \).

\[ \vec{a} + \vec{b} = (3-1)\mathbf{i} + (-2+4)\mathbf{j} = 2\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} \]

Question 2

Find the angle between \( \vec{u} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ \sqrt{3} \end{pmatrix} \) and \( \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} -\sqrt{3} \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \).

First calculate dot product and magnitudes:

\[ \vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = (1)(-\sqrt{3}) + (\sqrt{3})(1) = 0 \]

Since dot product is 0, the vectors are perpendicular (90°).

Question 3

Prove that the points \( A(1,2) \), \( B(4,5) \), and \( C(-2,-1) \) are collinear using vectors.

Find vectors \( \vec{AB} \) and \( \vec{AC} \):

\[ \vec{AB} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \vec{AC} = \begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix} \]

Since \( \vec{AC} = -1 \times \vec{AB} \), the points are collinear.
