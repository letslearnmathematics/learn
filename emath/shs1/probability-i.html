Introduction to Counting & Probability

Combinatorics is the branch of mathematics concerned with counting, arrangement, and probability. It helps us solve problems like:

  • How many possible passwords can be created with 8 characters?
  • What's the probability of winning a lottery?
  • How many ways can a committee be formed from a group?

Permutation:

An ordered arrangement of objects where order matters.

Example: Arrangements of letters A, B, C â†’ ABC, ACB, BAC, etc.

Combination:

A selection of objects where order doesn't matter.

Example: Choosing 2 fruits from apple, banana, orange â†’ apple+banana is same as banana+apple

Probability Basics

Probability measures how likely an event is to occur, expressed as a number between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain).

Probability formula:

\[ P(E) = \frac{\text{Number of favorable outcomes}}{\text{Total possible outcomes}} \]

Fundamental Counting Principle

Also known as the Multiplication Rule, it states that if one event can occur in m ways and a second can occur independently in n ways, then the two events can occur in m Ã— n ways.

Example: If you have 3 shirts and 4 pants, you can create:

\[ 3 \times 4 = 12 \text{ different outfits} \]

Extended Principle

For multiple events:

\[ m \times n \times p \times \ldots \]

Example: 3 shirts, 4 pants, 2 hats â†’ 3Ã—4Ã—2 = 24 combinations

With Restrictions

When some choices are limited:

Example: 5-digit PIN where first digit can't be 0 â†’ 9Ã—10Ã—10Ã—10Ã—10 = 90,000 possible PINs

Practice Problems

1. A restaurant offers 5 main dishes, 3 sides, and 4 drinks. How many possible meals can be ordered?

\[ 5 \times 3 \times 4 = 60 \text{ possible meals} \]

2. How many 4-letter words can be formed from the English alphabet if letters can be repeated?

\[ 26 \times 26 \times 26 \times 26 = 26^4 = 456,976 \text{ words} \]

Permutations and Combinations

Permutations

Arrangements where order matters. Two types:

With Repetition

When items can be reused:

\[ P(n, k) = n^k \]

Example: 3-digit codes using 0-9 â†’ 10Â³ = 1,000 permutations

Without Repetition

When items cannot be reused:

\[ P(n, k) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!} \]

Example: Arranging 4 books out of 7 â†’ 7!/(7-4)! = 7Ã—6Ã—5Ã—4 = 840 ways

Combinations

Selections where order doesn't matter:

\[ C(n, k) = \binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} \]

Example: Choosing 3 toppings from 8 available â†’ C(8,3) = 56 combinations

  • Combination: ABC = ACB

Notation:

Combinations are often read as "n choose k"

\[ n! = n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \times \ldots \times 1 \]

Example: 4! = 4Ã—3Ã—2Ã—1 = 24

Relationship Between Permutations and Combinations

Permutations and combinations are related through the ordering of elements:

\[ P(n, k) = C(n, k) \times k! \]

This shows that permutations are just combinations with the selected items arranged in different orders.

Conversion Formula

To convert between permutations and combinations:

\[ C(n, k) = \frac{P(n, k)}{k!} \]

Example: P(5,3) = 60 â†’ C(5,3) = 60/6 = 10

Calculating Probabilities

Basic probability rules and formulas:

Simple Probability

\[ P(E) = \frac{n(E)}{n(S)} \]

Where n(E) = favorable outcomes, n(S) = total possible outcomes

Complement Rule

\[ P(E') = 1 - P(E) \]

Probability of event not occurring

Independent Events

\[ P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B) \]

For events that don't affect each other

Mutually Exclusive Events

\[ P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) \]

Events that cannot occur together

Probability Examples

Example 1: Dice Roll

What's the probability of rolling a prime number on a 6-sided die?

Prime numbers on a die: 2, 3, 5 (3 outcomes)

\[ P(\text{Prime}) = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2} \]

Example 2: Card Draw

Probability of drawing a heart or a king from a standard deck?

P(Heart) = 13/52, P(King) = 4/52, P(Heart and King) = 1/52

\[ P(\text{Heart or King}) = \frac{13}{52} + \frac{4}{52} - \frac{1}{52} = \frac{16}{52} = \frac{4}{13} \]

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing permutations with combinations
  • Assuming events are independent when they're not
  • Forgetting to subtract overlapping events in "OR" probabilities
  • Miscounting total possible outcomes

Practice Exercise

Question 1

How many ways can 4 people be arranged in a line?

\[ 4! = 24 \text{ ways} \]

Question 2

A committee of 3 needs to be chosen from 10 people. How many different committees are possible?

\[ C(10,3) = 120 \text{ committees} \]

Question 3

What is the probability of getting exactly two heads when flipping three fair coins?

Possible outcomes: HHH, HHT, HTH, THH, HTT, THT, TTH, TTT (8 total)

Favorable outcomes: HHT, HTH, THH (3 outcomes)

\[ P(\text{Two Heads}) = \frac{3}{8} \]
