Graphical Solution to Linear Inequalities (One Variable)

Linear inequalities in one variable can be represented graphically on a number line. The solution shows all values that satisfy the inequality.

Basic Rules:

  • < (less than): Open circle, arrow to the left
  • > (greater than): Open circle, arrow to the right
  • ≤ (less than or equal to): Closed circle, arrow to the left
  • ≥ (greater than or equal to): Closed circle, arrow to the right
Graphical Representation of x ≥ 3
3
→

Example 1:

Solve and graph: \( 2x - 5 < 7 \)

\( 2x - 5 < 7 \)
Add 5 to both sides: \( 2x < 12 \)
Divide by 2: \( x < 6 \)

The graph shows an open circle at 6 with an arrow to the left.

Graphical Solution to Linear Inequalities (Two Variables)

Inequalities in two variables (like \( x \) and \( y \)) can be represented on the Cartesian plane. The solution is a shaded region called the half-plane.

Graphing Steps:

  1. Graph the corresponding equation (replace inequality with =)
  2. Use dashed line for < or > and solid line for ≤ or ≥
  3. Test a point (usually (0,0)) to determine which side to shade
Graph of \( y ≤ 2x + 1 \)

Example 2:

Graph the inequality \( 3x + 2y ≥ 6 \)

First graph the line \( 3x + 2y = 6 \)
Find x-intercept (y=0): \( x = 2 \) → (2, 0)
Find y-intercept (x=0): \( y = 3 \) → (0, 3)
Draw solid line through these points (since ≥)
Test (0,0): \( 0 ≥ 6 \) is false → shade opposite side

Graphical Solution to Systems of Linear Inequalities

A system of inequalities has solutions that satisfy all inequalities simultaneously. Graphically, this is the intersection of all individual solution regions.

Key Concepts:

  • Feasible Region: The overlapping area satisfying all inequalities
  • Bounded vs Unbounded: Regions that are enclosed or extend infinitely
  • Corner Points: Vertices of the feasible region (important for optimization)

Linear Programming

Linear programming involves optimizing (maximizing or minimizing) a linear objective function subject to linear constraints (inequalities).

Components:

  • Objective Function: What to maximize/minimize (e.g., profit, cost)
  • Constraints: Limitations on resources (inequalities)
  • Feasible Region: All possible solutions satisfying constraints
  • Optimal Solution: Found at a corner point of the feasible region

Solving Method

  1. Graph all constraints to find the feasible region
  2. Identify all corner points (vertices) of the feasible region
  3. Evaluate the objective function at each corner point
  4. Select the point that gives the optimal value
Linear Programming Problem

Example 4:

A farmer has 8 acres of land and can plant either corn (₵200 profit per acre) or beans (₵300 profit per acre). Constraints:

  • Maximum of 6 acres for corn
  • Maximum of 5 acres for beans
  • Total planting cannot exceed 8 acres

How many acres of each should be planted to maximize profit?

Let \( x \) = acres of corn, \( y \) = acres of beans
Objective: Maximize \( P = 200x + 300y \)
Constraints:
  • \( x ≤ 6 \)
  • \( y ≤ 5 \)
  • \( x + y ≤ 8 \)
  • \( x ≥ 0 \), \( y ≥ 0 \)
Corner points: (0,0), (0,5), (3,5), (6,2), (6,0)
Evaluate P at each point:
Point (x,y) P = 200x + 300y
(0,0) ₵0
(0,5) ₵1,500
(3,5) ₵2,100
(6,2) ₵1,800
(6,0) ₵1,200
Maximum profit of ₵2,100 at (3,5) → 3 acres corn, 5 acres beans

Real-World Applications

Linear programming has numerous applications in business, economics, engineering, and military operations.

Business

  • Product mix optimization
  • Inventory management
  • Transportation logistics

Economics

  • Resource allocation
  • Cost minimization
  • Revenue maximization

Engineering

  • Network design
  • Energy optimization
  • Production scheduling

Example 5: Manufacturing Problem

A company produces two products (A and B) with the following constraints:

  • Machine time: 2A + 3B ≤ 120 hours
  • Labor: 4A + 2B ≤ 160 hours
  • Profit: ₵50 per A, ₵70 per B

Find the optimal production mix to maximize profit.

Objective: Maximize \( P = 50A + 70B \)
Graph constraints to find feasible region
Corner points: (0,0), (0,40), (30,20), (40,0)
Evaluate P at each point:
  • (0,0): ₵0
  • (0,40): ₵2,800
  • (30,20): ₵2,900
  • (40,0): ₵2,000
Optimal solution: Produce 30 units of A and 20 units of B for ₵2,900 profit

Question 3

A baker makes cakes (₵20 profit) and pies (₵15 profit). Oven time: 2h per cake, 1h per pie, maximum 12h. Find optimal product mix.

Let x=cakes, y=pies. Maximize P=20x+15y with 2x+y≤12. Solution: (6,0) → 6 cakes, 0 pies for ₵120 profit
